# B2B Marketplace Playbook India — How to Build Supply, Demand, and Liquidity

In 2018, Udaan raised $225 million at a $1 billion valuation and became India's fastest startup to reach unicorn status. The pitch was compelling: India's B2B trade — estimated at $700 billion annually — was conducted almost entirely through informal broker networks, phone calls, and relationship-driven credit arrangements. A technology platform that could aggregate supply, create price transparency, and provide embedded financing would capture enormous value.

By 2023, Udaan had laid off 350 employees, cut its valuation significantly in a down round, and was fighting for profitability. By 2025, it had restructured and found a path to break-even on its core categories.

Udaan's journey is not a failure story. It is the most instructive case study in Indian B2B marketplace building. The mistakes Udaan made — moving too fast on categories, over-investing in logistics before unit economics worked, underestimating the complexity of Indian B2B credit — are mistakes that every new B2B marketplace founder is at risk of repeating.

This article is the complete playbook: the five fundamental challenges of Indian B2B marketplaces, how the successful ones solved them, the business models that work, and the unit economics you should target.

---

## Why B2B Marketplaces Are Different

Before getting into the India-specific playbook, it is worth understanding why B2B marketplaces are fundamentally different from B2C marketplaces. The differences are not minor — they change everything about how you build and operate.

| Dimension | B2C Marketplace | B2B Marketplace |
|---|---|---|
| Transaction frequency | High (daily/weekly) | Lower (weekly/monthly, but large) |
| Average order value | ₹500-5,000 | ₹10,000-5,00,000+ |
| Decision-making | Individual, impulse | Business, committee |
| Credit requirement | Cash/card at checkout | 30-90 day credit standard |
| Relationship importance | Low (price/speed win) | Very high (trust, reliability) |
| SKU complexity | Standardised | Non-standardised, specification-driven |
| Returns complexity | Moderate | Very complex (specification disputes) |
| Switching cost | Very low | Moderate to very high |
| CAC | ₹100-500 | ₹2,000-50,000 |
| LTV | ₹1,000-10,000 | ₹1,00,000-50,00,000+ |

The high LTV is the opportunity. The high CAC, credit requirement, and relationship complexity are the challenges.

## The Indian B2B Marketplace Landscape

### IndiaMART: The OG Aggregator (1996-present)

IndiaMART is the oldest and most successful Indian B2B marketplace. Listed on BSE/NSE, profitable, with revenue of ₹1,218 Cr in FY2025. The model: a directory/marketplace where 7+ million suppliers list products, and 200+ million buyers search and connect.

**What IndiaMART got right:** Lead generation for buyers and suppliers, not transaction facilitation. IndiaMART charges suppliers for premium listings (subscription fees of ₹15,000-3,00,000 per year). It does not manage transactions, logistics, or credit. This is a capital-light, high-margin model.

**The limitation:** IndiaMART connects buyers and sellers but does not guarantee transaction quality, delivery, or payment. The actual transaction happens offline. This means IndiaMART captures discovery value but not transaction value. Take rate on GMV is effectively zero — revenue is from subscriptions.

### Udaan: Managed Marketplace at Scale

Founded 2016 by former Flipkart executives. Raised $2+ billion. The model: not just connect buyers and sellers, but actively manage logistics, credit (UdaanCapital), and quality.

**What Udaan built:** A full-stack B2B marketplace for food, FMCG, pharma, and electronics — targeting kirana stores and small retailers (buyers) and manufacturers/distributors (sellers). Embedded working capital financing for buyers.

**The challenge:** Full-stack operations at scale require enormous capital. Udaan's logistics and credit book required continuous capital infusion. When funding dried up in 2022-23, the model became unsustainable without reaching the scale needed for unit economics to work.

**The lesson:** Managed marketplace models require achieving scale threshold before the unit economics work. If you run out of capital before reaching that scale, you are in trouble.

### OfBusiness: The Model That Actually Works

OfBusiness is arguably the most successful Indian B2B marketplace story that most people outside the startup ecosystem have not fully analysed. Founded 2016, reached unicorn status in 2021 with a valuation of $5 billion by 2022. EBITDA positive.

**The OfBusiness model:**

1. Start with a specific, well-defined category: raw materials for SME manufacturers (steel, chemicals, polymers)
2. Offer credit to buyers (OFB Credit, the NBFC arm) as the primary value proposition
3. Build the marketplace as a distribution channel for the credit-enabled procurement
4. Expand categories only after achieving market share and unit economics in the first category

OfBusiness revenue was approximately ₹19,400 Cr in FY2025, with EBITDA margins of 4-5%. The credit book (OFB Credit/Oxyzo Financial Services) is a separate listed entity worth $1 billion+.

**The key insight:** In Indian B2B, credit IS the product. The marketplace is the distribution channel. Whoever provides credit at the right price to SME buyers controls the category.

### Moglix: Industrial B2B

Founded 2015, raised $250M+, valued at ~$2.6 billion (2022). Category: industrial and MRO (Maintenance, Repair, Operations) supplies for manufacturing companies.

**What Moglix got right:**
- Targeted corporate and mid-market industrial buyers (not SMEs) — higher AOV, lower credit risk
- Standardised SKUs (industrial supplies have part numbers, unlike food or apparel)
- Built procurement software (SaaS) embedded into the marketplace — creating high switching costs
- Institutional supply chain (manufacturers, authorised distributors) rather than informal suppliers

Moglix's model is closer to a managed marketplace with embedded procurement software. Take rate: 8-15% depending on category.

### Zetwerk: Manufacturing Marketplace

Founded 2018, valued at $2.7 billion (2022). Category: custom manufacturing — connects brands and enterprises that need custom-manufactured components with manufacturing vendors.

**What Zetwerk solved:** The discovery and trust problem in custom manufacturing. Finding a reliable factory for a custom component was historically done entirely through personal networks. Zetwerk digitised the RFQ (Request for Quotation) process, added quality inspection, and managed logistics.

## The Five Fundamental Challenges

### Challenge 1: The Cold Start Problem (Chicken-and-Egg)

Every marketplace faces the classic cold start problem: buyers don't come without sellers, and sellers don't come without buyers. In B2B, this is more severe than B2C because:

- Buyers and sellers have existing relationships they trust
- Switching involves operational risk (what if the new supplier is unreliable?)
- B2B relationships involve credit terms that take time to establish

**How to Solve It: The Supply-First Rule**

The evidence from successful Indian B2B marketplaces is consistent: **seed supply first, then demand.**

Here is why this works:

1. In B2B, supply (sellers) is harder to aggregate than demand (buyers) in most categories
2. Buyers will join a marketplace with sufficient supply diversity even if there are few other buyers
3. Sellers who see buyer activity quickly bring more of their catalogue
4. Once you have supply diversity, the marketplace becomes a discovery platform for buyers

**The Zetwerk playbook for cold start:**

Zetwerk's founders used their personal manufacturing network to onboard 20-30 pre-verified manufacturing vendors before building buyer-facing features. They then brought their first buyers (enterprise clients from their Flipkart/Amazon network) who had an immediate need for custom manufacturing. The first transaction happened within the founding team's personal network. The platform came after the first transactions.

**Category selection for cold start:**

The easiest categories to cold-start are:
- High fragmentation on supply side (thousands of small suppliers) + concentrated demand side (fewer, identifiable buyers)
- Standardised products (price comparison is possible without samples)
- Existing digital discovery intent (buyers already searching online)

The hardest: relationship-heavy categories where all business is done on personal trust and credit history.

```
Cold Start Framework: Which Side to Seed First

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Is supply more fragmented than demand?  YES → Seed supply first │
│                                          NO → Seed demand first  │
│                                                                   │
│ Is the category price-transparent?       YES → Aggregator model  │
│                                           NO → Managed marketplace│
│                                                                   │
│ Is credit required?                      YES → Must solve credit  │
│                                           NO → Simpler model      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Challenge 2: The Trust Problem

Indian B2B buyers and sellers have traded for decades based on personal relationships. The trusted supplier is often the person you have done business with for 15 years, whose family you know, and who has never let you down. A marketplace asks you to transact with strangers at scale.

The trust problem has three layers:

**Layer 1: Product quality trust** — will the product I receive match what was shown?
**Layer 2: Payment trust** — will I get paid by the buyer (seller perspective)?
**Layer 3: Reliability trust** — will delivery happen on time, every time?

**How Successful Platforms Built Trust**

**Verification and credentialing:** Moglix requires suppliers to go through a supplier onboarding audit (physical facility inspection, quality management system verification, GST compliance check). Certified suppliers are badged prominently. This mirrors how enterprise procurement departments qualify vendors — B2B buyers immediately understand the credibility signal.

**Escrow and payment guarantees:** Several platforms hold payment in escrow until delivery confirmation. For buyers with credit concerns about the marketplace's financial stability, this creates security. For sellers who fear non-payment by buyers, this eliminates risk.

**Quality inspection:** Zetwerk employs a team of quality inspectors who verify manufactured goods before they leave the factory. The inspection report is shared with the buyer. This single feature resolved the biggest trust barrier in custom manufacturing.

**Ratings and history transparency:** Unlike B2C (where a seller with 1,000 reviews has credibility), B2B buyers are sceptical of star ratings. What works better: transaction history, buyer references (verifiable), and quality certifications.

**The relationship transition:** The most successful platforms acknowledge that the personal relationship is valuable and do not try to replace it. Instead, they make the relationship more efficient. The sales executive who managed the offline relationship becomes the platform champion who onboards their clients. The existing trust transfers to the platform.

### Challenge 3: The Credit Problem

This is the most important challenge. In Indian B2B, **credit is the product.** Whoever solves B2B credit wins.

Here is the data: According to RBI and industry estimates, 70-80% of B2B transactions in India involve some form of trade credit. The typical credit terms in different categories:

| Category | Typical Credit Terms |
|---|---|
| FMCG / Consumer goods | 30-45 days |
| Industrial supplies | 45-60 days |
| Construction materials | 60-90 days |
| Custom manufacturing | 30% advance, 70% on delivery |
| Agricultural commodities | 7-30 days |

A marketplace that asks buyers to pay upfront when their traditional suppliers give 45-day credit will fail. The marketplace must either:

1. **Provide credit itself** (requires NBFC licence or banking partnership, capital-intensive)
2. **Partner with an NBFC/fintech for credit** (revenue share model, reduces take rate)
3. **Facilitate invoice discounting** (connect buyer's receivable to fintech lender)

**The OfBusiness Credit Model**

OfBusiness built OFB Credit (later renamed Oxyzo Financial Services, now publicly listed) as a separate NBFC. This is the gold standard model because:

- The marketplace has first-party transaction data (what buyers purchase, frequency, volumes) which makes underwriting more accurate than bank scoring
- The credit product creates lock-in — buyers stay on the platform because their credit limit is with the platform
- The NBFC revenues (interest income) are a second, higher-margin revenue stream alongside marketplace take rate

**The economics of embedded credit in B2B marketplace:**

```
Embedded Credit Economics (illustrative, ₹5 lakh credit line):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Credit line size:              ₹5,00,000
Average utilisation:           65% (₹3,25,000 outstanding)
Interest rate (to buyer):      18% per annum
Annual interest income:        ₹58,500
Cost of funds:                 12% (NCD/bank borrowing)
Annual cost of funds:          ₹39,000
Net Interest Margin:           ₹19,500 (33% NIM on spread)

Marketplace take rate on same buyer's GMV:
Annual GMV (₹3,25,000/month × 12):  ₹39,00,000
Marketplace take rate (5%):          ₹1,95,000

Combined revenue per buyer:          ₹2,14,500/year
vs. Marketplace-only revenue:        ₹1,95,000/year
Credit adds ~10% to revenue per buyer at 65% utilisation
```

The more important insight is risk management: the platform's transaction data allows it to underwrite credit with a loss rate of 1.5-2.5% (vs 4-6% for unsecured lending without transaction data). The data moat creates a credit underwriting advantage.

### Challenge 4: The Fragmentation Problem

India's manufacturing and distribution ecosystem is extraordinarily fragmented. There are 6.3 crore (63 million) MSMEs in India — most of them micro-enterprises. In any given category — steel trading, chemical distribution, textile manufacturing — there are thousands of small players operating regionally.

This fragmentation creates the marketplace opportunity (aggregation creates value) but also makes it operationally complex:

**The fragmentation challenges:**

- **Catalogue quality:** Small suppliers do not have digital product catalogues. Images, specifications, and pricing must be captured and standardised — often manually at first
- **GST compliance:** 63% of SME suppliers are on the composition scheme or GST-exempt thresholds — their invoice formats and credit note processes differ from registered dealers
- **Logistics capacity:** Many small suppliers do not have standard packaging or logistics infrastructure. Marketplace must sometimes provide packaging materials or arrange pick-up
- **Quality consistency:** A supplier who produces good quality in one batch may have issues in the next batch. Quality control is ongoing, not one-time
- **Price discovery:** Prices fluctuate based on raw material costs, demand, and personal relationships. Real-time pricing on a marketplace requires active supplier management

**How to Manage Fragmentation**

The successful model is tiered supply:

1. **Tier 1 Suppliers (Top 10-20%):** Highest volume, best quality, dedicated account managers, SLA-bound relationships, premium placement on platform
2. **Tier 2 Suppliers (Middle 30-40%):** Standard quality, periodic audits, self-service tools
3. **Tier 3 Suppliers (Remaining 40-50%):** Listed but lower default placement; must earn way to higher tier through performance

Moglix's supplier development program trains SME manufacturers on quality, packaging, and digital tools — investing in supplier capability because supplier quality directly impacts buyer retention.

### Challenge 5: The Take Rate Problem

Indian B2B margins are thin. A steel trader makes 1-3% net margin. A chemical distributor makes 3-7%. A garment manufacturer makes 5-15%. When you put a marketplace take rate of 8-15% on top of a supply chain that already has thin margins, you eliminate the economics for one side.

**Sustainable Take Rates by Category**

| Category | Gross Margin of Supply | Sustainable Take Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Steel / Metals | 2-5% | 1-3% |
| Chemicals / Polymers | 5-12% | 3-6% |
| FMCG / Consumer goods | 8-20% | 5-10% |
| Industrial supplies / MRO | 20-40% | 10-18% |
| Custom manufacturing | 20-35% | 8-15% |
| Agri commodities | 3-8% | 1-4% |

The implication: choose your category based on the gross margin available in the supply chain. A marketplace in steel will earn a 2% take rate and need massive GMV to generate revenue. A marketplace in industrial MRO can earn 15% and generate strong revenue at moderate GMV.

**How to Justify Higher Take Rates**

Take rate can be justified by genuine value beyond price discovery:

- **Financing:** If the marketplace provides working capital credit, the take rate includes an implicit financing cost that buyers are willing to pay
- **Quality assurance:** Verified, inspected supply is worth a premium to buyers who have been burned by quality issues
- **Logistics:** Reliable, tracked delivery is worth 2-4% extra to industrial buyers who cannot afford production shutdowns from late deliveries
- **Compliance:** GST-compliant invoicing, e-way bills, digital audit trails — regulatory compliance has real value to SME buyers who need these for their own compliance

## The Three Business Models That Work

### Model 1: Pure Platform (IndiaMART Model)

**What it is:** Directory/listing platform. Connects buyers and sellers. Charges sellers for premium visibility. Does not manage transactions.

**Revenue model:** Subscription fees from suppliers (₹15,000-3,00,000/year per supplier)

**Unit economics:**

```
Pure Platform Unit Economics:
─────────────────────────────────────────
ARPU per paid supplier:    ₹60,000/year
Gross margin:              85-90% (SaaS-like)
Churn:                     15-25% annually
CAC per supplier:          ₹8,000-15,000
LTV per supplier:          ₹2,40,000-4,00,000
LTV/CAC ratio:             25-50x (excellent)
```

**Works best for:** Categories with high fragmentation and information asymmetry. Marketplaces where buyers search actively. Works at scale even if each transaction is offline.

**Limitation:** Does not capture transaction value. Cannot provide credit. Low competitive moat once network is established (competitors can replicate).

### Model 2: Managed Marketplace (Udaan/Moglix Model)

**What it is:** Platform manages logistics, quality assurance, and sometimes credit. Takes a transaction-based fee.

**Revenue model:** Commission/take rate on GMV (3-15% depending on category) + credit income (if NBFC)

**Unit economics (illustrative, industrial supplies category):**

```
Managed Marketplace Unit Economics (per ₹1,00,000 GMV):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Gross GMV:                  ₹1,00,000
Take rate (12%):            ₹12,000
Net revenue:                ₹12,000

Variable costs per ₹1L GMV:
- Logistics (3%):           ₹3,000
- Returns/quality issues:   ₹500
- Payment processing:       ₹200
- Credit cost allocation:   ₹1,500
Total variable costs:       ₹5,200

Contribution per ₹1L GMV:  ₹6,800 (6.8%)

Fixed cost per month:       ₹50,00,000 (tech, ops, sales)
GMV needed for breakeven:   ₹73.5 Cr/month
```

**Works best for:** Categories with logistics complexity, quality variability, and credit needs. Requires significant capital to reach break-even GMV.

### Model 3: Vertical SaaS + Marketplace

**What it is:** Sell procurement software (SaaS) to buyers first. Build marketplace functionality into the software. Supply comes through the marketplace.

**Revenue model:** SaaS subscription + marketplace take rate + credit income

**Why this model is increasingly preferred:**

The SaaS layer creates switching costs before the marketplace is large. Once the buyer is using your procurement software (integrated with their ERP, with approval workflows, digital contracts), migrating to a different platform is expensive. The marketplace embedded in the SaaS then benefits from captive buyer demand.

**Examples:**
- Moglix: Procurement SaaS + MRO marketplace
- Coupa (global): Procurement SaaS that became marketplace
- Lal10 (India, textile): Design and sourcing SaaS + fabric marketplace

## Unit Economics Benchmarks: What Good Looks Like

| Metric | Early Stage (Pre-Series A) | Growth Stage (Series A-B) | Mature Stage |
|---|---|---|---|
| GMV growth (MoM) | 15-25% | 8-15% | 3-7% |
| Take rate | Building (2-5%) | Target (5-15%) | Stable |
| Gross margin on net revenue | 30-50% | 50-70% | 60-80% |
| CAC payback | 12-24 months | 6-12 months | < 6 months |
| NRR (buyer cohorts) | 110-130% | 120-150% | 130-180% |
| Contribution margin % | Negative | 10-30% | 30-50% |

**The NRR Insight**

B2B marketplace NRR (Net Revenue Retention from buyer cohorts) is the most important metric. In a well-run B2B marketplace:

- Buyers spend more in year 2 than year 1 (as they trust the platform more)
- Buyers expand into new categories (if available)
- Buyers refer other buyers (word of mouth in trade networks is powerful)

An NRR of 130% means: buyers who spent ₹100 in year 1 spent ₹130 in year 2, on average, from expansion (not new buyers). This is the growth flywheel.

## GMV vs Net Revenue: Why the Distinction Matters

Most Indian B2B marketplaces report GMV (Gross Merchandise Value) — the total value of goods transacted on the platform. Investors, especially post-2022, care more about net revenue (what the platform actually earns after paying out to sellers).

```
GMV to Net Revenue Bridge (illustrative):
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total GMV:                           ₹100 Cr/month
Returns and cancellations (3%):      -₹3 Cr
Net GMV:                             ₹97 Cr/month

For pure marketplace (take rate 8%):
Net revenue:                         ₹7.76 Cr/month

For inventory-led model (buys and sells):
Net revenue = ₹97 Cr (reported as revenue by some)
But gross margin matters: if 5%, gross profit is ₹4.85 Cr
vs marketplace's ₹7.76 Cr at lower capital requirement

→ Pure marketplace model is more capital-efficient at same GMV
```

The comparison between GMV and net revenue matters critically for valuation. A marketplace being valued at 2x net revenue ARR is valued very differently from one valued at 0.2x GMV — even if the GMV and ARR imply the same absolute business.

## The Growth Flywheel That Creates Defensibility

Sustainable B2B marketplaces build a flywheel that competitors find expensive to replicate:

```
The Indian B2B Marketplace Flywheel:
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
More verified suppliers
       ↓
Better price + quality → More buyers join
       ↓
More buyer transactions → Better credit underwriting data
       ↓
Better credit → Buyers buy more → Higher GMV
       ↓
Higher GMV → More suppliers want to list
       ↓ (back to top)

Side effects that compound the flywheel:
- Transaction history → NRR expansion
- More data → Better fraud detection → Lower loss rates
- Scale → Better logistics rates → Lower cost to serve
- Brand → Lower CAC
```

The earliest moat is data (credit underwriting data and transaction history). The durable moat is supplier-buyer relationship density — once a buyer has 3+ years of transaction history with 20+ verified suppliers on your platform, the switching cost is enormous.

## What Indian B2B Marketplace Founders Get Wrong

### Mistake 1: Expanding Categories Too Early

Every successful B2B marketplace expanded categories after — not before — achieving market leadership in the first category. Udaan expanded into too many categories simultaneously (electronics, fashion, FMCG, pharma, food) before its operational model and unit economics were proven. OfBusiness stayed focused on raw materials for years before expanding.

**Rule:** Do not launch category 2 until category 1 has positive contribution margin and 120%+ NRR.

### Mistake 2: Building Technology Before Validating Demand

The classic startup mistake is especially costly in B2B, where technology builds are expensive. The first 20-30 transactions in an Indian B2B marketplace should be done manually — phone calls, WhatsApp, spreadsheets — to validate that buyers and sellers actually want to transact on a platform before investing in logistics and tech infrastructure.

### Mistake 3: Underestimating the Credit Requirement

The most common reason Indian B2B marketplaces fail to convert high GMV into revenue: buyers demand credit terms the platform cannot provide, so transactions happen offline (outside the platform). Solve credit from day one — even through a partnership if not your own NBFC.

### Mistake 4: Confusing GMV with Business Health

A marketplace can have high GMV with terrible unit economics. Track net revenue, gross margin, contribution margin, and NRR. GMV is a leading indicator; profitability is the lagging indicator that matters.
