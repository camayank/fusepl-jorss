## What Is DCF Valuation for Startups?

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) valuation estimates what your startup is worth today by projecting its future free cash flows for 5-10 years, then discounting those cash flows back to present value using a rate (WACC) that reflects the risk of actually achieving them. The sum of those discounted cash flows plus a terminal value equals your enterprise value. For Indian startups, that discount rate typically falls between 20-35% because early-stage companies carry far more execution risk than a listed Nifty 50 company. If you have at least 12 months of revenue data, DCF gives you a defensible, first-principles valuation that no investor can dismiss as hand-waving.

## Why This Matters for Indian Founders in 2026

The days of "vibes-based" valuations are over. After the 2022-2023 funding correction, Indian VCs now demand rigorous financial models before writing cheques. According to data tracked by Tracxn and Venture Intelligence, median pre-money valuations for Series A rounds in India dropped roughly 30-40% from their 2021 peaks and have only partially recovered. That means founders who walk into a pitch with a well-constructed DCF model -- not just a revenue multiple plucked from a TechCrunch headline -- gain an immediate credibility advantage.

There is also a regulatory reason this matters. While the 2024 Budget expanded the list of acceptable valuation methods and raised certain thresholds, DCF remains the most commonly used method by IBBI-registered valuers for determining fair market value of unlisted shares. If your CA or registered valuer is filing a Rule 11UA valuation report, there is a very high chance they are building a DCF model. Understanding the mechanics means you can challenge unreasonable assumptions rather than blindly accepting whatever number the valuer produces.

## How DCF Valuation Works: The Core Formula

The DCF formula is deceptively simple:

**Enterprise Value = Sum of [FCF_t / (1 + WACC)^t] + Terminal Value / (1 + WACC)^n**

Where:
- **FCF_t** = Free Cash Flow in year t
- **WACC** = Weighted Average Cost of Capital (your discount rate)
- **t** = Year number (1, 2, 3... n)
- **n** = Final year of your explicit projection period
- **Terminal Value** = Value of all cash flows beyond year n

In plain language: you forecast how much cash your startup will generate each year for the next 5-10 years, discount each year's cash flow to reflect the time value of money and risk, then add a "terminal value" representing everything beyond your projection period. The sum is what your business is worth today.

The difficulty is never the formula. It is the inputs. Get WACC wrong by 5 percentage points and your valuation swings by 40-60%. Use unrealistic revenue growth assumptions and the entire model collapses under investor scrutiny. The rest of this guide focuses on getting every input right for the Indian context.

## When DCF Works for Startups (and When It Does Not)

This is where most guides waste your time with hedging. Here is the direct answer:

### DCF Works Well When:

| Criteria | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| 12+ months of revenue history | You have a real baseline to project from, not guesses |
| Identifiable unit economics | CAC, LTV, gross margins are trackable and trending |
| Series A or later stage | You have enough data to make 5-year projections credible |
| Subscription/recurring revenue | Predictable cash flows make projections more reliable |
| Regulatory requirement (Rule 11UA) | DCF is the standard method for fair market value |

### DCF Does Not Work Well When:

| Criteria | What To Use Instead |
|---|---|
| Pre-revenue, pre-product | Berkus Method or Scorecard Method |
| Less than 6 months of revenue | Comparable Transactions (VC Method) |
| Highly pivotable business model | Scenario-weighted Real Options approach |
| Marketplace with zero take rate clarity | Revenue multiple benchmarking |
| Fundraising purely on vision/team | VC Method with comparable deal data |

The honest truth: if your startup is pre-revenue and you build a DCF, you are essentially fabricating the most important input (revenue). Every downstream number -- EBITDA, free cash flow, terminal value -- is fiction built on fiction. Use the VC method or scorecard method instead. Once you have 12+ months of real revenue data, come back to DCF.

## The Step-by-Step DCF Model for Indian Startups

### Step 1: Build Your Revenue Projection (Years 1-7)

Start with what you know: last twelve months (LTM) revenue. Then project forward using a growth rate that decays over time. No startup grows at 100% forever. Here is a realistic decay curve for an Indian SaaS startup:

| Year | Revenue (INR Cr) | YoY Growth | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| LTM (Base) | 4.0 | -- | Actual trailing revenue |
| Year 1 | 7.2 | 80% | Strong product-market fit traction |
| Year 2 | 11.5 | 60% | Expanding sales team, new segments |
| Year 3 | 16.1 | 40% | Market maturation begins |
| Year 4 | 20.9 | 30% | Category leadership or competition |
| Year 5 | 25.1 | 20% | Growth converging toward industry rate |
| Year 6 | 28.9 | 15% | Approaching steady state |
| Year 7 | 32.3 | 12% | Near terminal growth rate |

**Key rule:** Your Year 7 growth rate should be within striking distance of your terminal growth rate (more on that below). If you are projecting 50% growth in Year 7 and then a 5% terminal rate, the model has an internal contradiction that any sophisticated investor will flag.

**India-specific note:** For startups selling to Indian SMBs, revenue projections in INR need to account for pricing pressure that does not exist in US SaaS. Average contract values for Indian-market SaaS are typically 60-80% lower than US equivalents. Your model should reflect this in revenue-per-customer assumptions, not try to hide it in inflated customer count projections.

### Step 2: Derive EBITDA from Revenue

EBITDA = Revenue - COGS - Operating Expenses (before interest, tax, depreciation, amortization)

For startups, EBITDA margins typically start negative and improve over time as the business scales. Here is the realistic path:

| Year | Revenue (INR Cr) | EBITDA Margin | EBITDA (INR Cr) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | 7.2 | -25% | -1.80 |
| Year 2 | 11.5 | -10% | -1.15 |
| Year 3 | 16.1 | 5% | 0.81 |
| Year 4 | 20.9 | 15% | 3.14 |
| Year 5 | 25.1 | 20% | 5.02 |
| Year 6 | 28.9 | 22% | 6.36 |
| Year 7 | 32.3 | 24% | 7.75 |

**What investors check:** They compare your projected terminal EBITDA margin against publicly listed comparables. If Freshworks operates at roughly 10-15% EBITDA margins and Zoho (private, but estimated) at 25-30%, your terminal margin of 24% is defensible for a well-run Indian SaaS company. If you project 40% EBITDA margins with no explanation, expect pushback.

### Step 3: Calculate Free Cash Flow (FCF)

Free Cash Flow = EBITDA - Taxes - Capital Expenditure - Change in Working Capital

For Indian startups:

| Component | How to Estimate |
|---|---|
| **Taxes** | Use 25.17% (new tax regime for domestic companies incorporated after 01-Oct-2019 under Section 115BAA). For startups with carried-forward losses, effective tax rate may be 0% in early years. |
| **Capex** | Asset-light SaaS: 2-5% of revenue. Hardware/manufacturing: 10-20% of revenue. |
| **Working Capital Changes** | SaaS with annual prepay: negative working capital (good). D2C with inventory: significant working capital drag. |

Here is the FCF build for our example:

| Year | EBITDA (Cr) | Taxes (Cr) | Capex (Cr) | WC Change (Cr) | FCF (Cr) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | -1.80 | 0.00 | -0.22 | -0.10 | -2.12 |
| Year 2 | -1.15 | 0.00 | -0.35 | -0.15 | -1.65 |
| Year 3 | 0.81 | 0.00 | -0.48 | -0.12 | 0.21 |
| Year 4 | 3.14 | -0.45 | -0.63 | -0.10 | 1.96 |
| Year 5 | 5.02 | -1.06 | -0.75 | -0.08 | 3.13 |
| Year 6 | 6.36 | -1.40 | -0.87 | -0.06 | 4.03 |
| Year 7 | 7.75 | -1.75 | -0.97 | -0.05 | 4.98 |

Note: Taxes are zero in Years 1-3 because the company is utilizing carried-forward losses from prior loss-making years. This is common and acceptable -- just document the loss carry-forward schedule in your model.

### Step 4: Calculate WACC (The India-Specific Discount Rate)

WACC is where most Indian startup DCF models go wrong. Here is the exact build-up:

**WACC = (E/V) x Cost of Equity + (D/V) x Cost of Debt x (1 - Tax Rate)**

For most startups, there is no debt. The capital structure is 100% equity. So WACC simplifies to:

**WACC = Cost of Equity**

And Cost of Equity is calculated using the modified CAPM:

**Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Beta x Equity Risk Premium + Country Risk Premium + Size Premium + Startup-Specific Risk Premium**

Here is exactly how to source each component for India:

#### Risk-Free Rate: 7.10%

Use the yield on the 10-year Indian Government Security (G-Sec). As of early 2026, the benchmark 10-year G-Sec yield is approximately 6.9-7.1%. Use 7.10% as your reference point. Source this from RBI's weekly auction data or CCIL (Clearing Corporation of India Limited) daily reports.

**Do not use** the US 10-year Treasury yield (around 4.2-4.5%) unless you are building a USD-denominated model. If your cash flows are in INR, your risk-free rate must be in INR.

#### Equity Risk Premium (ERP) for India: 7.50-8.00%

Aswath Damodaran publishes updated equity risk premiums by country on his NYU Stern website. For India, his data (updated January 2025) shows:

- **Mature market ERP (base):** ~4.60%
- **India country risk premium (CRP):** ~2.91%
- **Total ERP for India:** ~7.51%

Some practitioners separate CRP from ERP. Others combine them. Either approach works as long as you do not double-count. In this guide, we use a combined India ERP of approximately 7.50%.

#### Beta: Sector-Dependent

Beta measures how volatile your startup's returns are relative to the market. Since your startup is not listed, use Damodaran's unlevered betas for the relevant sector and re-lever for your capital structure (which, for most startups, means the unlevered beta is your beta since there is no debt).

| Sector | Unlevered Beta (Damodaran, Jan 2025) |
|---|---|
| Software (System & Application) | 1.27 |
| Software (Internet/E-commerce) | 1.42 |
| Financial Services (Non-bank) | 0.82 |
| Healthcare Products | 1.01 |
| Retail (Online) | 1.52 |
| Entertainment / Media | 1.14 |
| Food Processing | 0.72 |

Source: Damodaran Online, "Betas by Sector (India)" dataset. Use the "Emerging Markets" or global dataset if India-specific is unavailable for your sub-sector.

#### Size Premium: 3.00-6.00%

Small companies carry more risk than large ones. For startups with revenue under INR 50 crore, add a size premium of 3-6%. Duff & Phelps (now Kroll) publishes size premium data, primarily for US companies, but the principle applies globally. For Indian micro-cap and nano-cap equivalents:

| Revenue Range | Size Premium |
|---|---|
| Above INR 500 Cr | 0-1% |
| INR 100-500 Cr | 1-3% |
| INR 10-100 Cr | 3-5% |
| Below INR 10 Cr | 5-7% |

#### Startup-Specific Risk Premium (Alpha): 3.00-10.00%

This is the most subjective component and the one most frequently debated. It captures risks unique to your company: founder dependency, customer concentration, regulatory uncertainty, technology risk.

| Risk Factor | Low Addition | High Addition |
|---|---|---|
| Single founder, no co-founder | +1% | +3% |
| Top 3 customers > 50% revenue | +1% | +3% |
| Regulatory risk (fintech, healthtech) | +1% | +3% |
| Technology obsolescence risk | +1% | +2% |
| No proven path to profitability | +1% | +3% |

**Typical total alpha for an Indian startup:** 3-6% for Series B+ with strong metrics. 6-10% for early Series A with limited track record.

#### Putting It All Together: WACC Calculation

For our Bengaluru SaaS startup raising Series A:

| Component | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Risk-Free Rate | 7.10% | 10-year G-Sec yield |
| Beta | 1.27 | Damodaran - Software sector |
| India ERP (combined) | 7.50% | Damodaran country data |
| Size Premium | 5.00% | Revenue under INR 10 Cr |
| Startup-Specific Alpha | 5.00% | Moderate risk profile |

**Cost of Equity = 7.10% + (1.27 x 7.50%) + 5.00% + 5.00%**

**Cost of Equity = 7.10% + 9.53% + 5.00% + 5.00% = 26.63%**

Round to **27%** for modeling simplicity.

This 27% WACC might seem high if you are used to seeing 8-12% for listed Indian companies. That is the point. Your startup is dramatically riskier than Infosys or TCS. A 27% discount rate is saying: "I need to earn 27% per year on this investment to compensate me for the risk of it going to zero." That is exactly what early-stage equity investors demand.

### Step 5: Calculate Terminal Value

Terminal value captures all cash flows beyond your explicit projection period. It typically accounts for 60-80% of total enterprise value in a startup DCF -- which is both its power and its danger.

**Terminal Value = FCF_n x (1 + g) / (WACC - g)**

Where:
- **FCF_n** = Free Cash Flow in final projected year
- **g** = Terminal growth rate (perpetuity growth rate)
- **WACC** = Your discount rate

#### What Terminal Growth Rate to Use for India

This is a critical India-specific decision. The terminal growth rate represents the rate at which your company will grow forever after the explicit projection period ends.

| Approach | Rate | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Conservative | 4.0-5.0% | Roughly India's long-term real GDP growth |
| Moderate | 5.0-6.0% | Real GDP growth + partial inflation |
| Aggressive | 6.0-7.0% | Nominal GDP growth rate proxy |

**The rule:** Terminal growth rate must be less than WACC (otherwise the formula produces infinity/nonsense) and should not exceed the long-term nominal GDP growth rate of the economy. For India, nominal GDP growth has averaged 10-12% over the past decade, but this includes high inflation periods. A terminal growth rate of 5-6% is defensible and commonly accepted by Indian valuers.

**For our example, we use 5.0%.**

Terminal Value = 4.98 x (1 + 0.05) / (0.27 - 0.05) = 5.23 / 0.22 = **INR 23.77 Cr**

### Step 6: Discount Everything to Present Value

Now discount each year's FCF and the terminal value back to today:

| Year | FCF (Cr) | Discount Factor (1.27^t) | Present Value (Cr) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | -2.12 | 1.270 | -1.67 |
| Year 2 | -1.65 | 1.613 | -1.02 |
| Year 3 | 0.21 | 2.048 | 0.10 |
| Year 4 | 1.96 | 2.601 | 0.75 |
| Year 5 | 3.13 | 3.304 | 0.95 |
| Year 6 | 4.03 | 4.196 | 0.96 |
| Year 7 | 4.98 | 5.329 | 0.93 |
| **Terminal Value** | **23.77** | **5.329** | **4.46** |

**Sum of PV of FCFs:** -1.67 + (-1.02) + 0.10 + 0.75 + 0.95 + 0.96 + 0.93 = **1.00 Cr**

**PV of Terminal Value:** 4.46 Cr

**Enterprise Value = 1.00 + 4.46 = INR 5.46 Cr**

### Step 7: Bridge from Enterprise Value to Equity Value

**Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash - Debt**

If the startup has INR 2.0 Cr cash on the balance sheet and zero debt:

**Equity Value = 5.46 + 2.00 - 0.00 = INR 7.46 Cr**

At a Series A where the founder holds 80% pre-dilution, the founder's stake is worth approximately INR 5.97 Cr on a DCF basis.

## Sensitivity Analysis: The Non-Negotiable Addition

No serious DCF model is complete without a sensitivity table. Investors will ask: "What happens if growth is slower? What if your WACC is higher?"

Build a two-way sensitivity table on WACC and terminal growth rate:

| | **g = 4%** | **g = 5%** | **g = 6%** | **g = 7%** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **WACC = 23%** | 7.58 | 8.92 | 10.89 | 14.07 |
| **WACC = 25%** | 6.72 | 7.72 | 9.12 | 11.19 |
| **WACC = 27%** | 6.04 | 6.82 | 7.86 | 9.31 |
| **WACC = 29%** | 5.49 | 6.11 | 6.92 | 7.99 |
| **WACC = 31%** | 5.03 | 5.53 | 6.17 | 7.00 |

*All values in INR Cr (Equity Value including cash)*

This table tells the real story. Your "base case" equity value is INR 7.46 Cr (at WACC 27%, g = 5%). But the reasonable range spans from INR 5.03 Cr to INR 14.07 Cr. When an investor says "I think your company is worth INR 6 Cr," you can point to the sensitivity table and say: "That implies you are using a 31% WACC with 5% terminal growth. Here is why I believe 27% is more appropriate" -- and then have a data-driven conversation instead of an emotional one.

## DCF vs. Other Valuation Methods: When to Use What

| Method | Best For | Weakness for Startups | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DCF** | Series A+ with revenue history | Garbage-in-garbage-out with bad assumptions | Rule 11UA filings, investor negotiations |
| **VC Method** | Seed to Series A | Relies on exit multiple assumptions | Quick back-of-envelope valuation |
| **Comparable Transactions** | Any stage with recent deal data | Indian startup deal data is sparse | Market-benchmarked sanity check |
| **Revenue Multiple** | SaaS, marketplaces | Ignores profitability entirely | Quick screening by VCs |
| **Scorecard Method** | Pre-revenue, angel stage | Highly subjective | Angel round pricing |
| **Berkus Method** | Idea / prototype stage | Caps valuation, not scalable | Very early conversations |

**The smart approach:** Use DCF as your primary method, then cross-check with one or two others. If your DCF says INR 7.5 Cr and comparable SaaS deals in India are happening at 8-12x ARR (which would imply INR 4.8-7.2 Cr for your INR 60L ARR), your DCF is in the right ballpark. If your DCF says INR 50 Cr and the market says INR 7 Cr, one of them is wrong -- and it is probably the DCF inputs.

## Seven Common Mistakes in Startup DCF Models

### Mistake 1: Using the US Risk-Free Rate for INR Cash Flows

If your cash flows are denominated in INR, your risk-free rate must be the Indian G-Sec yield (approximately 7.1%), not the US Treasury yield (approximately 4.3%). Mixing currencies in DCF is the single most common error and it understates your discount rate by 250-300 basis points, which inflates your valuation by 30-50%.

### Mistake 2: Hockey-Stick Revenue With No Decay

Projecting 100% growth for 7 straight years is not ambitious -- it is lazy. Revenue growth must decay toward the terminal growth rate. If Year 7 shows 40% growth and your terminal rate is 5%, the model has a cliff that makes the terminal value unreliable.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Working Capital

SaaS founders often ignore working capital changes because "we collect upfront." But if you have annual contracts with net-60 payment terms (common in Indian enterprise SaaS), receivables grow with revenue and consume cash. Model it.

### Mistake 4: Terminal Value Dominance Without Acknowledgment

If terminal value is more than 80% of your enterprise value, flag it. Do not hide it. Investors will calculate this ratio instantly. When terminal value dominates, it means your explicit projections barely matter -- the valuation is almost entirely driven by what you assume happens in perpetuity. That is a fragile model. Extend your projection period to 10 years to shift more value into the explicit period.

### Mistake 5: Not Adjusting Beta for India

Damodaran publishes global betas, US betas, and emerging market betas. If you grab a US-only software beta (roughly 1.05) instead of the emerging market software beta (roughly 1.27), you are understating risk. Always use the emerging market or India-specific dataset.

### Mistake 6: Forgetting Section 115BAA Tax Rate

Many models still use the old 30% corporate tax rate. Companies incorporated after October 1, 2019 (which includes most startups) can opt for the 25.17% effective rate under Section 115BAA (22% base + surcharge + cess). Use the correct rate for your entity.

### Mistake 7: Not Running Scenarios

A single-point DCF is a dartboard. Always present three scenarios: bear case (WACC +3%, growth -20%), base case, and bull case (WACC -2%, growth +15%). The range is the valuation. The base case is just the midpoint you negotiate from.

## Real Founder Scenario: A Bengaluru SaaS Startup's Series A DCF

When a Bengaluru-based vertical SaaS startup building for Indian manufacturers needed to justify its valuation to a leading VC fund, the founders made a common mistake: they grabbed a 10x ARR multiple from a US SaaS benchmark report and applied it to their INR 1.2 Cr ARR. That implied a INR 12 Cr pre-money valuation.

The VC pushed back. "Indian vertical SaaS does not trade at 10x. Show me the cash flows."

The founders came to **First Unicorn Startup's** valuation framework and rebuilt their model from first principles:

**Their actual inputs:**
- LTM Revenue: INR 1.2 Cr (growing 90% YoY)
- Gross margin: 78%
- Net revenue retention: 115%
- EBITDA margin: -35% (investing in product and sales)
- Cash on balance sheet: INR 3.5 Cr (from seed round)

**Their DCF build:**
- Projection period: 7 years
- Revenue growth: 90% decaying to 12% by Year 7
- EBITDA margins: -35% improving to 22% by Year 7
- WACC: 28% (they had a single founder and high customer concentration)
- Terminal growth rate: 5%

**Result:** The DCF produced an enterprise value of approximately INR 8.2 Cr. Adding INR 3.5 Cr cash, the equity value was approximately INR 11.7 Cr.

The VC's initial offer was INR 8 Cr pre-money. The founders presented the DCF with the sensitivity table showing a range of INR 9-16 Cr depending on assumptions. They negotiated to INR 11 Cr pre-money -- a 37.5% improvement over the initial offer.

**What made the difference:** The founders did not argue about multiples. They argued about assumptions. "You are using a 32% WACC -- here is why 28% is more appropriate given our 115% NRR and diversifying customer base." That is a conversation a VC respects. "But US SaaS gets 10x" is a conversation a VC dismisses.

The DCF did not "set" the valuation. The negotiation did. But the DCF gave the founders a structured framework to negotiate within, and it moved the conversation from opinions to data.

## Practitioner Insight: What IBBI-Registered Valuers Actually See Go Wrong

Registered valuers under the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India (IBBI) review dozens of startup DCF models annually for Rule 11UA compliance, ESOP valuations, and transaction advisory. Here are the patterns they flag most often:

**1. Revenue projections disconnected from market sizing.** A startup projects INR 500 Cr revenue in Year 7 but operates in a total addressable market of INR 800 Cr. That implies 62.5% market share, which is unrealistic for almost any startup. Valuers cross-check your revenue projections against credible TAM estimates.

**2. No documentation for the discount rate.** The model says "WACC = 25%" with no build-up showing risk-free rate, beta, ERP, size premium, or alpha. In a regulatory filing, every WACC component must be sourced and justified. "Industry standard" is not a source.

**3. Terminal value exceeding 85% of enterprise value.** When this happens, the valuer knows the explicit projections are almost irrelevant. Some valuers will require the founder to extend the projection period to 10 years or use an exit-multiple approach for terminal value instead of the perpetuity growth method.

**4. Inconsistent tax treatment.** The model applies 25.17% tax from Year 1 even though the company has INR 8 Cr of accumulated losses. Those losses create a tax shield that should be modeled explicitly. Overpaying modeled taxes understates FCF and depresses valuation.

**5. No comparison with other methods.** IBBI guidelines and RBI's regulatory framework for valuation reports increasingly expect a comparison of at least two methods. A standalone DCF without a cross-check against market multiples or comparable transactions raises credibility questions.

**The bottom line from practitioners:** A clean DCF model with well-documented assumptions, a proper WACC build-up, realistic growth decay, and a sensitivity analysis will survive regulatory scrutiny and investor diligence. A sloppy model with unjustified inputs will not -- regardless of what valuation it produces.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What discount rate should I use for an Indian startup?

For an Indian startup at Series A stage, expect a WACC (cost of equity, since most startups have no debt) between 22% and 32%. The exact rate depends on your sector beta (from Damodaran's dataset), the current 10-year G-Sec yield (approximately 7.1%), India's equity risk premium (approximately 7.5%), and company-specific premiums for size and risk. Build the rate component by component using CAPM -- never just pick a round number.

### Can I use DCF for a pre-revenue startup?

You can, but you should not. DCF requires cash flow projections, and projecting cash flows for a company with zero revenue history means every input is a guess. The model will produce a number, but that number is entirely driven by assumptions with no empirical basis. Use the Berkus Method, Scorecard Method, or VC Method for pre-revenue startups. Switch to DCF once you have 12 or more months of revenue data.

### How many years should I project cash flows in the DCF model?

Project 5-7 years for startups with established revenue, or up to 10 years for very early-stage companies where profitability is far away. The key constraint is that your final projection year should show the business in or near a steady state -- positive FCF, growth rate within 2-3x of the terminal rate, and stable margins. If your company will not reach steady state for 8 years, use 8 years as your projection period.

### What terminal growth rate should I use for an Indian startup?

Use 5-6% for most Indian startup DCF models. This roughly approximates India's long-term real GDP growth rate plus a portion of inflation. The terminal growth rate must be lower than your WACC (otherwise the formula breaks) and should not exceed the long-term nominal GDP growth rate of the economy (10-12% for India historically). A rate of 5% is conservative and widely accepted. Going above 7% will draw scrutiny from any sophisticated investor or valuer.

### Is DCF required for Rule 11UA compliance?

DCF is not the only method permitted under Rule 11UA for valuing unlisted shares, but it is by far the most commonly used by IBBI-registered valuers. Rule 11UA also accepts the Net Asset Value (NAV) method and, following 2023-2024 amendments, allows pricing based on a compulsorily convertible preference share framework and methods used by specified funds. For most tech startups, DCF is the standard because NAV dramatically understates value for asset-light companies.

### How does DCF differ from the VC method of valuation?

The VC method works backward from a target exit: estimate exit value in 5-7 years using a revenue or earnings multiple, then discount it back at the VC's target IRR (typically 30-50%) to get today's pre-money valuation. DCF works forward from projected cash flows: estimate each year's free cash flow, discount them at WACC, and add terminal value. DCF is more granular and assumption-transparent. The VC method is faster and better for very early-stage companies. Both should produce valuations in the same ballpark if assumptions are consistent.

### Should I use INR or USD for my DCF model?

Use the currency your revenues are denominated in. If you sell to Indian customers and collect in INR, build the model in INR and use Indian G-Sec yields as your risk-free rate. If you are a SaaS company with 80%+ revenue in USD, build in USD and use US Treasury yields. Never mix currencies -- that is, never use USD risk-free rates with INR cash flows or vice versa. If you have mixed-currency revenues, build in your dominant currency and convert the rest at forward exchange rate estimates.

### How do I handle negative cash flows in early years of the DCF?

Negative cash flows in early years are normal for startups and do not break the model. Discount them exactly like positive cash flows -- they just subtract from enterprise value instead of adding to it. The model works as long as cash flows eventually turn positive and the terminal value is positive. If your model shows negative FCF in all 7 projection years and relies entirely on terminal value for a positive enterprise value, extend your projection period until at least 2-3 years of positive FCF appear in the explicit forecast.

## Key Takeaways

- **DCF works for Indian startups at Series A and beyond** with 12+ months of revenue data. For earlier stages, use the VC method or scorecard method instead.

- **Your WACC for an Indian startup will typically be 22-32%**, built from the 10-year G-Sec yield (~7.1%), Damodaran's India ERP (~7.5%), a sector beta, a size premium, and a company-specific risk premium. Build it component by component and document every input.

- **Terminal value will dominate your DCF** (60-80% of enterprise value), which is why the terminal growth rate (use 5-6% for India) and final-year FCF assumptions are the two most important numbers in your model. Get these right, and the model holds. Get them wrong, and nothing else matters.

- **Always present a sensitivity table** showing enterprise value across a range of WACC and terminal growth rate assumptions. The range is your valuation -- the base case is just the starting point for negotiation.

- **Cross-check DCF against at least one other method** (revenue multiples, comparable transactions, or VC method). If the methods diverge wildly, investigate why before presenting to investors or filing under Rule 11UA.
