## What Is WACC and Why Does It Destroy Startup Valuations?

WACC stands for Weighted Average Cost of Capital. It is the discount rate you plug into a DCF model to convert future cash flows into today's money. If your startup is projected to generate ₹10 crore of free cash flow five years from now, WACC tells you what that ₹10 crore is actually worth today — accounting for the real possibility that you never get there.

The formula is straightforward:

```
WACC = Ke × [E / (E + D)] + Kd × (1 - T) × [D / (E + D)]

Where:
  Ke  = Cost of equity
  Kd  = Cost of debt
  T   = Corporate tax rate
  E   = Market value of equity
  D   = Market value of debt
  E/(E+D) = Equity weight in capital structure
  D/(E+D) = Debt weight in capital structure
```

Get WACC wrong by even 5 percentage points and you can swing a startup's DCF valuation by 40-60%. A founder who builds a DCF with a 15% WACC when the correct rate is 30% will present an enterprise value that looks twice as large as it should be. An investor who spots this immediately discounts the founder's credibility across every other number in the deck.

This is not academic. In 2026, Indian VCs reviewing Series A deals routinely back-calculate the implied WACC from a founder's DCF model. If that number is below 25% for a pre-revenue or early-revenue startup, the first question in the meeting will be: "How did you arrive at your discount rate?" Most founders cannot answer it. This guide makes sure you can.

## Why Indian Startup WACC Is Higher Than US Startup WACC

Before building the components, it is worth understanding why WACC for an Indian startup in 2026 is structurally higher than WACC for a comparable US startup. There are four reasons.

### 1. Higher Risk-Free Rate

The risk-free rate is the starting point for the cost of equity. In the US, this is the 10-year Treasury yield — approximately 4.2-4.5% in early 2026. In India, the equivalent is the 10-year Government of India bond yield, which stood at approximately **7.0-7.2% in March 2026** (RBI data). India's higher inflation, currency risk, and sovereign credit profile mean the baseline cost of money is 270-300 basis points higher than in the US before you account for any startup-specific risk.

### 2. Country Risk Premium

India carries a country risk premium (CRP) that US startups do not pay. Based on Aswath Damodaran's January 2026 estimates (NYU Stern), India's total equity risk premium is approximately **10.5-11.5%**, comprising a mature market ERP of roughly 4.6% and a CRP of approximately 5.9-6.9%. The CRP reflects Moody's Baa3 / S&P BBB- sovereign rating for India, which places it in investment grade but at the lower end, resulting in a sovereign credit spread of approximately 160-180 basis points over the US 10-year as of early 2026. Damodaran's methodology converts this bond spread to an equity risk premium by multiplying by a relative volatility factor of approximately 1.5x.

### 3. Currency Risk for Investors

Many institutional investors in Indian startups are USD-denominated funds (Sequoia/Peak XV, Lightspeed, Accel, Matrix). Their returns are ultimately measured in US dollars. The Indian rupee has depreciated against the USD at approximately 3-4% per annum over the past decade. This expected depreciation is an additional hurdle that USD investors mentally embed in their return requirements — even if it is not always explicitly modeled in WACC.

### 4. Smaller and Less Liquid Exit Market

The Indian public markets are deep (NSE market cap exceeded $4 trillion in 2025), but the tech IPO market for startups is younger, less liquid, and more volatile than the US. Secondary buyouts and strategic acquisitions, the other primary exit routes, also happen at lower multiples in India than in the US for comparable businesses. A lower expected exit multiple mechanically requires a higher annualised return from the investment period, which translates into a higher discount rate.

## Component 1: Cost of Equity (Ke)

The cost of equity is what equity investors demand as their minimum return for bearing the risk of owning shares in your startup. It is calculated using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):

```
Ke = Rf + β × ERP

Where:
  Rf  = Risk-free rate (Indian 10-year G-Sec yield)
  β   = Beta (systematic risk of the business)
  ERP = Equity Risk Premium for India (country-adjusted)
```

### Step 1: Risk-Free Rate (Rf)

Use the current 10-year Government of India bond yield as your risk-free rate. As of March 2026, this is approximately **7.05%**. Source: RBI or NSE/BSE government securities data. Do not use a short-term rate (91-day T-bill) — you are discounting long-term cash flows, so match the duration.

### Step 2: Equity Risk Premium (ERP) for India

Use Damodaran's India-adjusted ERP. As of January 2026, Damodaran's estimate for India's total ERP is approximately **10.5-11.0%**. For a conservative model, use **10.7%**. For an aggressive model, use **11.2%**.

If you are modeling for a USD investor, you may additionally need to incorporate an explicit currency risk premium of 2-3%, which would push ERP to 12.5-14%. For simplicity, most models use the INR-denominated ERP and accept that USD investors will apply their own hurdle rate adjustment separately.

### Step 3: Beta (β)

Beta measures how much your business's returns correlate with and amplify market movements. For listed companies, beta is calculated from historical stock prices. For private startups, you use an "unlevered sector beta" — the beta of comparable listed companies with debt stripped out — and then re-lever it for your capital structure.

Damodaran publishes sector betas for India (Emerging Markets dataset). Key unlevered betas for Indian startup sectors as of January 2026:

| Sector | Unlevered Beta (India) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Software / SaaS | 0.95 | Enterprise and consumer SaaS |
| Fintech / Financial Services | 1.10 | Higher regulatory and credit risk |
| E-Commerce / Marketplace | 1.15 | High GMV volatility |
| Edtech | 1.05 | Post-2022 sector risk repricing |
| Healthtech / Digital Health | 0.90 | Relatively defensive |
| Agritech | 1.20 | Weather and commodity exposure |
| Logistics | 1.00 | Asset intensity moderates beta |
| Consumer / D2C | 1.10 | Brand and distribution risk |

For a pre-revenue or early-revenue startup, apply an additional illiquidity and small-firm premium. Practitioners typically add **0.2-0.5 to the unlevered beta** for startups relative to their sector comparables, or alternatively add a specific small company premium of 2-4% directly to Ke.

For a typical seed/Series A startup:
- Use sector unlevered beta
- Re-lever for your capital structure using: β_levered = β_unlevered × (1 + (1-T) × D/E)
- Most early-stage Indian startups are equity-funded, so D/E ≈ 0, meaning β_levered ≈ β_unlevered

### Calculating Ke: Worked Examples

**Example A: Seed-stage B2C consumer startup**
```
Rf  = 7.05%
β   = 1.10 (consumer sector) + 0.40 (illiquidity premium) = 1.50
ERP = 10.7%

Ke  = 7.05% + 1.50 × 10.7%
Ke  = 7.05% + 16.05%
Ke  = 23.1%

Add small-company premium: +5%
Adjusted Ke = 28.1%
```

**Example B: Series A B2B SaaS startup**
```
Rf  = 7.05%
β   = 0.95 (SaaS sector) + 0.25 (illiquidity premium) = 1.20
ERP = 10.7%

Ke  = 7.05% + 1.20 × 10.7%
Ke  = 7.05% + 12.84%
Ke  = 19.89%

Add small-company premium: +3%
Adjusted Ke = 22.9%
```

**Example C: Series B profitable fintech**
```
Rf  = 7.05%
β   = 1.10 (fintech) + 0.10 (modest illiquidity) = 1.20
ERP = 10.7%

Ke  = 7.05% + 1.20 × 10.7%
Ke  = 7.05% + 12.84%
Ke  = 19.89%

No additional premium for established business
Adjusted Ke = 19.9%
```

## Component 2: Country Risk Premium — Understanding the Mechanics

The country risk premium deserves its own section because Indian founders often misapply it. There are two schools of thought:

**School 1 (Damodaran method):** Build CRP into the ERP itself. When you use Damodaran's India-specific ERP of 10.7%, CRP is already embedded. You do not add it separately. This is the cleaner approach.

**School 2 (Additive method):** Start with the US ERP (approximately 4.6%), add a country risk premium separately (approximately 5.9-6.9% for India), and add the risk-free rate. The math is equivalent but presented differently.

Most Indian CA valuers and investment bankers use the additive method because they learned it from CFA materials. For consistency, use whichever method your investors and their valuers prefer. If your lead VC is a US fund, they will likely use the Damodaran all-in ERP. If you are filing a Rule 11UA valuation, your IBBI valuer will likely use the additive CRP method.

The sovereign spread for India (Moody's Baa3 rating) versus a AAA-rated benchmark is approximately **155-175 basis points** as of March 2026, based on Bloomberg sovereign CDS data. Damodaran's conversion factor to equity risk premium is approximately 1.5x, giving a CRP of roughly **2.3-2.6% added to the base ERP** — though the all-in numbers cited above already incorporate this.

## Component 3: Cost of Debt (Kd)

The cost of debt is the interest rate your startup pays on borrowed money, after accounting for the tax deductibility of interest (the "tax shield").

```
After-tax Kd = Pre-tax Kd × (1 - T)

Where T = corporate tax rate
```

India's corporate tax rate for domestic companies (non-MAT, Sections 115BAA) is **22% + 10% surcharge + 4% cess = effective 25.17%** for startups with turnover under applicable thresholds. For Startup India registered entities, there is a 3-year tax holiday under Section 80-IAC, but this only applies if the startup has taxable profits — most early-stage startups are loss-making, meaning the tax shield on debt is currently worth zero.

### Debt Rates for Indian Startups in 2026

Indian startups pay very different rates depending on their stage and funding source:

| Debt Type | Lender | Typical Rate (2026) | Who Can Access |
|---|---|---|---|
| Venture Debt | Alteria, Trifecta, Innoven | 15-18% | Series A+ with institutional equity |
| Revenue-Based Financing | Recur, GetVantage, Velocity | 18-24% (effective) | Profitable or near-profitable |
| NBFC Term Loan | CredAvenue, Lendingkart | 14-18% | Established unit economics |
| Working Capital (PSU Bank) | SBI, Bank of Baroda | 10.5-13% | Profitability required, rare for startups |
| SIDBI Startup Loans | SIDBI (Fund of Funds) | 11-14% | DPIIT-registered, limited quantum |
| CGTMSE-backed Loan | Various banks | 10-12% + guarantee fee | Micro/small, collateral substitute |

For a typical VC-backed Series A startup with venture debt:
```
Pre-tax Kd = 16%
T (effective, assuming loss-making) = 0%
After-tax Kd = 16% × (1 - 0) = 16%

(If profitable, T = 25.17%)
After-tax Kd = 16% × (1 - 0.2517) = 11.97%
```

## Component 4: Capital Structure Weighting

The weights assigned to equity and debt in the WACC formula should ideally reflect target or market-value capital structure, not book values. For Indian startups:

**Pre-Series A / Seed:** Virtually 100% equity-funded. A few founders take SAFE notes or convertible notes from angels, but these are treated as equity for WACC purposes (they convert to equity). E/(E+D) ≈ 1.0, D/(E+D) ≈ 0.

**Series A:** 85-95% equity. Some startups take venture debt representing 10-20% of the equity round, so D/(E+D) might be 5-15%.

**Series B+:** 75-90% equity. Venture debt, RBF, and working capital lines become more common. D/(E+D) might be 10-25% for capital-efficient businesses.

**Late-stage / Pre-IPO:** 60-80% equity possible, particularly for fintech and lending businesses with debt funding their loan books.

For most early-stage Indian startups, the practical implication is that **WACC ≈ Ke** because the equity weight is 90-100%. The debt component, even if present, moves WACC by less than 2 percentage points.

## Worked Example: Series A B2B SaaS Startup WACC

Let us build a complete WACC for a hypothetical Series A B2B SaaS startup called DataFlow India. The company has:
- ₹3.5 Cr ARR, growing 120% YoY
- 5 enterprise customers, average ACV ₹70 lakhs
- 12 months runway post-Series A close
- Pre-money valuation of ₹35 Cr (approximately $4.2M)
- ₹2 Cr venture debt from Alteria at 16%
- Capital structure: ₹33 Cr equity, ₹2 Cr debt

### Step 1: Risk-Free Rate
```
Rf = 7.05% (10-year G-Sec yield, March 2026)
```

### Step 2: Beta
```
Sector: Software/SaaS
Damodaran unlevered beta (India, Jan 2026) = 0.95
Company-specific adjustment: Series A, 5 customers = +0.30
Unlevered beta = 1.25

D/E ratio = 2/33 = 0.061
Tax rate = 0% (loss-making)
Levered beta = 1.25 × (1 + (1 - 0) × 0.061) = 1.25 × 1.061 = 1.33
```

### Step 3: Equity Risk Premium
```
India ERP (Damodaran, Jan 2026) = 10.7%
```

### Step 4: Cost of Equity
```
Ke = Rf + β_levered × ERP
Ke = 7.05% + 1.33 × 10.7%
Ke = 7.05% + 14.23%
Ke = 21.28%

Add small-company premium (Series A startup, limited track record): 3.0%
Adjusted Ke = 24.28% ≈ 24.3%
```

### Step 5: Cost of Debt
```
Pre-tax Kd = 16% (Alteria venture debt rate)
Tax rate = 0% (loss-making startup)
After-tax Kd = 16%
```

### Step 6: Capital Structure Weights
```
Total capital = ₹33 Cr (equity) + ₹2 Cr (debt) = ₹35 Cr
E/(E+D) = 33/35 = 94.3%
D/(E+D) = 2/35 = 5.7%
```

### Step 7: WACC
```
WACC = 24.3% × 94.3% + 16% × 5.7%
WACC = 22.92% + 0.91%
WACC = 23.83% ≈ 24%
```

**DataFlow India's WACC: approximately 24%**

This is on the lower end of the Series A B2B SaaS range (27-33%) because DataFlow has strong ARR growth, enterprise customers, and a reasonably clean capital structure. A founder presenting this WACC needs to defend the small-company premium and beta assumptions — both are judgment calls that an investor will probe.

## Why VCs Use a Higher Hurdle Rate Than Your WACC

You may notice a disconnect: this analysis produces a Series A WACC of 24-30%, but when you talk to VCs, they describe return targets of 30-40%+ on individual investments. Why the gap?

VCs are not applying your startup's WACC to their portfolio decisions. They are applying their **fund-level hurdle rate** — the return they need across all portfolio companies to deliver an acceptable multiple to their LPs.

The math works like this:
```
VC fund structure assumption:
- 10 investments from a ₹100 Cr fund
- Expected outcome based on typical power law distribution:
  * 4 investments go to zero (₹40 Cr lost)
  * 3 investments return 1x (₹30 Cr returned)
  * 2 investments return 3x (₹20 Cr → ₹60 Cr)
  * 1 investment returns 20x (₹10 Cr → ₹200 Cr)

Total returned: ₹0 + ₹30 Cr + ₹60 Cr + ₹200 Cr = ₹290 Cr
Fund multiple: 2.9x on ₹100 Cr over 7 years
IRR: approximately 16.5%

For the fund to target 25% net IRR (a good but not exceptional fund):
- The "home run" investment needs to return 30-40x on capital
- The next-tier investments need 3-5x
- This requires entry valuations to be conservative enough that a 10x revenue
  growth gets you to the target return
```

In practice, VCs mentally apply a **30-40% annual return requirement** to their individual investments not because that is your WACC, but because they need that return potential to justify the binary risk of the investment failing entirely. WACC-based analysis does not capture the fat-tail downside (zero) that VC investments carry.

For founders, the implication is: your DCF WACC (24-30% for Series A) tells you what an academically rigorous fair value is; the VC hurdle rate (30-40%) tells you what they need to see in exit scenarios to invest. Both numbers matter and they serve different purposes.

## How IBBI Valuers Approach WACC in India

If you are commissioning a Rule 11UA valuation report for angel tax compliance, your IBBI-registered valuer will build a WACC. Here is what they typically do versus what the theoretically correct approach is:

| Component | IBBI Valuer Typical Approach | Theoretically Correct Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Risk-free rate | 10-year G-Sec yield (correct) | Same |
| Beta | Industry beta from NSE listed peers | Damodaran unlevered beta, re-levered |
| ERP | 5.5-7% (market practice) | Damodaran India ERP (10.7%) |
| Small company premium | Often omitted | 2-5% for startups |
| Resulting WACC | Often 15-22% | Should be 22-35% |

**The practical implication:** IBBI valuers often produce WACC numbers that are too low for genuine startups, resulting in higher FMV numbers. This is actually beneficial for angel tax purposes (higher FMV = less likely the investor paid above FMV = less angel tax risk). However, for fundraising and internal decision-making, using the theoretically correct higher WACC gives you a more honest picture of what your business is actually worth.

## Common WACC Mistakes by Indian Startup Founders

### Mistake 1: Using the Indian bank lending rate as the risk-free rate
Some founders use the SBI MCLR or repo rate (6.5% in early 2026) as the risk-free rate. Wrong. Use the 10-year government bond yield (7.05%). The risk-free rate should match the duration of your cash flow projection period.

### Mistake 2: Using a US beta for an Indian startup
A SaaS startup's beta in the US is approximately 1.1-1.3 for a mature listed company. Indian SaaS companies on public markets show higher betas (1.3-1.6) due to lower liquidity and higher volatility. Using a US beta understates your WACC.

### Mistake 3: Applying a tax shield when the company is loss-making
The interest tax shield (the (1-T) term in the debt cost) only exists if you have taxable profits. Early-stage startups with carried-forward losses have no current-year tax liability, so the after-tax cost of debt equals the pre-tax cost. Many models incorrectly apply a 25% tax shield and understate the debt cost.

### Mistake 4: Using book value weights instead of market value weights
The weights in the WACC formula should reflect the market value of equity and debt, not the book value. Use your pre-money valuation as the market value of equity for weighting purposes.

### Mistake 5: Never updating WACC as the startup matures
WACC should decline as your startup de-risks. After Series B with proven unit economics and positive EBITDA, you should be rebuilding your WACC with a lower beta, lower illiquidity premium, and potentially a higher debt weight if you have started using structured debt. A founder who uses a 40% WACC at Series C is leaving money on the table.

## Building the WACC Spreadsheet: Template Structure

Here is the calculation structure you should use in your financial model:

```
Tab: WACC Calculator

Section A: Risk-Free Rate
  Cell A1: "10-year G-Sec Yield (source: RBI)"
  Cell A2: 7.05%  [input — update quarterly]

Section B: Equity Risk Premium
  Cell B1: "India ERP (source: Damodaran, update annually)"
  Cell B2: 10.7%  [input — update annually from damodaran.com]

Section C: Beta
  Cell C1: "Sector Unlevered Beta (Damodaran EM dataset)"
  Cell C2: [input]
  Cell C3: "Startup Illiquidity Premium (add 0.2-0.4)"
  Cell C4: [input]
  Cell C5: "D/E Ratio" = D3/E_market_value
  Cell C6: "Tax Rate (0% if loss-making, 25.17% if profitable)"
  Cell C7: "Levered Beta" = (C2+C4) × (1 + (1-C6) × C5)

Section D: Cost of Equity
  Cell D1: "Ke" = A2 + C7 × B2
  Cell D2: "Small Company Premium (2-5%)"
  Cell D3: "Adjusted Ke" = D1 + D2

Section E: Cost of Debt
  Cell E1: "Pre-tax Kd (current debt rate)"
  Cell E2: "Tax Rate" [link to C6]
  Cell E3: "After-tax Kd" = E1 × (1 - E2)

Section F: Capital Structure
  Cell F1: "Market Value of Equity (pre-money)"
  Cell F2: "Total Debt"
  Cell F3: "Total Capital" = F1 + F2
  Cell F4: "Equity Weight" = F1/F3
  Cell F5: "Debt Weight" = F2/F3

Section G: WACC
  Cell G1: "WACC" = D3 × F4 + E3 × F5
```

## Stage-Specific WACC Decision Tree

Use this decision tree to quickly arrive at a starting WACC estimate before doing the full calculation:

```
Is your startup pre-revenue?
  YES → Start at 45-55% WACC. Reduce if:
        - Strong team (IIT/IIM/FAANG background): -3%
        - Signed LOIs or pilots: -3%
        - Proven sector (SaaS vs experimental): -2%
        Minimum: 35%
  NO → Do you have 12+ months of revenue?
        YES → Is revenue growing >50% YoY?
               YES → Is EBITDA positive?
                      YES → Use 18-25% (Series B+ range)
                      NO  → Use 25-32% (Series A range)
               NO  → Use 28-38% (struggling growth)
        NO → Use 35-45% (early revenue, high uncertainty)
```

## WACC in the Context of a Full DCF Model

WACC does not exist in isolation. It is one of three driving assumptions in a DCF model, alongside:

1. **Revenue growth projection** — how fast you grow
2. **Free cash flow margin** — how efficiently you convert revenue to cash
3. **Terminal value assumption** — what multiple you assign to exit-year cash flows or revenue

All three interact. A higher revenue growth rate supports a lower WACC (because faster-growing businesses carry less execution risk at the margin). A higher FCF margin supports a lower WACC (less reinvestment risk). A lower terminal value assumption requires a lower WACC to produce the same enterprise value.
