# Startup India Seed Fund Scheme — The Complete 2026 Guide

The Startup India Seed Fund Scheme (SISFS) gives early-stage startups up to **₹20 lakh as a grant** for proof of concept and prototype development, and up to **₹50 lakh as convertible debentures or debt** for product trials, market entry, and commercialization. The total scheme corpus is **₹945 crore**, disbursed through **300+ approved incubators** across India over a four-year period starting April 2021.

If you are a DPIIT-recognized startup, incorporated less than two years ago, and have not received more than ₹10 lakh in monetary support under any other central or state government scheme, you are eligible. You do not apply to the government directly. You apply through an approved incubator in your state or sector.

This guide covers everything: eligibility down to the fine print, the exact application process, the complete list of approved incubators state by state, real disbursement data, how incubators actually select startups, what happens after you get the money, and when this fund makes sense versus when you should look elsewhere.

---

## Why the Seed Fund Scheme Is the Most Underutilized Funding Source in India

Here is a striking fact. The government allocated ₹945 crore for this scheme. By the end of FY 2024-25, approximately ₹550-600 crore had been disbursed to incubators. But the actual disbursement to startups has been slower. Many approved incubators still have undeployed capital sitting in their SISFS accounts.

This means there is money available, right now, that startups are not applying for.

Why? Three reasons:

**First, founders do not know about it.** Most first-time founders in Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities have never heard of SISFS. They know about Shark Tank. They do not know that the government will hand them ₹20 lakh as a non-dilutive grant.

**Second, the process looks intimidating.** The application goes through an incubator, not directly to the government. Founders assume this means knowing someone at the incubator. It does not. The process is online, structured, and merit-based.

**Third, founders in funded ecosystems skip it.** A Bangalore or Mumbai founder raising an angel round of ₹50 lakh to ₹1 crore does not bother with a ₹20 lakh government grant. But they should. It is non-dilutive capital. Every rupee of grant money you take is a rupee of equity you do not give away.

The startups that benefit most from SISFS are exactly the ones least likely to know about it: early-stage founders in states like Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Assam, and Jharkhand building real products for real markets, who need ₹10-50 lakh to go from idea to first customers.

This guide exists to close that gap.

## Scheme Overview: The Numbers That Matter

### The Basics

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Scheme Name** | Startup India Seed Fund Scheme (SISFS) |
| **Launched** | April 1, 2021 |
| **Administered By** | Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT), Ministry of Commerce and Industry |
| **Total Corpus** | ₹945 crore |
| **Duration** | 4 years from April 2021 (extended operations continue through approved incubators) |
| **Disbursement Channel** | Through DPIIT-approved incubators |
| **Number of Approved Incubators** | 300+ (across 30+ states and union territories) |
| **Startups Supported** | 7,000+ startups targeted; 4,500+ startups have received support as of early 2025 |
| **Governing Body** | Experts Advisory Committee (EAC) constituted by DPIIT |
| **Application Portal** | seedfund.startupindia.gov.in |

### What You Actually Get

The scheme provides two types of financial assistance, and they serve different stages:

**Type 1: Grant (up to ₹20 lakh)**

- Purpose: Validation of proof of concept, prototype development, product trials
- Nature: Grant. You do not pay this back. You do not give equity for it.
- Disbursement: Directly from the approved incubator to your startup's bank account
- Conditions: Must be used for the stated purpose. Receipts and utilization certificates required.

**Type 2: Debt/Convertible Debentures (up to ₹50 lakh)**

- Purpose: Market entry, commercialization, scaling after prototype stage
- Nature: Convertible debentures or debt instruments. This may convert to equity or be repaid.
- Disbursement: From the approved incubator, typically in tranches linked to milestones
- Conditions: Terms set by the incubator. Conversion terms negotiated at the time of investment.

**Important clarification:** You do not automatically get both. The incubator evaluates your stage and decides whether you qualify for the grant component, the debt component, or both. Most early-stage startups receive the grant of ₹10-20 lakh. The ₹50 lakh debt component is typically for startups that are further along.

### How the Money Flows

The flow of funds works like this:

1. DPIIT allocates funds from the ₹945 crore corpus to the EAC
2. EAC approves incubators and allocates funds to them (typically ₹5 crore per incubator)
3. Incubators receive the funds in their designated SISFS bank accounts
4. Incubators evaluate startup applications and select beneficiaries
5. Incubators disburse funds to selected startups
6. Incubators report utilization back to EAC/DPIIT

Each approved incubator typically receives **₹5 crore** over the scheme period. This means each incubator can fund roughly 25-50 startups depending on the mix of grants and debt instruments.

## Eligibility Criteria: The Complete Checklist

### For Startups

You must meet ALL of the following criteria:

| # | Criterion | Detail |
|---|-----------|--------|
| 1 | **DPIIT Recognition** | Your startup must be recognized by DPIIT under the Startup India initiative. This is a separate registration you must complete first at startupindia.gov.in. |
| 2 | **Incorporation Date** | Your startup must have been incorporated not more than 2 years ago at the time of application to the incubator. Incorporation means the date on your Certificate of Incorporation (for Pvt Ltd or LLP) or Udyam registration (for proprietorship/partnership). |
| 3 | **No Prior Large Funding** | You must not have received more than ₹10 lakh of monetary support under any other Central or State Government scheme. This does not include tax benefits, exemptions, or non-monetary support. Only direct monetary grants/funding count. |
| 4 | **Entity Type** | Must be a Private Limited Company, Registered Partnership Firm, or Limited Liability Partnership (LLP). Sole proprietorships registered under Udyam are also eligible in practice but check with the specific incubator. |
| 5 | **Indian Operations** | The startup must have its primary operations in India. |
| 6 | **Shareholding** | Indian promoters must hold at least 51% of shares at the time of application. |
| 7 | **Not a Subsidiary** | The startup must not be a subsidiary, division, or spinoff of an existing large company. |
| 8 | **Use of Funds** | The startup must use the funds for the purpose stated in the application — proof of concept, prototype, product trial, market entry, or commercialization. |

### Common Eligibility Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Not having DPIIT recognition first.** This is a prerequisite. If you have not registered on the Startup India portal and received your DPIIT recognition certificate, you cannot apply for SISFS. Getting DPIIT recognition takes 2-7 days and is free. Do it now.

**Mistake 2: Counting from founding date, not incorporation date.** The two-year clock starts from your date of incorporation as shown on your Certificate of Incorporation, not from when you started working on the idea or registered a domain name.

**Mistake 3: Confusing ₹10 lakh monetary threshold.** If you received a ₹5 lakh grant from a state startup scheme, you are still eligible (you have only used ₹5 lakh of the ₹10 lakh threshold). But if you received ₹8 lakh from a state scheme and ₹3 lakh from another central scheme, totaling ₹11 lakh, you are not eligible.

**Mistake 4: Assuming you need to be located in the incubator's city.** You do not. Many incubators accept virtual incubatees. You can apply to an incubator in a different city or even a different state. However, some incubators prefer local startups, so applying to one in your region improves your chances.

### For Incubators (Why This Matters to You)

Understanding the incubator eligibility criteria helps you identify which incubators are likely to be well-run and worth applying to:

- Must be a legal entity (registered society, trust, Section 8 company, or similar)
- Must have been operational for at least two years at the time of application to EAC
- Must have physical infrastructure: minimum 3,000 sq ft of built-up area
- Must have at least 5 startups physically incubated at the time of application
- Must have a full-time CEO and at least two other full-time team members
- Must have a functional website with details of incubation activities
- Must have access to mentors, domain experts, and industry networks
- Must have clearly defined governance and selection processes

## Step-by-Step Application Process

### Step 1: Get DPIIT Recognition (If You Haven't Already)

**Time required: 2-7 days**

1. Go to **startupindia.gov.in**
2. Click "Register" and create an account
3. Fill in the startup recognition application form:
   - Entity details (name, CIN/LLPIN, date of incorporation)
   - Founder details
   - Brief description of your innovation or business model
   - Upload Certificate of Incorporation
   - Upload a brief write-up about how your entity is working towards innovation
4. Submit the application
5. DPIIT reviews and issues the recognition certificate
6. You receive a **DPIIT Recognition Number** — save this, you will need it

**Pro tip:** The write-up about innovation does not need to describe a deep-tech breakthrough. It needs to describe how your startup is creating a new product, process, or service, or significantly improving an existing one. Most legitimate startups qualify.

### Step 2: Identify the Right Incubator

**Time required: 1-3 days of research**

Go to **seedfund.startupindia.gov.in** and navigate to the incubator list. You will see the full list of 300+ approved incubators.

How to choose the right incubator:

- **Geography:** Start with incubators in your state. They often prioritize local startups and can provide in-person mentoring.
- **Sector alignment:** Some incubators specialize in specific sectors (agritech, healthtech, fintech, social impact). Choose one that understands your domain.
- **Track record:** Look for incubators that have already disbursed funds to startups. Some incubators were approved but have been slow to deploy. Ask directly: "How many startups have you funded under SISFS so far?"
- **Incubation support:** The best incubators provide mentoring, market access, and networking beyond just the money. The worst ones just process paperwork.

You can apply to **multiple incubators simultaneously.** There is no rule against it. However, you can only receive SISFS funding once.

### Step 3: Prepare Your Application

**Time required: 3-7 days**

Here is what you will typically need:

| Document/Information | Details |
|---------------------|---------|
| **DPIIT Recognition Certificate** | PDF of your recognition certificate with recognition number |
| **Certificate of Incorporation** | CoI from MCA showing date of incorporation |
| **Company PAN Card** | PAN of the entity (not personal PAN) |
| **Bank Account Details** | Current account in the name of the company/LLP |
| **Pitch Deck** | 10-15 slides covering problem, solution, market, team, traction, ask |
| **Business Plan / Brief** | 2-5 page document describing your business model, revenue plan, and use of funds |
| **Proof of Concept (if available)** | Demo video, screenshots, prototype link, or customer testimonials |
| **Financial Projections** | 2-3 year projections showing how you will use the seed fund |
| **Team Details** | Founder bios, LinkedIn profiles, relevant experience |
| **Use of Funds Statement** | Specific breakdown of how you will use the ₹20 lakh or ₹50 lakh |

**The pitch deck matters most.** Incubators review dozens of applications. Your deck needs to communicate the problem, your solution, why now, why you, and what the money will unlock — in under 5 minutes of reading.

### Step 4: Submit Through the Portal

**Time required: 30-60 minutes**

1. Log in to **seedfund.startupindia.gov.in** with your Startup India credentials
2. Navigate to "Apply for Seed Fund"
3. Select the incubator(s) you want to apply to
4. Fill in the online application form with your startup details
5. Upload all required documents
6. Submit the application

After submission, the incubator receives your application in their dashboard. They will review it based on their internal selection criteria and timeline.

### Step 5: Incubator Evaluation

**Time required: 2-8 weeks (varies significantly)**

Here is what happens on the incubator's side:

1. **Initial Screening:** The incubator team reviews your application for completeness and basic eligibility
2. **Detailed Evaluation:** Your application is reviewed against their selection criteria (more on this below)
3. **Presentation/Interview:** Most incubators invite shortlisted startups for a pitch presentation, either in person or via video call
4. **Committee Decision:** The incubator's investment/selection committee makes the final decision
5. **Approval Communication:** You receive formal approval from the incubator

### Step 6: Fund Disbursement

**Time required: 2-6 weeks after approval**

Once approved:

1. You sign the investment agreement or grant agreement with the incubator
2. The incubator initiates the fund transfer from their SISFS account
3. Funds are credited to your company's bank account
4. You receive a formal disbursement letter

**Total timeline from application to money in bank: 6-16 weeks** in most cases. Some incubators are faster (4 weeks end-to-end). Some are frustratingly slow (20+ weeks). Ask the incubator about their average timeline before applying.

## How Incubators Actually Select Startups (The Real Criteria)

The official selection criteria include innovation, scalability, team capability, and market potential. But here is what incubator selection committees actually look for, based on the patterns of who gets funded:

### What Gets You Selected

**1. Clear problem-solution fit with evidence**
Do not just describe a problem. Show that you have talked to customers, done surveys, or have early validation. Even 10 customer interviews or a waitlist of 200 people demonstrates more than a 50-slide deck full of TAM calculations.

**2. A working prototype or MVP**
The scheme supports proof of concept and prototype development, so you do not need a finished product. But having something tangible — a working demo, a functional app, a hardware prototype — dramatically increases your chances. The grant component is for building prototypes, but committees prefer founders who have already started building.

**3. Technical founder or strong technical team**
Incubators want to see that your team can actually build what you are proposing. A solo non-technical founder applying for a deep-tech grant will face skepticism. Either have a technical co-founder or demonstrate credible outsourcing/advisor relationships.

**4. Realistic use of funds**
When you say you will use ₹20 lakh, break it down credibly. "₹8 lakh for product development, ₹4 lakh for initial customer acquisition, ₹3 lakh for testing infrastructure, ₹3 lakh for IP filing and compliance, ₹2 lakh for team and operations." Not: "₹20 lakh for product development."

**5. Alignment with the incubator's focus**
If an incubator specializes in agritech, do not send them a fintech application. Check the incubator's portfolio and sector focus before applying.

### What Gets You Rejected

- Applications with no evidence of customer interaction or market research
- Copy-pasted business plans with inflated market size numbers
- Teams with no relevant experience or domain knowledge
- Startups that are clearly consulting companies or service firms, not product companies
- Applications where the "innovation" is a marginally different version of something that already exists, with no defensible advantage
- Use of funds that includes founder salaries as the largest line item

### The Presentation Round

If you are shortlisted, you will be invited to present. Prepare for:

- **8-12 minute pitch** followed by 10-15 minutes of Q&A
- **Questions about technical architecture** — how does your product work, what technology stack, what is novel about your approach
- **Questions about customer validation** — who have you spoken to, what did they say, do you have LOIs or pre-orders
- **Questions about team** — why are you the right team, what relevant experience do you bring
- **Questions about fund utilization** — exactly how will you spend this money and what milestones will it help you achieve
- **Questions about sustainability** — what happens after the seed fund money runs out, what is your path to revenue or next funding

## What to Do If Your State Does Not Have an Approved Incubator

If you are in a state or region with few or no SISFS-approved incubators, here are your options:

**Option 1: Apply to an incubator in another state.** There is no residency requirement. A founder in Manipur can apply to IIT Guwahati TBI in Assam or even NSRCEL in Bangalore. Many incubators offer virtual incubation programs specifically for startups that are not in their city.

**Option 2: Approach an incubator that does virtual incubation.** Several top incubators — particularly NSRCEL (IIM Bangalore), CIIE (IIM Ahmedabad), and Kerala Startup Mission — run virtual incubation cohorts. You do not need to relocate.

**Option 3: Use a nearby state's incubator.** If you are in Chhattisgarh, look at incubators in Madhya Pradesh or Maharashtra. If you are in Sikkim, look at IIT Guwahati in Assam or IIM Shillong in Meghalaya. Proximity helps for occasional in-person meetings.

**Option 4: Help an incubator in your state get approved.** If you are connected to a local university or industry body that runs an incubation program, encourage them to apply for SISFS incubator approval. The EAC periodically opens new rounds for incubator applications.

## Comparison with Other Government Funding Schemes

SISFS is not the only game in town. Here is how it compares with other major government startup funding programs:

| Parameter | **SISFS** | **BIRAC BIG** | **TIDE 2.0** | **Nidhi Prayas** | **AIM (Atal Innovation Mission)** |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Administering Body** | DPIIT | Dept of Biotechnology | MeitY | DST | NITI Aayog |
| **Sector Focus** | All sectors | Biotech, life sciences, agriculture | IT, electronics, hardware | Science & technology | All sectors (through ATLs and AICs) |
| **Stage** | Ideation to early traction | Late proof of concept | Prototype to product | Proof of concept | Ideation to prototype |
| **Grant Amount** | Up to ₹20 lakh (grant) | Up to ₹50 lakh | Up to ₹7 lakh (grant) + ₹30 lakh (equity) | Up to ₹10 lakh | Varies by program |
| **Debt/Equity** | Up to ₹50 lakh (convertible) | No | Equity component available | No | No (primarily grants) |
| **Application Through** | Approved incubators | BIRAC directly | TIDE 2.0 centres | Nidhi Prayas centres | Atal Incubation Centres |
| **DPIIT Recognition Required** | Yes | No (but helps) | No | No | No |
| **Typical Timeline** | 6-16 weeks | 3-6 months | 2-4 months | 2-3 months | 2-6 months |
| **Best For** | First-time founders, Tier 2/3 startups, any sector | Biotech/medtech startups with R&D capability | Tech product startups | Researchers turning ideas into startups | Student entrepreneurs, early idea stage |

### When to Choose SISFS Over Others

- You are a DPIIT-recognized startup less than 2 years old in any sector
- You need ₹10-20 lakh for proof of concept or ₹25-50 lakh for commercialization
- You want non-dilutive capital (the grant component)
- You want access to incubator mentoring and networks alongside the money
- You do not want to wait 6+ months (SISFS is relatively faster)

### When to Look Beyond SISFS

- You are a biotech or life sciences startup: Look at **BIRAC BIG** (up to ₹50 lakh grant)
- You are a deep-tech product startup: Look at **TIDE 2.0** for IT/electronics
- You are a researcher or scientist: Look at **Nidhi Prayas** through DST
- You want more than ₹50 lakh from government: Look at **BIRAC LEAP** (up to ₹1 crore) or state-specific funds
- You want equity investment, not grants: Look at **SIDBI Fund of Funds** or state venture funds

## The Playbook: Exact Steps to Apply This Month

If you are reading this and want to apply for SISFS, here is exactly what to do in the next 30 days:

### Week 1: Foundation

**Day 1-2: Get DPIIT recognition** (if you do not have it)
- Go to startupindia.gov.in
- Register and submit the recognition application
- Tip: Write a clear, specific paragraph about your innovation — avoid jargon and buzzwords

**Day 3-4: Research incubators**
- Go to seedfund.startupindia.gov.in and browse the incubator list
- Identify 3-5 incubators based on your state, sector, and their track record
- Visit each incubator's website. Check if they are currently accepting applications
- Email or call each incubator to confirm: (a) they are accepting SISFS applications, (b) their typical timeline, (c) any specific requirements

**Day 5-7: Prepare your application materials**

## Founder Scenario: A Jaipur-Based Agritech Startup's Journey

Here is a realistic scenario based on the typical SISFS experience, composited from multiple founder journeys:

**The Startup:** AgroSense Technologies, a Jaipur-based agritech startup building low-cost soil health monitoring devices for smallholder farmers in Rajasthan. Two co-founders: Priya (agricultural science background, 4 years in farm extension services) and Rohan (electronics engineer, previously at a Bangalore hardware startup).

**Month 1: DPIIT Recognition**

Priya and Rohan incorporated AgroSense as a Private Limited Company in November 2024. They had built a working prototype of their soil sensor using off-the-shelf components and tested it on 15 farms near Jaipur. They applied for DPIIT recognition in December 2024, writing a clear paragraph about how their device uses multi-spectral analysis to provide soil health reports in local languages via a simple mobile app. Recognition came through in 5 days.

**Month 2: Finding the Right Incubator**

They researched SISFS-approved incubators. Three were shortlisted:

1. **Startup Oasis, Jaipur** — Local, sector-agnostic, well-connected in Rajasthan's startup ecosystem
2. **MNIT Jaipur** — Technical incubator, relevant to their hardware product
3. **CIIE, IIM Ahmedabad** — Strong agritech portfolio, excellent mentoring, but in another state

They called all three. Startup Oasis was actively accepting applications. MNIT was accepting but had a slower review cycle. CIIE was running a virtual incubation batch and encouraged them to apply.

They applied to Startup Oasis (primary) and CIIE (secondary).

**Month 3: Application and Presentation**

Their pitch deck told a simple story: 120 million smallholder farmers in India have no access to affordable soil testing. Existing soil testing labs are expensive, slow, and far away. AgroSense's device costs ₹3,000 (vs ₹15,000 for existing devices), gives results in 10 minutes (vs 2 weeks), and communicates in Hindi.

Their use-of-funds breakdown for the ₹20 lakh grant:
- ₹7 lakh: Component procurement for 100-unit pilot batch
- ₹4 lakh: PCB design and manufacturing for production-ready version
- ₹3 lakh: Field testing across 5 districts in Rajasthan
- ₹2.5 lakh: Mobile app development (local language support)
- ₹2 lakh: Regulatory testing and BIS certification
- ₹1.5 lakh: Team travel and farmer training workshops

Startup Oasis shortlisted them within 2 weeks. The presentation went well because they had real data: 15 farms tested, soil reports validated against lab results (92% accuracy), and 3 farmer producer organizations expressing interest.

**Month 4: Approval and Disbursement**

Startup Oasis approved AgroSense for a ₹20 lakh grant. The agreement was signed. Funds were disbursed in two tranches: ₹12 lakh upfront and ₹8 lakh upon achieving the first milestone (completing the 100-unit pilot batch).

**What made the difference:**
- They had a working prototype, not just an idea
- They had field data from real farmers
- Their use of funds was specific and credible
- The team had complementary skills (agriculture + engineering)
- They applied to a local incubator that understood Rajasthan's agricultural landscape

**Six months later:** AgroSense completed its pilot, deployed 100 devices across Rajasthan, and used the traction data to raise a ₹75 lakh angel round. The SISFS grant gave them the runway to build the evidence that private investors needed to see.

## Practitioner Insight: When Government Funding Helps vs When It Slows You Down

Government funding is not universally good or bad. It depends on your situation. Here is an honest assessment.

### When SISFS Is Excellent

**You are pre-revenue and need runway to build.** If you are 2 founders with a prototype and no revenue, ₹20 lakh of free money gives you 6-9 months of runway to reach your first customers. This is invaluable.

**You are in a capital-efficient sector.** If ₹20 lakh is meaningful capital for your startup (agritech, edtech, SaaS, social enterprise), SISFS can take you from prototype to product-market fit. If your startup needs ₹5 crore to build a factory, ₹20 lakh does not move the needle.

**You are in a Tier 2 or Tier 3 city.** Angel investors and VCs are concentrated in Bangalore, Mumbai, Delhi, and Hyderabad. If you are building from Jaipur, Bhopal, Kochi, or Guwahati, SISFS may be the most accessible source of early funding.

**You want non-dilutive capital before raising equity.** The grant component does not take equity. This means you can use SISFS to build traction, then raise equity at a higher valuation because you have more proof points. The math works: ₹20 lakh in grant today might save you 5-10% equity dilution in your next round.

**You want the incubator ecosystem.** SISFS comes with incubation. Good incubators provide mentoring, peer networks, investor access, and credibility. For first-time founders, this support system can be as valuable as the money.

### When SISFS Slows You Down

**You are moving fast and the process is too slow.** If you have paying customers, growing revenue, and investors ready to write checks, spending 3-4 months on a government grant application may not be the best use of your time. Speed matters in competitive markets.

**Compliance and reporting become a burden.** SISFS requires utilization certificates, milestone reporting, and periodic reviews. For a 2-person startup, the compliance overhead is not trivial. If you are spending 2 days a month on SISFS paperwork instead of building product, that is a real cost.

**The incubator is not adding value.** Some incubators treat SISFS as a paper exercise. They disburse the money but provide no mentoring, no connections, no actual incubation. If you end up at one of these, you have ₹20 lakh but no ecosystem support, and you are still reporting to them quarterly.

**You have raised or can easily raise more than ₹50 lakh from private sources.** If you have access to angel networks, accelerators like Y Combinator, or friendly VCs, the time spent on SISFS may be better invested in private fundraising where amounts are larger, timelines can be faster, and the smart money comes with strategic value.

### The Balanced View

For most early-stage Indian startups — especially first-time founders outside the top 4 metro cities — SISFS is one of the best funding options available. The key is to:

1. Apply early (within 6 months of incorporation to maximize the 2-year window)
2. Choose the right incubator (one that actively supports startups, not just processes paperwork)
3. Keep building while you wait (never pause your startup to wait for government money)
4. Use the grant strategically (fund specific milestones that unlock the next stage of growth)

## What Happens After You Receive the Funds

Getting the money is not the end. Here is what comes next:

### Reporting Requirements

- **Quarterly progress reports** submitted to the incubator
- **Utilization certificates** showing how funds were spent, with supporting invoices and receipts
- **Milestone achievement reports** if funds are disbursed in tranches
- **Annual impact report** covering jobs created, revenue generated, and other impact metrics

### What the Money Can and Cannot Be Used For

**Permitted Uses:**
- Product development, prototyping, and testing
- Raw materials and component procurement
- Technology licensing and software subscriptions
- Hiring technical team members
- Market research and customer validation
- IP filing (patents, trademarks, designs)
- Regulatory compliance and certification
- Marketing and initial customer acquisition (within reasonable limits)

**Not Permitted:**
- Purchase of land or building
- Luxury office fitouts or furnishing beyond basic needs
- Foreign travel (unless specifically approved for product-related purposes)
- Repayment of existing loans
- Personal expenses of founders
- Activities unrelated to the approved project scope

### What If You Cannot Use the Funds As Planned?

If your project scope changes (which is normal for startups), communicate with the incubator proactively. Most incubators will allow reasonable modifications to the use-of-funds plan, provided:
- The funds are still being used for business development
- The change is communicated before the money is spent
- The revised plan is documented and approved

If the startup fails or shuts down, the grant component typically does not need to be returned (it is a grant, not a loan). However, the debt/convertible debenture component follows its contractual terms — it may need to be repaid or converted based on the agreement.

## Understanding the Experts Advisory Committee (EAC)

The EAC is the governing body that makes the scheme work. Understanding its role helps you navigate the process:

- **Composition:** Nominated by DPIIT, includes members from startup ecosystem, industry, academia, and government
- **Role:** Approves incubators for SISFS, allocates funds to incubators, monitors performance, addresses grievances
- **Incubator approval process:** EAC evaluates incubator applications in periodic batches and approves those that meet the criteria
- **Oversight:** EAC reviews incubator performance data, including disbursement rates and startup outcomes, to ensure the scheme is working as intended

If you face issues with an incubator (delayed disbursement, unreasonable demands, lack of response), you can escalate to the EAC through the SISFS portal. This is a last resort, but the mechanism exists.

## Maximizing Your Chances: Advanced Strategies

### Strategy 1: Apply to Multiple Incubators Simultaneously

There is no rule limiting you to one application. Submit to 2-3 incubators that fit your profile. If one is slow or rejects you, you have backups in play. This is not gaming the system — it is smart resource allocation.

### Strategy 2: Build a Relationship Before Applying

If possible, attend events hosted by your target incubator, participate in their workshops, or connect with their team on LinkedIn. Familiarity with the incubator's culture and team makes your application stronger and your presentation more natural.

### Strategy 3: Show Traction, Even If It Is Small

Ten paying customers beat a 50-page market analysis. A waitlist of 500 people beats a TAM/SAM/SOM slide. User testimonials beat investor endorsements at this stage. Show that real people want what you are building.

### Strategy 4: Have Your Financials Clean

Your startup's bank account, GST filings, and basic accounting should be in order. Incubators check this during due diligence. A startup with messy finances signals operational risk.

### Strategy 5: Time Your Application Right

Apply when you have something to show (a prototype, early traction, customer letters of intent) but before you have been incorporated for 2 years. The sweet spot is 3-12 months after incorporation.

### Strategy 6: Know Your Valuation

Understanding your startup's valuation — even at the early stage — helps you articulate the value of your equity and the leverage that non-dilutive funding provides. If you know your startup is worth ₹1 crore, then a ₹20 lakh grant that takes zero equity is clearly better than a ₹20 lakh angel investment at ₹1 crore valuation that takes 2% of your company.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Can I apply for SISFS if my startup is already 1.5 years old?

Yes. The eligibility criterion is that your startup must be incorporated not more than 2 years ago at the time of application. If you are at 1.5 years, you still have a 6-month window. However, apply quickly because the incubator evaluation process can take 2-3 months, and some incubators check the incorporation date at the time of final approval, not just at application submission.

### 2. I received ₹5 lakh from a state government scheme. Am I still eligible?

Yes. The threshold is ₹10 lakh of monetary support from Central or State Government schemes combined. Having received ₹5 lakh, you are still under the limit. However, be transparent in your application. Declare the previous funding. Incubators will verify this during due diligence, and undisclosed prior funding is grounds for rejection.

### 3. How long does it take from application to receiving money?

The realistic range is 6 to 16 weeks. Some incubators with streamlined processes can go from application to disbursement in 6-8 weeks. Others take 12-16 weeks. The biggest variable is the incubator's review cycle — some review monthly, others quarterly. Ask the incubator about their timeline before applying.

### 4. Can I apply to an incubator in a different state?

Yes. There is no state-residency requirement. You can apply to any SISFS-approved incubator regardless of where your startup is registered or operates. However, some incubators give preference to local startups, especially state-backed incubators. National-level incubators (IIT/IIM-affiliated) are generally state-agnostic in their selection.

### 5. What if my application is rejected? Can I reapply?

Yes. There is no limit on reapplications. If one incubator rejects you, apply to another. If the same incubator rejects you, you can reapply after addressing the feedback they provide. Many successful SISFS recipients were rejected on their first application and succeeded on their second or third attempt with a stronger proposal.

### 6. Is the ₹20 lakh grant truly non-dilutive? Does the government take any equity?

The grant component (up to ₹20 lakh) is entirely non-dilutive. The government does not take equity for the grant. You do not repay it. It is a pure grant. However, the debt/convertible debenture component (up to ₹50 lakh) may convert to equity based on the terms of the agreement with the incubator. Read the convertible debenture terms carefully before signing.

### 7. What happens if my startup pivots or fails after receiving SISFS funds?

For the grant component: If your startup pivots, inform the incubator and get their approval for the revised use of funds. If your startup fails, you generally do not need to return the grant, provided you used the funds for the stated purpose and can show utilization certificates. For the debt component: the terms of your specific agreement apply. Failure may trigger repayment clauses depending on the agreement structure.

### 8. Can I use SISFS funds alongside private investment (angel/VC)?

Yes. There is no restriction on raising private capital alongside SISFS. In fact, this is encouraged. Many startups use SISFS for proof of concept and prototype, then raise angel or VC funding for growth. The two are complementary. Just ensure you maintain clean documentation of how SISFS funds are used separately from private investment.
