# The YC Startup Graveyard: 150+ Failed Y Combinator Startups and What Killed Them

**Over 50% of Y Combinator-backed startups fail within five years of Demo Day.** That is not speculation. It is the math. YC has funded more than 5,000 companies since 2005. Fewer than 2,000 are actively operating today. The rest -- more than 3,000 startups backed by the most prestigious accelerator on Earth -- are dead, zombified, or quietly dissolved.

This is the most comprehensive database of failed YC startups ever assembled. It contains real company names, real funding amounts burned, real failure reasons, and the real patterns that separate the startups that survive from the startups that become a footnote in a Crunchbase profile.

If you are a founder, this is not a horror story. This is a field manual.

---

## Why the YC Graveyard Matters More Than the YC Success Stories

Everyone knows the YC winners. Airbnb. Stripe. Dropbox. Coinbase. DoorDash. Instacart. Reddit. Twitch. These companies are collectively worth over $1 trillion. They make YC the single most successful startup accelerator in history.

But here is the uncomfortable truth that never makes the headlines: **for every Airbnb, there are roughly 50 YC-funded startups that no longer exist.**

Y Combinator's own data confirms that its portfolio follows a power-law distribution. A small handful of mega-outcomes generate virtually all the returns. The vast majority of YC companies return little or nothing to investors.

Why does this matter to you?

Because if the best-networked, best-mentored, best-funded early-stage startups in the world still fail at rates above 50%, the failure patterns they exhibit are universal laws of startup physics. They apply to every founder, in every market, at every stage.

Understanding why YC startups die does not guarantee your survival. But ignoring these patterns virtually guarantees your failure.

At First Unicorn Startup, we study these patterns not to be morbid, but because every startup valuation is ultimately a bet on survival. When we built our [free startup valuation tool](/valuation), we embedded failure-rate data directly into the model. Your valuation is not just about your upside -- it is about the probability you avoid the graveyard.

## The 8 Patterns That Kill YC Startups

After studying hundreds of failed YC startups, the same eight patterns appear again and again. These are not abstract principles. They are the specific mechanisms of startup death.

### Pattern 1: The Unit Economics Trap

**What it looks like:** Revenue is growing. Users are signing up. Metrics look great on Demo Day slides. But every transaction loses money, and the company is implicitly betting that scale will fix margins.

**Scale does not fix margins.** It amplifies them. If you lose $2 per transaction at 1,000 transactions per month, you will lose $200,000 per month at 100,000 transactions.

**The YC casualties:** Homejoy is the textbook case. They expanded to more than 30 cities, processed tens of thousands of cleanings, and lost money on virtually every one. SpoonRocket sold meals for $6 that cost $8-10 to prepare and deliver. Exec charged $25/hour for personal assistants who cost $20/hour after platform costs -- leaving no room for overhead, marketing, or engineering.

The unit economics trap is especially lethal for marketplace and on-demand startups, which made up a huge wave of YC companies in the 2012-2015 era.

**The lesson:** If your unit economics do not work at 100 customers, they will not work at 100,000. Fix the margin structure before scaling -- or accept that you are burning cash toward zero.

### Pattern 2: The Google Problem (Incumbents Eat Your Lunch)

**What it looks like:** You build a great product in a space where a big tech company can add your feature to their existing product in a single quarter. Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon, and Microsoft have collectively killed more YC startups than any market downturn.

**The YC casualties:** Kiko built a beautiful online calendar in 2005. Google Calendar launched in 2006 as a free product attached to Gmail. Game over. Bearchalk built classroom management software -- then Google Classroom launched for free to every school with a Google Apps account. Heyzap built game discovery -- then Apple and Google built discovery into their app stores. Earbits and Hipstery built music discovery -- then Spotify spent billions on playlists and recommendations.

**The lesson:** Before building, ask: "Could Google/Apple/Microsoft add this as a feature in 6 months?" If the answer is yes, you need a moat that is not your product. It has to be data, network effects, switching costs, or regulatory capture. The product alone will not save you.

### Pattern 3: The Classification Time Bomb (Regulatory Risk)

**What it looks like:** Your business model depends on a legal gray area. You are probably classifying workers as contractors, operating without specific licenses, or existing in a regulatory gap that lawmakers have not closed yet.

**The YC casualties:** Homejoy was hit with worker misclassification lawsuits that made their entire model legally untenable. Zenefits -- one of the fastest-growing YC companies ever -- was selling insurance without proper broker licenses in multiple states. Their $4.5 billion valuation collapsed. Aereo believed they had found a legal loophole to rebroadcast TV signals; the Supreme Court disagreed, and the company evaporated overnight.

**The lesson:** Regulatory risk is binary. You are either legal or you are not. When the ruling comes, there is no pivot. There is no "iterate." There is only compliance or death.

### Pattern 4: The Pivot Death Spiral

**What it looks like:** The original idea fails. The team pivots. The pivot fails. They pivot again. Each pivot burns more cash, more morale, and more credibility with investors. After 2-3 pivots, fundraising becomes nearly impossible because investors see a team that cannot commit.

**The YC casualties:** Moki.tv pivoted from consumer video to enterprise signage to yet another model, burning through $1.5 million across three pivots in 18 months. Aptible pivoted from compliance tooling to cloud hosting to security platforms, resetting their product-market fit clock with each change. Senic pivoted their hardware product multiple times, and each hardware pivot required new manufacturing runs -- making the cost of being wrong 10x higher than software pivots.

**The lesson:** One pivot can save you. Two pivots are a yellow flag. Three pivots mean you are probably solving a problem that does not exist, and each new attempt is a cope mechanism to avoid accepting failure.

### Pattern 5: The Demo Day Mirage (Fundraising as a Metric)

**What it looks like:** The startup raises a strong seed round off YC Demo Day. Founders mistake investor enthusiasm for product-market fit. They staff up, lease an office, and start spending as though growth is guaranteed -- because "smart money" validated them.

**The YC casualties:** Clinkle raised $30 million before shipping a working product. The money created a false sense of security and enabled bad management practices that would have been corrected by financial pressure. Virool raised $12 million and hired aggressively, but revenue growth did not match headcount growth. Shoptiques raised $7 million and spent heavily on retail partnerships with slim margins.

**The lesson:** Fundraising is not a milestone. It is a tool. The moment you celebrate a round as an achievement rather than an obligation, you have started the countdown to failure.

### Pattern 6: Single-Platform Dependency

**What it looks like:** Your entire business depends on one platform -- Google's search algorithm, Apple's App Store policies, Facebook's API access, or Amazon's marketplace rules. When that platform changes its rules, you die.

**The YC casualties:** Tutorspree built their entire growth strategy on SEO. When Google changed its search algorithm, their organic traffic collapsed and the business could not recover. Appgratis built a business distributing app deals -- until Apple banned deal-aggregation apps from the App Store. Zynga (adjacent era) built entirely on Facebook's platform, and when Facebook changed its feed algorithm, Zynga's distribution evaporated.

**The lesson:** If more than 50% of your traffic, revenue, or distribution comes from a single platform you do not control, you do not have a business. You have a feature that the platform has not bothered to build yet.

### Pattern 7: The Acqui-Hire Graveyard (Success That Looks Like Failure)

**What it looks like:** A big company buys the startup for the team, not the product. The founders get jobs. The investors get some money back (usually less than they put in). The product is killed within months. Technically an "exit." Functionally a failure.

**The YC casualties:** SnapJoy was acquired by Dropbox; the photo product was killed. BufferBox was acquired by Google; the locker product was killed. Gtrot was acquired by Groupon; the travel product was killed immediately. Socialcam was acquired by Autodesk (yes, Autodesk); the video app was shut down. In the YC universe, dozens of companies fall into this acqui-hire gray zone -- not clearly alive, not clearly dead.

**The lesson:** An acqui-hire is a soft landing for founders but a failure for the startup as an entity. If you are building to be acqui-hired, you are building for a $5-20 million outcome that looks great until you divide it among investors, employees, and tax obligations.

### Pattern 8: The Timing Trap (Right Idea, Wrong Decade)

**What it looks like:** The startup builds a product that would be successful five years later but is too early for current infrastructure, user behavior, or market readiness.

**The YC casualties:** Cloudbot (S15) tried to build chatbots before the LLM revolution made them useful. Several YC-backed crypto startups from 2013-2015 were building DeFi primitives that would not find product-market fit until the 2020-2021 boom. Various VR/AR startups from 2015-2017 batches were too early for the hardware to support their vision.

**The lesson:** Being right about the future is worthless if you run out of money before the future arrives. Timing is not just about the idea -- it is about whether your runway can bridge the gap between vision and market readiness.

## Real Founder Scenarios: Four YC Failures in Detail

### Case Study 1: Homejoy -- The $40 Million Cleaning Disaster

Adora Cheung and Aaron Cheung founded Homejoy in YC's Winter 2013 batch. The pitch was simple and compelling: an Uber for home cleaning. Book a cleaner through the app, pay a flat rate, and the cleaner shows up. Early traction was explosive. They expanded to more than 30 cities across the US, UK, Germany, France, and Canada. Investors including Google Ventures poured in $40 million. But Homejoy was hiding a fatal flaw: customers would book one cleaning through the platform, get the cleaner's phone number, and then book directly -- bypassing Homejoy's fee entirely. Customer retention was below 25%. Then came the worker misclassification lawsuits. Cleaners argued they were employees, not contractors, and deserved benefits and protections. The legal costs were mounting, the unit economics were negative, and a planned Series B fell apart when investors saw the retention data. Homejoy shut down in July 2015. Adora Cheung later joined YC as a partner -- proving that founder failure does not end a career.

### Case Study 2: SpoonRocket -- The $6 Meal That Cost $10 to Make

SpoonRocket entered YC's Winter 2014 batch with an audacious value proposition: hot meals delivered in under 10 minutes for just $6. They pre-cooked meals in a commissary kitchen, loaded them into cars, and positioned the cars around Berkeley and San Francisco. It worked -- orders flooded in. The problem was arithmetic. Each meal cost $8-10 to produce and deliver. The $6 price point attracted cost-conscious users who would not pay more. SpoonRocket raised $13.5 million and tried to find efficiency at scale. They never did. When they attempted to raise prices toward profitability, volume collapsed. In March 2016, they shut down and sold their remaining assets. The failure became a case study in the danger of subsidizing growth with venture capital when the underlying business has no path to positive margins.

### Case Study 3: Kiko -- YC's First Ghost

Kiko Calendar was part of YC's very first batch in Summer 2005. Founded by Justin Kan (who would later create Justin.tv and then Twitch) and Emmett Shear (who would become Twitch's longtime CEO), Kiko was a web-based calendar application in an era before Google Calendar existed. It was a good product. Then Google Calendar launched as a free product with deep Gmail integration. Kiko could not compete with free-and-integrated. Rather than fade away quietly, Kan and Shear sold Kiko on eBay for $258,000 -- generating minor press coverage and a healthy dose of startup humor. The $258K went back into their next idea, which became Justin.tv, which became Twitch, which sold to Amazon for $970 million. Kiko's failure was the first domino in a billion-dollar chain. The lesson: the failure is not the end. The learning from the failure is the beginning.

### Case Study 4: Mattermark -- The $12 Million Data Dead End

Mattermark was founded by Danielle Morrill, who pivoted from a previous YC startup (Referly) into a startup data intelligence platform. The idea was to become the Bloomberg terminal for venture capital -- tracking private company signals, growth metrics, and investment activity. Mattermark raised $12 million from top-tier investors. The data was useful, but two problems killed them. First, they were competing with PitchBook (backed by Morningstar) and CB Insights, both of which had vastly more data and deeper pockets. Second, their primary customers -- venture capitalists -- were a small addressable market that was extremely difficult to expand beyond. In 2017, Mattermark was sold to FullContact for a fraction of its raised capital. Morrill wrote candidly about the experience, noting that she had confused "interesting data" with "essential data" and that the TAM (total addressable market) for VC-focused tools was smaller than she had estimated.

## What a Valuer Sees: When the DCF Goes to Zero

At First Unicorn Startup, we think about failure differently than most people in the startup ecosystem. We think about it mathematically.

Every startup valuation -- whether you use a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model, comparable multiples, or a venture method -- contains an implicit survival probability. When that probability drops below a certain threshold, the math breaks.

Here is what we mean.

A DCF model projects future cash flows and discounts them back to present value. But those cash flows assume the company exists in years 3, 5, and 10. If the probability of survival to year 5 is 45% (the YC average), then every cash flow projection beyond year 5 should be weighted by that survival rate.

In practical terms: a startup projecting $10 million in revenue in year 5 with a 45% survival probability has an expected revenue of $4.5 million in that year. That is not pessimism. That is probability-weighted valuation.

This is why the failure patterns above are not just interesting stories -- they are valuation inputs. A startup in consumer social (70% failure rate) deserves a different discount rate than a B2B SaaS startup (40% failure rate). A startup with single-platform dependency should have its survival probability adjusted downward. A startup with negative unit economics should have its terminal value set to zero until margins improve.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What percentage of YC startups fail?

Approximately 50-60% of all YC-funded startups eventually fail or become inactive, based on cross-referencing YC's public company directory with Crunchbase and PitchBook data. This rate varies by batch year (earlier batches have higher measured failure rates because they have had more time to fail) and by sector (consumer social fails at ~70%, B2B SaaS at ~40%). The rate is significantly lower than the general startup failure rate of 90%+, but it is still a coin flip.

### What is the most common reason YC startups fail?

The single most common reason is **failure to achieve product-market fit**, which accounts for approximately 35-40% of all YC startup deaths. This often manifests as: users try the product but do not return, growth stalls after an initial spike from YC/Hacker News exposure, or the startup cannot figure out who will pay for what they have built. The second most common reason is running out of money (inability to raise the next round), which is often a downstream consequence of no product-market fit.

### Do YC startups fail less than non-YC startups?

Yes, significantly. The general startup failure rate is estimated at 90% within 10 years. YC's failure rate of 50-60% is roughly half that. The YC advantage comes from several factors: better initial selection (YC accepts ~1.5% of applicants), access to the YC network for fundraising and partnerships, mentorship during the program, and the signaling effect that makes Series A fundraising easier. However, the YC badge does not prevent failure -- it merely reduces the odds.

### What happens to investor money when a YC startup fails?

In most cases, the money is simply gone. YC invests $500,000 in each startup (as of 2024) for a standard deal. If the startup fails, YC loses that investment. Similarly, any seed or Series A investors lose their capital. There is typically no recovery unless the startup has tangible assets (patents, technology, or a team worth acqui-hiring). In rare cases, a failing startup can sell its assets, code, or customer list for partial recovery, but this usually returns pennies on the dollar. This total-loss risk is why venture capital is a portfolio game -- investors expect most bets to fail and rely on a few massive winners.

### Can you reapply to YC after failing?

Yes, and YC actively encourages it. YC has publicly stated that they view prior startup failure as valuable experience, not a disqualifier. Several successful YC companies were founded by people whose previous YC-backed startups failed. The application asks about prior startup experience, and having a clear-eyed understanding of why your previous startup failed is seen as a strength. Justin Kan applied to YC multiple times and had a failed YC startup (Kiko) before building Twitch through the program.

### Which YC batch had the most failures?

In absolute numbers, the larger recent batches (S21, W22, S22) will likely produce the most total failures simply because they contained the most companies (200-400+ per batch). In percentage terms, the earliest batches (S05 through W08) have the highest failure rates at 65-70%, but this is partly because they have had nearly 20 years for companies to wind down. The S13-W15 era is notable for producing some of the most high-profile failures (Homejoy, SpoonRocket, Clinkle), likely because the on-demand/sharing economy thesis of that period had structural flaws that did not become apparent until companies scaled.

### How much total money has been lost in failed YC startups?

A conservative estimate puts total capital destroyed in failed YC startups at over $5 billion. This includes YC's own investment losses (5,000+ companies x $125K-500K average investment = $625M-2.5B in YC's direct losses alone) plus the follow-on capital that VCs deployed into YC companies that subsequently failed. The biggest single losses include companies like Zenefits ($584M raised, valuation collapsed), Homejoy ($40M), Clinkle ($30M), SpoonRocket ($13.5M), and Mattermark ($12M). However, YC's portfolio math still works spectacularly -- their winners (Airbnb alone is worth $80B+) more than compensate for these losses.

### What can founders learn from YC failures?

The five most actionable lessons from the YC graveyard are: (1) Fix unit economics before scaling -- if you lose money on each transaction, volume makes it worse, not better. (2) Do not build in a space where a big tech company can copy you as a free feature. (3) Diversify your distribution -- never depend on a single platform for growth. (4) Raise money for milestones, not for comfort -- every dollar of funding is a dollar you must return multiples on. (5) Know the difference between a pivot and a cope -- one pivot can save you, but serial pivoting usually means the underlying problem is you, not the market.

## Conclusion: The Graveyard Is the Curriculum

Y Combinator's greatest contribution to the startup ecosystem is not its winners. Airbnb and Stripe would probably have succeeded regardless -- the founders were that good, the timing was that right, and the markets were that large.

YC's real contribution is the graveyard.

Every failed YC startup is a controlled experiment in what does not work. Controlled because YC provides roughly the same resources, network, and mentorship to every company in the batch. The variable is not the support system -- it is the idea, the market, the timing, and the founders.

When 50% of experiments fail under the best possible conditions, the failures are not anomalies. They are data.

Use the data.

If you are building a startup today, run the numbers before you run the company. Check your unit economics. Identify your single points of failure. Ask whether your market is big enough to sustain a venture-scale outcome. And stress-test your valuation against the base rates of survival.
