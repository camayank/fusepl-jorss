> **Expert Summary:** The co-founder equity split is one of the most consequential decisions in a startup's history — and the one made with the least data. Most founding teams either split equity emotionally (50/50 "to keep things fair") or defer the conversation until a crisis forces it. Neither approach serves the company or the founders well. A defensible equity split is grounded in a structured assessment of each founder's contribution, aligned with vesting schedules that protect the company if a co-founder leaves early.

## Why the Equity Split Decision Cannot Be Deferred

Many founding teams avoid the equity conversation because it is uncomfortable. This is a mistake with compounding consequences:

- **Investors will ask about it in the first meeting.** A team that has not addressed equity split signals immaturity and potential conflict.
- **The longer you wait, the harder it gets.** Early contributions are easier to assess before money and traction are involved.
- **Informal agreements are not binding.** WhatsApp conversations and verbal agreements have no legal standing. The equity split must be documented in a Founders' Agreement and reflected in share allotments registered with the MCA.

## The Five Factors That Should Drive the Equity Split

### Factor 1: Idea Origination

Who identified the problem and conceived the initial solution? This is the starting point of the startup's existence.

**Weight in equity decision:** Low to medium (10–20%). Ideas are cheap; execution is what creates value. Overweighting idea origination rewards the past at the expense of the future.

### Factor 2: Full-Time Commitment and Opportunity Cost

Who is going all-in, and what are they giving up to do so? A founder leaving a ₹40 LPA job has a different risk profile than one who has just graduated with no prior income.

**Weight in equity decision:** Medium to high (20–30%). Full-time commitment is the baseline expectation — part-time founders should receive significantly lower equity or none at all.

### Factor 3: Domain Expertise and Skill Value

What unique skills or knowledge does each founder bring that would be expensive to hire externally?

- **Technical co-founder** building the product: High skill value
- **Sales/BD co-founder** with an existing customer network: High skill value
- **Operations co-founder** in a regulated industry: Moderate skill value
- **Generalist co-founder** with no specific differentiation: Lower skill value

**Weight in equity decision:** Medium to high (20–30%).

### Factor 4: Capital Contribution

Has any co-founder invested personal capital in the company? This is increasingly less common at the ideation stage but does occur.

**Weight in equity decision:** Low (5–15%). Cash contributions should ideally be separated from equity — paid back as a loan once funding comes in, rather than permanently embedded in the equity split.

### Factor 5: Future Role and Responsibilities

What is each founder expected to do going forward? A CTO building the product and a CEO selling and fundraising may have equal importance regardless of who had the original idea.

**Weight in equity decision:** High (30–40%). Future contribution is where the majority of startup value is created.

## Common Equity Split Models

### The 50/50 Split

**When it works:** Two founders with genuinely equal contributions, complementary skills, and equal full-time commitment.

**When it fails:** When one founder is more committed, more skilled, or has contributed more capital. The 50/50 split creates a structural deadlock in voting and governance that can paralyse decision-making as the company scales.

**Investor view:** 50/50 splits are acceptable but raise the governance question — who has final decision authority? Some investors require founders to agree on a tiebreaker mechanism before closing.

### The 60/40 or 70/30 Split

**Most common structure for two-founder teams.** One founder (typically the CEO) holds a slight or meaningful majority.

**Advantages:**
- Clear decision authority in the majority founder
- Still motivating for the minority founder
- Investors generally prefer a clean decision-making structure

### The 3-Founder Split

Three founders create more complexity. A common structure:
- Lead founder (CEO): 40–45%
- Technical co-founder (CTO): 30–35%
- Business co-founder (COO/CMO): 20–25%

Avoid equal three-way splits (33/33/33) for the same governance reasons as 50/50 — it creates a 2-vs-1 dynamic that is structurally problematic.

## Vesting: The Mechanism That Makes the Split Work

**Vesting** means equity is earned over time based on continued contribution — not granted in full on day one. It protects the company and the remaining founders if a co-founder leaves early.

### Standard Vesting Schedule in India

**4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff:**
- 0–12 months: 0% vested (the cliff)
- 12 months: 25% vests immediately (cliff milestone)
- Months 13–48: Remaining 75% vests monthly (2.08% per month)

**Why the cliff matters:** If a co-founder leaves in the first year — before any equity vests — the company can reabsorb those shares without diluting the remaining team. Without a cliff, a founder who leaves after 3 months keeps proportional equity indefinitely.

### Single Trigger vs Double Trigger Acceleration

**Single trigger:** Vesting accelerates 100% upon acquisition of the company.

**Double trigger:** Vesting accelerates only if (1) there is an acquisition AND (2) the founder is terminated without cause or resigns for good reason.

**Recommended:** Double trigger for Indian startup founders. Investors generally insist on double trigger to prevent founders from cashing out immediately post-acquisition without contributing to the transition.

## The Founders' Agreement: Documenting the Split

A **Founders' Agreement** is the binding legal document that captures:

- Equity split percentages and vesting schedules
- Roles and responsibilities of each co-founder
- Decision-making authority (who has final say in what domains)
- IP assignment (all work done for the company is owned by the company)
- Buyback provisions if a founder exits (right of first refusal for remaining founders)
- Non-compete and non-solicitation clauses

**This must be signed before any external funding.** Investors will ask for it in due diligence, and an unsigned or informal founders' agreement is a red flag.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the most common co-founder equity split in Indian startups?

There is no single most common split, but 50/50 and 60/40 splits are the most prevalent for two-founder teams in India. 50/50 is chosen for its perceived fairness; 60/40 reflects a lead founder dynamic. The right split is the one that accurately reflects each founder's contribution and future role — not the one that feels most comfortable to discuss.

### Should I give equity to an advisor or early employee?

Advisors typically receive 0.1–1% equity (vesting over 2 years with no cliff) in exchange for meaningful, ongoing strategic contribution. Early employees should receive ESOPs under a formal scheme, not direct equity. Giving direct equity to early employees creates cap table complexity and governance issues that are difficult to unwind later.

### What happens to a co-founder's equity if they leave the startup?

Under a properly structured Founders' Agreement, a departing co-founder keeps only the equity that has vested to date. Unvested equity is repurchased by the company at cost (Face Value) or forfeited. The terms should be explicitly documented — what constitutes a voluntary exit, what constitutes a termination for cause, and how the repurchase price is determined in each scenario.

### Can I change the equity split after the startup is incorporated?

Yes, but it is legally complex and tax-sensitive. Equity redistribution after incorporation involves share transfers (subject to stamp duty and capital gains tax) or share buybacks. The cleanest approach is to agree on the equity split before incorporation and document it correctly from the start.

### Do investors care about the co-founder equity split?

Yes. Investors assess the equity split for two things: whether it is motivating enough for each founder to stay committed long term, and whether it creates governance clarity. A founder with 10% equity may not be sufficiently motivated; a 50/50 split without a clear tiebreaker mechanism creates governance risk. Both scenarios raise concerns during due diligence.
