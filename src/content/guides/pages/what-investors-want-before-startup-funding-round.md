> **Expert Summary:** Indian investors evaluate startups on three dimensions before committing capital: the founding team's ability to execute, the business's evidence of product-market fit, and the legal and financial hygiene that determines deal risk. Founders who understand all three dimensions — not just the pitch narrative — dramatically reduce the time from first meeting to term sheet. Most deals that die in due diligence fail on the third dimension, which is entirely within the founder's control to fix in advance.

## The Investor Decision Framework: What They Are Really Evaluating

When an Indian investor sits across from you, they are running a simultaneous evaluation across three filters:

**Filter 1: Can this team win?**
**Filter 2: Is this business working?**
**Filter 3: Is this deal clean enough to close?**

Most founders prepare only for Filter 1. That is not enough.

---

## Filter 1: Team Quality — The Non-Negotiable Starting Point

### What "Strong Team" Actually Means to Indian Investors

Strong does not mean pedigreed. It means **credibly positioned to execute on this specific problem in this specific market**.

Investors look for:

**Domain expertise**: Has the founder spent years in this industry? Do they understand the problem from the inside? This is worth more than an IIT degree in an unrelated field.

**Prior execution track record**: Have they built something before — even if it failed? A founder who has previously hired, sold, and shipped product is a safer bet than one who has not.

**Technical depth in technical problems**: For deep-tech, fintech, or healthtech startups, at least one founder must have genuine technical credibility. A non-technical founding team building a product that requires technical differentiation is a red flag.

**The ability to sell**: Every startup is eventually a sales problem. Does the founder have evidence of selling — customers signed, partnerships closed, employees recruited? A founder who cannot sell is an investor's nightmare.

## Filter 2: Traction — Evidence That the Business Is Working

### The Traction Hierarchy

Not all traction is equal. Investors weight traction signals in this order:

1. **Revenue** — Paying customers are the strongest signal. Even ₹1 lakh MRR from 5 customers is more valuable than 50,000 free users.
2. **Retention** — Do customers stay? High retention proves the product has real value, independent of acquisition spend.
3. **Engagement** — For consumer apps, daily/monthly active user ratios, session length, and return visit rates.
4. **Pipeline** — Qualified prospects in a sales process, with verifiable timelines and decision-maker access.
5. **Intent signals** — Letters of Intent, signed pilots, waitlist with ICP-matched users.

**Zero traction acceptable at:** Pre-seed only, and only with exceptional team signals or a genuinely novel insight.

### The Metrics That Matter by Stage

**For seed-stage evaluation, investors typically want to see:**
- MRR of ₹2–20 lakhs with consistent month-over-month growth
- Gross retention above 80%
- CAC payback under 18 months
- At least one repeatable acquisition channel

**For pre-Series A evaluation:**
- ARR of ₹5–20 Cr
- Net Revenue Retention above 100%
- LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or higher
- Evidence of second-order growth (referrals, virality, or network effects)

## Filter 3: Deal Hygiene — What Investors Check in Due Diligence

This is the filter that kills the most deals in India. Founders who have built real traction but have messy legal and financial structures watch deals collapse after term sheets are signed.

### Legal Due Diligence Checklist

**Incorporation and governance:**
- Certificate of Incorporation, MOA, AOA
- All Board resolutions — for share allotments, key contracts, appointment of directors
- Register of Members, Directors, and Charges
- All MCA annual filings up to date (AOC-4, MGT-7, MGT-14 where applicable)

**Share structure:**
- Cap table with all shareholders, share classes, and conversion terms
- PAS-3 filings for all historical allotments
- Shareholder agreements and investor rights agreements from prior rounds
- Any convertible instruments (SAFEs, CCNs, convertible notes) documented in full

**Intellectual Property:**
- Trademark registrations (or applications filed)
- Domain ownership in company name, not personal name
- Software copyright documentation
- Employment and contractor agreements with IP assignment clauses

**Regulatory compliance:**
- DPIIT recognition certificate
- GST registration and return filings (no defaults)
- TDS filings and payments current
- If raising from non-residents: FEMA compliance framework ready (FC-GPR)

### Financial Due Diligence Checklist

- Audited financial statements (or professionally prepared if not yet required to audit)
- Bank statements for the last 12–24 months
- All outstanding invoices and accounts receivable
- Vendor contracts and any long-term liabilities
- Loan agreements if any debt is outstanding
- GST and income tax returns for all prior years

## The Data Room: How to Organise What Investors Need

A **data room** is a secure, organised folder (Google Drive, Notion, or Docsend) containing all due diligence documents. Investors will request access to a data room after the first or second meeting. Not having one ready adds 2–4 weeks to your close timeline.

**Recommended data room structure:**

```
/Data Room
  /01 - Corporate Documents
    Certificate of Incorporation
    MOA & AOA
    Board Resolutions
  /02 - Cap Table & Shares
    Current Cap Table
    All Historical Allotments (PAS-3)
    Shareholder Agreements
  /03 - Financials
    Audited Statements (if available)
    MIS Reports (last 12 months)
    Financial Model
    Bank Statements (last 12 months)
  /04 - Legal & Compliance
    DPIIT Recognition
    GST Registration & Returns
    TDS Challans
    IP Registrations
  /05 - Team
    Founder CVs
    Key Employee Contracts
    ESOP Scheme (if applicable)
  /06 - Product & Business
    Product Demo or Screenshots
    Customer Contracts / LOIs
    Key Vendor Agreements
```

## What Investors Want to Hear in the Pitch

Beyond documents, investors are evaluating narrative clarity:

**A crisp problem statement**: Can you explain the problem in 2 sentences that make the listener immediately feel the pain?

**A differentiated solution**: Not just "we solve X" but "we solve X in a way that is 10x better than current alternatives because of Y."

**A credible go-to-market**: How do you get your first 100 customers? First 1,000? What does the unit economics look like at each step?

**A clear use of funds**: What specific milestones will this capital unlock, and what will those milestones prove about the business?

**A fundable exit story**: Who acquires companies like yours? Who has done so in the last 5 years? What would make this a ₹500 Cr+ outcome?

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What do early-stage investors in India care about most?

At the pre-seed and seed stage, Indian investors care most about founding team quality and early traction signals. The team must have credible founder-market fit, and the traction must show evidence of a repeatable value proposition — even if at small scale. Legal and financial hygiene become the primary concern during due diligence after initial interest is established.

### How long does investor due diligence typically take in India?

Indian institutional investors typically complete due diligence in 4–8 weeks after a term sheet is signed. Angel investors and early-stage funds may move faster (2–4 weeks). Complex deals involving FEMA compliance, prior investor rights, or legal remediation can extend to 12+ weeks. Having a clean data room reduces this timeline significantly.

### What is the biggest mistake founders make when pitching Indian investors?

The biggest mistake is presenting a polished narrative without the underlying data to support it. Indian investors, particularly institutional ones, will move quickly from the pitch to the model and the metrics. Founders who cannot go deep on their unit economics, churn data, or competitive positioning in real time lose credibility regardless of how good the deck looks.

### Do investors check DPIIT recognition during due diligence?

Yes. DPIIT recognition status is checked early in the due diligence process. Its absence triggers an immediate Angel Tax risk assessment, which adds legal complexity and cost to the transaction. Founders without DPIIT recognition will face additional questions about regulatory awareness and compliance posture.

### How should I respond if an investor asks for information I do not have?

Be honest immediately. "I do not have that data available right now, but I will send it to you within 48 hours" is a credible and professional response. Attempting to deflect, guess, or fabricate an answer destroys trust in a way that is almost impossible to recover from in a due diligence process.
