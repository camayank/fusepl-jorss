# Anti-Dilution Clauses: Full Ratchet vs Weighted Average (With Math)

Anti-dilution protection is buried in every investor term sheet as a single line. Most founders accept it without understanding the numbers.

When times are good, it's irrelevant. When you have a down round — and 30–40% of Indian startups that raised in 2021 are facing down rounds in 2024–2026 — that single line determines whether your founding team retains meaningful ownership.

This article shows you the exact math of each anti-dilution type in a realistic scenario.

---

## Why Anti-Dilution Exists

When an investor buys shares at ₹400 per share, and you later issue shares at ₹250 per share, the investor's original shares are "worth less" — not in absolute terms, but in terms of their ownership percentage of the company.

Anti-dilution protection says: "If you raise at a lower price, I get more shares to compensate."

The question is: how many more shares?

The answer depends on which type of anti-dilution protection was agreed.

---

## Type 1: Full Ratchet (The Aggressive Version)

**Mechanism:** The investor's conversion price is reset to match the new (lower) round price exactly. Every share they hold converts as if they had paid the new price.

**Formula:**
```
New Conversion Price = New Round Price (that's it)
New Share Count = Original Investment / New Round Price
```

**Example:**
- Investor paid: ₹8 crore at ₹400/share = 2,00,000 shares
- Down round price: ₹250/share
- New conversion price: ₹250 (full ratchet)
- New share count: ₹8Cr / ₹250 = **3,20,000 shares**
- Extra shares: **1,20,000 shares** at no additional cost

These 1,20,000 extra shares come from the founders' allocation (via the creation of new shares or transfer).

**The impact:** If total shares are 10,00,000 pre-down round and the investor goes from 2,00,000 to 3,20,000 shares, founders who together owned 60% now own significantly less.

**Full ratchet creates a runaway dilution problem:** Even a tiny down round (e.g., ₹395 instead of ₹400) triggers full repricing to the new price. The size of the down round is irrelevant — even a 1% price reduction triggers full adjustment.

**When you see full ratchet:** In distressed situations (company has no leverage), highly investor-friendly funds, and pre-2000 term sheets. In modern Indian seed/Series A rounds, full ratchet is rare and should be pushed back firmly.

---

## Type 2: Narrow-Based Weighted Average

**Mechanism:** The adjustment is weighted by actual shares issued in the down round, using only issued and outstanding shares in the denominator.

**Formula:**
```
New Conversion Price = Old Conversion Price ×
  (Outstanding Shares + Shares Issuable at Old Price) /
  (Outstanding Shares + New Shares Issued)

Where "Outstanding Shares" = only issued and outstanding equity shares
(does NOT include ESOP pool, warrants, convertibles)
```

**Example:**
- Old price: ₹400
- Outstanding shares: 8,00,000 (excludes 2,00,000 ESOP reserved but unissued)
- New investment: ₹10Cr at ₹250/share = 4,00,000 new shares
- Shares issuable at old price: ₹10Cr / ₹400 = 2,50,000

```
New Price = ₹400 × (8,00,000 + 2,50,000) / (8,00,000 + 4,00,000)
= ₹400 × 10,50,000 / 12,00,000
= ₹400 × 0.875
= ₹350
```

New share count for investor: ₹8Cr / ₹350 = **2,28,571 shares** (up from 2,00,000)
Extra shares: **28,571**

---

## Type 3: Broad-Based Weighted Average (The Founder-Friendly Standard)

**Mechanism:** Same calculation as narrow-based, but the denominator includes ALL shares on a fully diluted basis — issued shares + ESOP pool + warrants + convertibles.

**Formula:**
```
New Conversion Price = Old Conversion Price ×
  (Fully Diluted Shares + Shares Issuable at Old Price) /
  (Fully Diluted Shares + New Shares Issued)

Where "Fully Diluted Shares" = ALL shares including ESOP pool, warrants, convertibles
```

**Example (same scenario):**
- Old price: ₹400
- Fully diluted shares: 10,00,000 (includes 2,00,000 ESOP reserved)
- New investment: ₹10Cr at ₹250/share = 4,00,000 new shares
- Shares issuable at old price: ₹10Cr / ₹400 = 2,50,000

```
New Price = ₹400 × (10,00,000 + 2,50,000) / (10,00,000 + 4,00,000)
= ₹400 × 12,50,000 / 14,00,000
= ₹400 × 0.8929
= ₹357.14
```

New share count for investor: ₹8Cr / ₹357.14 = **2,24,000 shares** (up from 2,00,000)
Extra shares: **24,000**

## The Side-by-Side Comparison

| Anti-Dilution Type | New Conversion Price | New Share Count | Extra Shares | Founder Dilution |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Full Ratchet** | ₹250 | 3,20,000 | 1,20,000 | Severe |
| **Narrow-Based WA** | ₹350 | 2,28,571 | 28,571 | Moderate |
| **Broad-Based WA** | ₹357.14 | 2,24,000 | 24,000 | Minimal |
| **No Anti-Dilution** | ₹400 (unchanged) | 2,00,000 | 0 | None |

**At the same down round, the difference between full ratchet and broad-based WA is 96,000 extra investor shares — at zero cost to the investor, at full cost to founders.**

On a ₹500 crore exit, 96,000 shares represent approximately ₹4.8 crore of exit proceeds transferring from founders to the investor.

## Carve-Outs: When Anti-Dilution Doesn't Apply

Most SHAs include carve-outs — situations where issuing shares at a lower price does NOT trigger anti-dilution. These are standard and important to understand:

| Carve-Out | Why It Exists |
|---|---|
| ESOP issuances from approved plan | ESOPs are below FMV by design — triggering anti-dilution every time would make ESOPs impossible |
| Shares issued as acquisition consideration | M&A transactions use shares at negotiated prices |
| Shares issued to strategic partners | Partnership agreements at negotiated prices |
| Shares issued in rights offerings | Pro-rata issuances to existing shareholders |
| Shares issued as conversion of existing instruments | Converting CCDs, CCPS — already accounted for |

**What matters for founders:** Make sure ESOP issuances are explicitly carved out. If they're not, every ESOP grant below Series A price triggers anti-dilution — which is almost always the case.

---

## Negotiating Anti-Dilution: What to Ask For

**Non-negotiable (always get this):**
Broad-based weighted average. This is standard market practice. Narrow-based and full ratchet are both investor-friendly deviations from the standard.

**Negotiable in strong markets:**
- "Pay-to-play": anti-dilution only applies if the existing investor participates in the down round. If they don't participate, they lose anti-dilution protection. This aligns investor incentives with supporting the company.

**The pay-to-play ask:**
"We're happy with broad-based weighted average anti-dilution. We'd like to include a pay-to-play provision so that the protection is linked to your continued participation in the company's growth."

Most institutional investors will accept pay-to-play in a competitive fundraising environment.

---

## The Most Important Thing to Know

Anti-dilution matters most in the scenarios you hope never happen — down rounds. When you're raising your Series A at a good valuation, you're signing an SHA that will affect you 3–5 years later in a market you can't predict.

**The right approach:**
1. Always push for broad-based weighted average
2. Include pay-to-play if you have leverage
3. Run the down round math before signing — what does your cap table look like if you raise your next round at half price?
4. Know the carve-outs in your SHA, especially for ESOP

The founders who get hurt by anti-dilution are not the ones who had bad luck. They're the ones who signed without running the numbers.
