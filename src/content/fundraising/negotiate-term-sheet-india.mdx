# How to Negotiate Your Term Sheet Without a Lawyer Bleeding You Dry

Most founders negotiate their term sheet in one of two ways:

**Way 1:** They hand it to their CA, who marks up 40 clauses and charges ₹3 lakh for the privilege of making the investor annoyed and slowing the deal by 6 weeks.

**Way 2:** They sign it as-is because they're afraid negotiating will kill the deal.

Both approaches are wrong.

The right approach: identify the 5 clauses that materially affect your economics and control. Negotiate those firmly but professionally. Accept the rest. Close fast.

This guide tells you exactly which clauses those are, what to ask for, what to say when the investor pushes back, and what you can safely ignore.

---

## The Negotiation Mindset First

Before the tactics, the mindset.

**You are not adversaries.** If this investor is right for you, you'll be partners for 5–8 years. The negotiation is the first test of how you work together. Coming in with 40 redlines signals you don't understand market norms and will be difficult to work with. Coming in with 5 focused asks signals you know what matters and you're a serious operator.

**Valuation is already negotiated.** By the time you have a term sheet, valuation is largely set. Trying to reopen it after term sheet signing is bad form unless there's been a material change. Focus on governance and economic terms, not the headline number.

**Market norms exist.** Many terms in a term sheet are standard. Pushing back on standard terms is a rookie mistake — it signals you haven't done deals before. Know what's market before you negotiate.

**Speed matters.** Deals die when they take too long. Negotiate focused, negotiate fast, close.

## The 5 Clauses Worth Fighting For

### Clause 1: Liquidation Preference Structure

**What you'll see:** "1x participating preferred liquidation preference"
**What you want:** "1x non-participating preferred liquidation preference"

**Why it matters:** Participating preferred means the investor gets their money back first AND participates pro-rata in remaining proceeds. On a ₹100Cr exit where the investor put in ₹15Cr for 20%, participating preferred might give them ₹15Cr + ₹17Cr = ₹32Cr instead of ₹20Cr. That ₹12Cr comes from founders and other shareholders.

**The ask:**
"We're comfortable with the 1x multiple — that gives you full downside protection. We'd like to remove the participating feature and go non-participating, which is the market standard for Series A rounds in India at this stage."

**If they push back:** Ask why. If they say "it's our standard," point to recent comparable deals (check Tracxn/Entrackr for comps). If they insist on participating, ask for a participation cap (e.g., "participating until 3x total return, then converts to common").

**What to accept if you can't win:** 1x participating with a 3x cap. Not great but livable.

**What to walk away from:** 2x+ participating preferred. This is not market and will haunt you at every exit scenario.

---

### Clause 2: ESOP Pool Timing (Pre-Money vs Post-Money)

**What you'll see:** "10% ESOP pool to be created on a pre-money fully diluted basis"
**What you want:** "10% ESOP pool on a post-money fully diluted basis"

**Why it matters:** Pre-money ESOP pool dilutes only founders. Post-money ESOP pool dilutes everyone proportionally including the investor.

**The math:** On a ₹40Cr Series A (₹32Cr pre-money):
- Pre-money 10% ESOP: You bear the full 10% dilution before investor calculates their stake
- Post-money 10% ESOP: Everyone (founders + investor) is diluted proportionally

Difference to founders: roughly 3–4% of company depending on cap table structure. At a ₹500Cr exit, that's ₹15–20Cr.

**The ask:**
"We want to make sure we have the right ESOP pool to hire great people — that's good for everyone. We'd prefer to create the pool post-money so the dilution is shared, which is more aligned incentive-wise. Can we do that?"

**If they push back:** Ask them to size the ESOP pool to what you actually need for the next 18 months of hiring, not a large buffer. A 7% pool post-money is better for you than a 10% pool pre-money.

**Counter-offer:** "If it's pre-money, can we size it to 7% rather than 10%? That covers our hiring plan for the next round."

---

### Clause 3: Anti-Dilution — Broad-Based Weighted Average

**What you'll see:** "Weighted average anti-dilution protection"
**What you want:** "Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution protection"

**Why it matters:** "Weighted average" without "broad-based" defaults to narrow-based in many SHAs — which is more punitive in a down round. The word "broad" matters.

**The ask:** Simply ensure the final SHA says "broad-based weighted average." Most investors will accept this without much pushback because it's standard. The risk is not noticing that "weighted average" became "narrow-based" in the SHA.

**Red flag:** If the term sheet says "full ratchet anti-dilution" — that is not standard. Push back hard or walk away.

**What full ratchet means:** If you raise a down round at any point, the investor's original price per share resets to the new lower price. This can hand investors a dramatically increased ownership stake in a down round at the expense of founders. It is aggressive and uncommon in legitimate seed/Series A rounds in India.

---

### Clause 4: Reserved Matters — Scope and Thresholds

**What you'll see:** A list of 15–25 "reserved matters" requiring investor consent
**What you want:** A shorter list with appropriate thresholds for routine business decisions

**Why it matters:** Reserved matters are daily operational constraints. If "any contract above ₹10 lakh" requires investor consent, you'll be getting approvals for routine vendor agreements. If "any hire with CTC above ₹25 lakh" requires consent, you'll need investor sign-off to hire a decent engineer.

**The specific asks:**

| Reserved Matter | What They Ask | What to Push For |
|---|---|---|
| Capital expenditure threshold | ₹10–25L | ₹50L or higher |
| Contract/commitment threshold | ₹10L | ₹25–50L |
| Individual hire threshold | ₹25L CTC | VP/C-suite only |
| Business scope change | Any change | Material change only |
| Litigation settlement | Any | Above ₹25L |
| Related party transactions | Any | Above ₹10L |

**The ask:**
"We want to make sure we can operate efficiently without needing board votes for routine decisions. Can we increase the thresholds so that reserved matters apply to genuinely significant decisions? Specifically, we'd like capex threshold at ₹50L, contracts at ₹25L, and hires limited to senior management (VP and above)."

**Where you'll get pushback:** Investors worry about founders making bad decisions without accountability. Frame higher thresholds as enabling faster operations, not avoiding accountability. Offer to include a monthly MIS that gives full transparency on decisions below the threshold.

---

### Clause 5: Founder Vesting — Credit for Past Service + Double-Trigger

**What you'll see:** "4-year vesting from closing date with 1-year cliff"
**What you want:** Credit for time already worked + double-trigger acceleration on acquisition

**Why it matters:**

Credit for past service: If you've been working for 18 months before closing, you should be 18 months into your vesting schedule, not starting from zero.

Double-trigger acceleration: If the company is acquired and you're fired by the acquirer within 12 months, you should vest fully. Without this, acquirers can terminate you and recapture your unvested shares.

**The ask on past service:**
"We've been working on this for [X] months before closing. We'd like credit for that time — effectively starting our vesting schedule from [incorporation date] rather than closing date. At minimum, we'd like credit for the first 12 months."

**The ask on acceleration:**
"We'd like double-trigger acceleration — 100% vesting if we're terminated without cause or resign for good reason within 12 months of an acquisition. This is standard in well-structured term sheets and protects both us and the acquirer."

**Most investors will accept both.** These are reasonable, standard asks. Resistance here is a yellow flag about the investor's intentions.

## The 10 Clauses You Can Accept Without a Fight

These are standard terms. Don't waste negotiating capital on them.

| Clause | Why It's Standard | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **1x liquidation preference multiple** | Standard downside protection | Accept |
| **Right of First Refusal (ROFR)** | Standard in all SHAs | Accept |
| **Tag-along rights** | Standard minority protection | Accept |
| **Information rights** (quarterly/annual) | Reasonable and good for you too | Accept |
| **Pro-rata rights** | Standard for follow-on | Accept |
| **Drag-along at 75%+** | Standard with high threshold | Accept if threshold is high enough |
| **Board seat for lead investor** | Standard | Accept |
| **ESOP plan approval as reserved matter** | Standard | Accept |
| **Confidentiality in term sheet** | Standard and binding | Accept |
| **Exclusivity period (30–45 days)** | Standard | Accept |

## The 3 Red Flag Terms That Should Make You Pause

### Red Flag 1: Investor Can Fire the CEO

**What it looks like:** "Appointment or removal of CEO/MD requires investor consent as a reserved matter"

**Why it's a problem:** You can be replaced as CEO of your own company without your agreement. This has happened to multiple Indian founders.

**What to do:** Push back firmly. If the investor insists, insist that removal requires a supermajority including founder consent, except in cases of gross misconduct.

### Red Flag 2: Board Majority Before Series B

**What it looks like:** Term sheet gives investor 2 board seats immediately, putting board at 2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent.

**Why it's a problem:** With 2-2-1, the independent director is the swing vote on every decision. If the investor influenced the independent director selection, they effectively control the board.

**What to do:** For seed/Series A, push for 2-1-1 (2 founders, 1 investor, 1 independent). If they insist on 2 seats, ensure you control the independent director appointment.

### Red Flag 3: Drag-Along Threshold Below 50%

**What it looks like:** "Drag-along can be exercised by holders of 40% of preference shares"

**Why it's a problem:** If the investor holds 40% of preference shares (entirely possible by Series A), they can unilaterally force a sale.

**What to do:** Drag-along threshold should require consent of both founders and investors — or at minimum be set at 75%+. Never accept a drag-along that the investor can trigger alone.

## How to Have the Negotiation Conversation

The actual words matter. Here's how to approach each ask:

**Opening the negotiation after receiving the term sheet:**

> "Thank you for the term sheet — we're excited about this partnership. We've reviewed it carefully and there are a few points we'd like to discuss. Everything else looks good and we want to move fast. Can we get on a call in the next 2 days to work through these?"

**Tone:** Positive, specific, quick.

**Making an ask:**

> "On the liquidation preference — we're comfortable with the 1x multiple, that's reasonable. We'd like to remove the participating feature and go non-participating. That's standard for rounds at this stage and aligns our incentives better. Is that something you can work with?"

**Tone:** Frame as what you want, not what you're refusing.

**When they say no:**

> "I understand. Can you help me understand why participating is important to you? I want to make sure we understand your concerns so we can find something that works for both of us."

**Tone:** Curious, not combative. Understanding their reason often reveals room for creative compromise.

**When they won't move:**

> "We appreciate you explaining that. Before we decide, can you walk us through how this would play out in a few exit scenarios? I want to make sure we understand the economics before we agree."

Running the math together often reveals whether a term is as bad as you fear — or confirms it is.

## The Leverage Reality: When You Have It and When You Don't

Negotiating leverage comes from one place: **alternatives.**

**If you have multiple term sheets:** You have significant leverage. Investors know it. You can push harder on every clause and move faster with timelines. The best negotiating position is not needing any single deal.

**If you have one term sheet:** You have less leverage but not zero. You still have the ability to walk away. The investor knows closing you is better than starting over.

**If you have no term sheet and are running low on runway:** You have almost no leverage. In this position, focus only on the absolute red flags (board control, bad leaver provisions, full ratchet anti-dilution) and accept the rest. A deal with some bad terms is better than no deal.

**The honest question to ask yourself before every negotiation:** "Am I negotiating from a position of genuine alternatives, or am I negotiating out of principle?" If it's principle, pick your battles carefully.

## What to Do After the Term Sheet Is Signed

1. **Start legal due diligence immediately** — don't wait. Every day of delay costs you momentum.
2. **Fix known issues** — IP assignments, employment contracts, statutory filings. Do this before the investor finds them.
3. **Get a startup lawyer** — not your family CA, not a generalist firm. Get someone who does startup transactions regularly (Trilegal, AZB, Khaitan, Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas, or a boutique startup specialist).
4. **SHA negotiation is where the details live** — the term sheet is a summary. The SHA will add provisions not in the term sheet. Read the SHA against the term sheet for every material point.
5. **Build the relationship, not just the deal** — use the due diligence period to get to know your investor. Their behavior during this process is a preview of the next 5 years.

## The Budget Reality: What Good Legal Counsel Actually Costs

| Firm Type | Typical Seed Round Cost | Typical Series A Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Top-tier national firm (AZB, Trilegal, Cyril) | ₹3–8L | ₹6–15L |
| Mid-tier startup specialist | ₹1.5–4L | ₹3–8L |
| Boutique startup lawyer | ₹75K–2L | ₹2–5L |
| Generalist CA / small firm | ₹25K–75K | ₹50K–1.5L |

**The right choice:** For a seed round under ₹2 crore, a boutique startup specialist is fine. For a Series A above ₹5 crore, a mid-tier or top-tier firm. The generalist CA is appropriate only for statutory compliance (ROC filings, FEMA reporting) — not for transaction negotiation.

**The math:** On a ₹20 crore Series A, spending ₹5 lakh on a good lawyer is 0.25% of the deal. One poorly negotiated clause (participating preferred vs. non-participating on a ₹200Cr exit) costs 5–10%. The legal bill is not the place to cut corners.

## Checklist: Before You Sign the Term Sheet

- [ ] Liquidation preference is 1x non-participating (or acceptable structure)
- [ ] ESOP pool is post-money or sized reasonably
- [ ] Anti-dilution is broad-based weighted average (or better)
- [ ] Reserved matters thresholds are operational (not micro-management level)
- [ ] Founder vesting includes credit for past service
- [ ] Founder vesting includes double-trigger acceleration
- [ ] Board composition gives founders meaningful control (2-1-1 or 2-0-1 at seed)
- [ ] Drag-along threshold is 75%+ and requires founder consent
- [ ] No CEO removal as reserved matter
- [ ] No full ratchet anti-dilution
- [ ] Exclusivity period is reasonable (30–45 days, not 90 days)
- [ ] No personal founder guarantees or indemnities
