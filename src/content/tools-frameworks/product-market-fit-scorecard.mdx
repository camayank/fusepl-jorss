# Product-Market Fit Scorecard: Do You Actually Have PMF?

Sean Ellis, who coined the "40% test" for PMF, surveyed his startups and discovered a clean threshold: if 40% or more of your users say they'd be "very disappointed" if they could no longer use your product, you have PMF. Below 40%, you don't.

That test is useful. But it's insufficient for Indian startups because:
1. Indian users are more polite — they say "very disappointed" even when they'd switch tomorrow
2. It's a survey — behavior matters more than stated preference
3. PMF looks different across B2B, D2C, consumer, and marketplace models

This scorecard gives you 20 behavioral and metric-based questions across 5 dimensions. It produces a score and a clear diagnosis of where you are on the PMF spectrum.

---

## The PMF Spectrum

PMF is not binary. There are 4 stages:

| Stage | Description | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Pre-PMF** | No signal. Customers don't come back or tell others. | 0–30 |
| **PMF signal** | Weak but real signal. Some users love it, most don't. | 31–55 |
| **PMF achieved** | Clear signal. Retention strong, organic growth emerging. | 56–75 |
| **Strong PMF** | Exceptional signal. Customers pull the product forward. | 76–100 |

---

## The 20-Question Scorecard

Score each question 0, 3, 5, or 7 based on your situation. Be brutally honest.

### Dimension 1: Retention (25 points max)

**Q1: Day 7 retention**
- 0 pts: < 10% of new users return on Day 7
- 3 pts: 10–20% return on Day 7
- 5 pts: 20–35% return on Day 7
- 7 pts: > 35% return on Day 7

*(For B2B SaaS: replace D7 with weekly active usage rate — % of seats used in any given week)*

**Q2: Day 30 / Month 1 retention**
- 0 pts: < 5% retained at Day 30
- 3 pts: 5–15% retained
- 5 pts: 15–25% retained
- 7 pts: > 25% retained

*(For B2B SaaS: monthly churn rate — 0 pts if > 7%, 7 pts if < 2%)*

**Q3: Long-term retention (Month 3 or Q1 for B2B)**
- 0 pts: Same or worse retention curve as Month 1
- 3 pts: Slight flattening of churn curve
- 5 pts: Clear flattening — retention stabilises
- 7 pts: Retention curve flattens to > 20% by Month 6 (consumer) or < 2% monthly churn sustained (B2B)

**Q4: NRR / Expansion**
- 0 pts: Customers are downgrading (NRR < 90%)
- 3 pts: NRR 90–100% (stable but no expansion)
- 5 pts: NRR 100–110% (slight expansion)
- 7 pts: NRR > 110% (customers pay you more over time)

---

### Dimension 2: Organic Growth (20 points max)

**Q5: Word-of-mouth (NPS / referral rate)**
- 0 pts: No organic referrals. All growth is paid.
- 3 pts: Occasional referrals. NPS between 20–40.
- 5 pts: 15–25% of new users come from referrals. NPS 40–60.
- 7 pts: > 25% organic. NPS > 60.

*(India note: Indian users rarely give formal referrals but share on WhatsApp groups. Track WhatsApp referral codes if applicable)*

**Q6: CAC trend (is it getting cheaper or more expensive?)**
- 0 pts: CAC increasing every quarter despite scale
- 3 pts: CAC flat
- 5 pts: CAC decreasing as organic grows
- 7 pts: Organic acquisition > 40% of total; blended CAC declining 20%+ YoY

**Q7: Brand pull (customers find you without marketing)**
- 0 pts: Nobody has heard of you. All inbound is from ads.
- 3 pts: Occasional inbound from SEO or press
- 5 pts: Meaningful inbound — 20%+ of leads come to you
- 7 pts: Customers reference you by name. Waiting lists. People share your product.

---

### Dimension 3: Customer Desperation (20 points max)

**Q8: Sean Ellis 40% test**
*Ask active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" — % who say "very disappointed"*
- 0 pts: < 20%
- 3 pts: 20–30%
- 5 pts: 30–40%
- 7 pts: > 40%

**Q9: Usage depth**
*Do users use the core feature regularly? (Not just log in and leave)*
- 0 pts: Users log in but rarely use the core workflow
- 3 pts: 30–50% of active users use core feature weekly
- 5 pts: > 50% use core feature multiple times per week
- 7 pts: Product is embedded in daily workflow. Users notice when it's down.

**Q10: Urgency of reactivation**
*When you reach out to churned users, do they come back?*
- 0 pts: Churned users don't respond. They've found alternatives.
- 3 pts: Some come back with prompting
- 5 pts: 15–25% reactivation rate on churn campaigns
- 7 pts: Churned users proactively ask to come back. They miss it.

**Q11: Customer frustration at removal**
*Have any customers complained, threatened to leave, or escalated when a feature was removed?*
- 0 pts: No. Customers barely notice changes.
- 3 pts: Occasional feedback on feature changes
- 5 pts: Customers actively request features back. Constructive frustration.
- 7 pts: Customers organise to protect features. Feature removal causes significant backlash.

---

### Dimension 4: Sales Velocity (20 points max)

**Q12: Sales cycle trend**
- 0 pts: Sales cycle lengthening or stalling
- 3 pts: Sales cycle stable
- 5 pts: Sales cycle shortening as product speaks for itself
- 7 pts: Product-led — customers convert in the trial/freemium phase without sales touch

**Q13: Close rate on qualified demos (B2B) / trial-to-paid (consumer)**
- 0 pts: < 10% close rate
- 3 pts: 10–20% close rate
- 5 pts: 20–35% close rate
- 7 pts: > 35% (B2B) or > 5% (consumer freemium-to-paid)

**Q14: Average deal size trend**
- 0 pts: Deals are getting smaller — you're selling to whoever will buy
- 3 pts: Deal size stable
- 5 pts: ACV increasing as customers upgrade or expand
- 7 pts: Customers proactively expand contracts. Multi-year deals emerging.

**Q15: Customer champion strength**
*Do buyers become internal advocates after purchase?*
- 0 pts: No. The buyer moves on after purchasing.
- 3 pts: Buyer provides positive review if asked
- 5 pts: Buyer proactively recommends to colleagues
- 7 pts: Buyer becomes a reference customer, case study, or external advocate

---

### Dimension 5: Market Pull (15 points max)

**Q16: Inbound from outside your network**
- 0 pts: All your customers know you personally or came through paid ads
- 3 pts: Occasional inbound from press or SEO
- 5 pts: Steady stream of inbound from search, communities, or word-of-mouth
- 7 pts: Investors, partners, and acquirers are finding you — not the reverse

**Q17: Competitive pressure on your existing customers**
*Are competitors trying to poach your customers?*
- 0 pts: Nobody is competing for your customers (market might not exist)
- 3 pts: Competitors pitch your customers occasionally; low win rate for them
- 5 pts: Competitors are actively targeting you; your customers are staying
- 7 pts: You are the product competitors benchmark against. Being imitated = leading.

**Q18: Industry / community recognition**
- 0 pts: No recognition beyond your immediate network
- 3 pts: Mentioned in industry publications or communities
- 5 pts: Considered a category leader or notable player
- 7 pts: Defining the category. Journalists call you first.

**Q19: Ability to raise prices without significant churn**
- 0 pts: Price increase attempts have failed; customers leave
- 3 pts: You've never tested pricing; afraid to raise
- 5 pts: Raised prices 10–20%; modest churn but net positive revenue impact
- 7 pts: Raised prices 30%+; customers stayed. Pricing power demonstrated.

**Q20: Net-zero sales hypothesis**
*If you stopped all paid marketing and outbound sales today, would you still grow?*
- 0 pts: All growth stops immediately
- 3 pts: Organic base maintains but no new growth
- 5 pts: Some organic channels sustain slow growth
- 7 pts: Product would continue growing organically without sales or marketing spend

## Your Score and What It Means

**Add up your total score (max 100).**

### 0–30: Pre-PMF

You don't have PMF yet. This is normal and honest. The path forward:

1. **Identify your top 10% most engaged users** — who are they? What's different about them?
2. **Do customer discovery interviews** (not surveys) — find the pain point that makes customers desperate
3. **Narrow your ICP** — you're likely trying to serve too many segments. Pick one.
4. **Stop scaling.** More users won't fix a PMF problem. It will make it more expensive.

**Should you raise money?** Only if you have a clear hypothesis on what PMF looks like and you need capital to test it. Don't raise to buy time to find PMF — raise to execute on a specific test.

### 31–55: PMF Signal

You have a real signal but it's not yet clear enough to scale on. This is actually the most dangerous stage — things are working well enough that you're tempted to scale, but the signal isn't strong enough to sustain it.

**What to do:**
1. Identify the cohort of customers who love your product (the 40% Sean Ellis segment)
2. Over-serve that cohort — make your product irreplaceable for them
3. Ignore everyone else temporarily
4. Rebuild acquisition strategy to focus exclusively on customers who look like your best cohort

**Should you raise money?** Yes, if you can articulate the PMF signal clearly and have a plan to strengthen it. Investors understand the journey.

### 56–75: PMF Achieved

You have PMF. Now the job is to understand it deeply and scale the signal before expanding.

**What to do:**
1. Document your ICP precisely (industry, company size, buyer role, pain point, trigger event)
2. Build repeatable sales and marketing around the proven ICP
3. Invest in customer success to protect retention
4. Start expanding carefully to adjacent segments

**Should you raise money?** Yes. You can credibly show retention, organic growth, and a path to scale. This is the right time for Series A.

### 76–100: Strong PMF

This is a special state. Your product is pulling the market toward it. Protect it by:
1. Not over-hiring — don't dilute culture and quality with premature scale
2. Not moving upmarket too fast — strong PMF in SMB doesn't automatically mean enterprise
3. Not adding too many features — complexity kills what made you good

**Should you raise money?** Yes — and you'll raise it on strong terms. Run a competitive process.

## The PMF Diagnostic Questions to Ask Your Best Customers

These 5 questions (asked in conversations, not surveys) reveal PMF faster than any metric:

1. **"What would you do if we shut down tomorrow?"**
   Strong PMF: "I'd be devastated. I have no alternative." Weak PMF: "I'd find something else."

2. **"Why did you initially choose us over alternatives?"**
   Listen for specificity. "It was cheaper" is weak. "It was the only solution that did X" is strong.

3. **"What would have to change for you to stop using us?"**
   Strong PMF: hard to imagine stopping. Weak PMF: "If the price goes up" or "if a better option appears."

4. **"Who else have you recommended us to?"**
   Strong PMF: spontaneous referrals with specific names. Weak PMF: "I might mention it sometime."

5. **"What does our product do for your business that nothing else does?"**
   Strong PMF: clear, specific, painful-to-lose value articulation. Weak PMF: vague or generic answer.

---

*This scorecard is a diagnostic tool, not a pass/fail test. Use it every quarter to track your PMF trajectory — the trend matters as much as the score.*
