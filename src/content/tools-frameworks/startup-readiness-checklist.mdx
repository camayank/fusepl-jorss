# Startup Readiness Checklist — Are You Ready to Launch, Raise, or Scale?

The three most consequential decisions in any startup's early life are: when to launch to real customers, when to start fundraising, and when to start scaling aggressively. Each of these transitions has a natural timing — go too early and you burn runway on an unproven model; go too late and a competitor takes the market.

Most Indian founder advice on timing is vague. "Launch when you have product-market fit." "Raise when your metrics are strong." "Scale when you are ready." This kind of guidance is useless — it does not tell you what fit, strong, or ready actually mean in measurable terms.

This article provides specific, verifiable criteria for all three transitions. Each section has a 25-35 point checklist with Yes/No items. The sections are colour-coded: Green (ready), Amber (proceed with caution, specific items to fix), Red (not ready — fix these first).

---

## How to Use This Checklist

**Scoring:**
- Each item is Yes (1 point) or No (0 points)
- Red items: if any Red item is No, stop and fix it before proceeding
- Amber items: if more than 3 Amber items are No, address them before proceeding
- Green items: directional guidance; a few No answers are acceptable

**Frequency:** Run each checklist at the relevant transition. For ongoing health monitoring, run the full checklist quarterly.

## Section 1: Ready to Launch

**Definition of "Launch":** Making your product available to paying customers (or free-plan users with a clear monetisation path) with active outreach and onboarding.

### Legal and Corporate (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| L1 | Company incorporated (Pvt Ltd recommended; LLP acceptable) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| L2 | Business bank account open and linked to company PAN | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| L3 | GST registration applied/obtained (if turnover will exceed ₹20L in year) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| L4 | DPIIT recognition applied for or obtained | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| L5 | Founders' shareholder agreement signed with equity split documented | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**Why L5 is Red:** Undocumented equity splits are the most common cause of early co-founder disputes that kill startups. The conversation is uncomfortable; the legal consequence of avoiding it is existential. Have the conversation and sign the agreement before launch.

---

### Product Readiness (8 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| P1 | Core use case works end-to-end without manual workarounds | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| P2 | Product tested with at least 10 potential customers (not just friends/family) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| P3 | All critical bugs that prevent core use case are resolved | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| P4 | User onboarding takes < 30 minutes for a new user to get to first value | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| P5 | Basic analytics/tracking in place (can you see what users do?) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| P6 | Data backup and recovery process exists (not just a plan — actually tested) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| P7 | Customer can reach support (email/WhatsApp/chat) and get a response within 24 hours | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |
| P8 | Privacy policy and Terms of Service published on website/app | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |

**On P2:** The 10 customer threshold is a minimum, not a target. The point is that feedback must come from people who have no social obligation to be positive. If you have only tested with people who know and like you, your validation is biased. Find at least 5 strangers (in your target customer profile) to test before launch.

**On P4:** "Time to first value" is one of the most important product metrics for a new user. If a new customer takes more than a day to understand what your product does for them, your onboarding is broken. This is an Amber item (not Red) because some products have inherently longer time-to-value — but you should measure and minimise it.

---

### Team Readiness (4 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1 | Can the founding team build the product AND sell it? | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| T2 | At least one founder is actively doing sales (not delegating sales) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| T3 | Roles and responsibilities between co-founders are clear | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| T4 | Team is sufficient to handle 3x current user volume without additional headcount | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |

**On T1 and T2:** The most dangerous founding team configuration for a launch is one technical founder who has never sold anything and assumes the product will sell itself. The founder must do the first 20-30 sales calls personally. This is non-negotiable. You cannot delegate learning about why customers buy (or do not buy) your product. Every founder, regardless of technical or product background, must spend the first 6 months doing sales.

---

### Market Readiness (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| M1 | Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) is defined: specific industry, company size, job title, use case | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| M2 | Pricing hypothesis tested: at least 10 conversations that included "would you pay ₹X/month?" | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| M3 | You can identify 100 specific companies/individuals who match your ICP in India | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| M4 | You have a clear answer to "why will a customer choose you over their current solution?" | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| M5 | Competitive landscape mapped: at least the top 3-5 alternatives customers currently use | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |

**On M1:** ICP vagueness is a launch killer. "Everyone who needs CRM" is not an ICP. "B2B founders of SaaS startups in India with 5-50 employees who are currently using Google Sheets for customer tracking" is an ICP. Specificity is what allows you to find your first 20 customers, create relevant messaging, and build a product that is genuinely better than the alternative for that specific segment.

---

### Finance Readiness (3 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | Minimum 6 months of runway post-launch costs calculated (not estimated — actually calculated with specific line items) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| F2 | Monthly burn for the next 6 months modelled with specific assumptions | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| F3 | Breakeven/profitability conditions identified: how many customers at what ARPU needed? | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |

**Launch Readiness Score:**

```
Maximum score:        25 points
Red items (count):    ___
Amber No items (count): ___
Green No items (count): ___

Go/No-Go:
If ANY Red item = No → NOT ready to launch (fix Red items first)
If > 3 Amber items = No → Proceed with caution; address Amber items in first 30 days
If ≤ 3 Amber No items → Ready to launch
```

## Section 2: Ready to Raise (Fundraising Readiness Checklist)

**Definition of "Ready to Raise":** Starting formal investor conversations with the goal of closing a priced equity round.

This is a 30-point checklist with traffic-light (Green/Amber/Red) scoring. Green = must have. Amber = should have, will be asked about. Red = blocking issue — investors will not fund if these are unresolved.

### Traction Thresholds (by Model)

The most important fundraising readiness question is whether you have the right level of traction for the stage and model you are raising for. These are the minimum thresholds Indian institutional investors (seed funds, Angel investors, Series A VCs) expect in 2025-26:

**For SaaS (Seed Round, ₹2-8 Cr):**

| # | Item | Colour | Threshold | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TR1 | Monthly Recurring Revenue | 🔴 Red | ₹5-20 lakh MRR | Yes / No |
| TR2 | Paying customer count | 🔴 Red | 5-20 paying customers | Yes / No |
| TR3 | Month-over-month growth trend | 🟡 Amber | 15-25% MoM for 3+ months | Yes / No |
| TR4 | NRR (if measurable) | 🟢 Green | > 100% | Yes / No |

**For SaaS (Series A, ₹15-50 Cr):**

| # | Item | Colour | Threshold | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TR5 | ARR | 🔴 Red | ₹2-8 Cr ARR | Yes / No |
| TR6 | Revenue growth rate | 🔴 Red | > 100% YoY | Yes / No |
| TR7 | NRR | 🟡 Amber | > 105% | Yes / No |
| TR8 | Logo churn | 🟡 Amber | < 10% annually | Yes / No |

**For D2C (Seed Round):**

| # | Item | Colour | Threshold | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TR9 | Revenue | 🔴 Red | ₹10-30 lakh/month | Yes / No |
| TR10 | Gross margin | 🔴 Red | > 45% | Yes / No |
| TR11 | Repeat purchase rate | 🟡 Amber | > 30% within 90 days | Yes / No |
| TR12 | CAC payback | 🟡 Amber | < 6 months | Yes / No |

**For Marketplace (Seed Round):**

| # | Item | Colour | Threshold | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TR13 | GMV | 🟡 Amber | ₹50L-2 Cr/month | Yes / No |
| TR14 | Net revenue / take rate established | 🔴 Red | > 0 (any take rate working) | Yes / No |
| TR15 | Liquidity metric | 🔴 Red | > 60% of posted demand fulfilled | Yes / No |

---

### Financial Model Readiness (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| FM1 | 18-month financial model built with monthly detail | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| FM2 | Three scenarios (base/bull/bear) with different growth assumptions | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| FM3 | Key assumptions documented and defensible (not pulled from thin air) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| FM4 | Model shows how this fundraise's capital will be deployed (by function) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| FM5 | Milestones that will be achieved by end of this funding round clearly stated | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**On FM3:** Investors will scrutinise assumptions. "We will grow 20% MoM" is not a defensible assumption unless you have already grown 20% MoM for at least 3 months. Assumptions must be either historically demonstrated or based on specific, identifiable assumptions (e.g., "we have 5 enterprise deals in pipeline worth ₹1.2 Cr each; we expect to close 2-3 in the next quarter based on current deal stage").

---

### Pitch and Materials Readiness (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | 12-slide investor deck (problem/solution/market/model/traction/team/ask) complete | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| D2 | 2-minute verbal elevator pitch rehearsed and tested on non-technical listeners | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| D3 | 1-pager (teaser) that can be shared via email before deck | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| D4 | Data room: financials, cap table, incorporation docs, customer contracts accessible | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| D5 | 5-minute product demo rehearsed (live product, not slides) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |

**On D4:** The data room is the due diligence package. Having it ready before investor meetings means you can close faster when interest is there. The data room should include: audited/unaudited financials for last 2 years, MIS (management information system) reports, cap table, DPIIT certificate, copies of key customer contracts (anonymised if needed), employment agreements for key team, and any IP documentation.

---

### Legal and Corporate Cleanliness (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| LC1 | Cap table is clean: no undocumented convertible notes, no missing share certificates | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| LC2 | Shareholder agreement in place with all current shareholders | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| LC3 | No material disputes with employees, co-founders, or customers (or disclosed and resolved) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| LC4 | All IP (code, brand, patents) owned by the company — no personal ownership by founders | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| LC5 | Founder vesting schedule in place (4-year vest with 1-year cliff recommended) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |

**On LC4:** This is the most frequently discovered issue during investor due diligence. In India, technical co-founders often write the codebase before the company is incorporated and never formally assign the IP to the company. The fix requires an IP assignment agreement. If this has not been done, do it now — before starting fundraising.

---

### Investor Network Readiness (5 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| IN1 | Target investor list of 20-30 investors researched and relevant to your stage/sector | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| IN2 | Warm introduction pathway identified for at least 10 target investors | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| IN3 | At least 2-3 meetings already completed (test pitches with friendly investors) | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |
| IN4 | Rejection feedback from at least 5 investors incorporated into deck/pitch | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |
| IN5 | Your ask (cheque size + valuation expectation) benchmarked against recent comparable deals | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**Fundraising Readiness Score:**

```
Maximum score:        30 points
Red items No:         ___
Amber items No:       ___

Go/No-Go:
If > 2 Red items = No → NOT ready to raise (significant issues to fix)
If 1-2 Red items No AND they are fixable within 4 weeks → Fix then start
If ≤ 1 Red item No AND < 4 Amber items No → Ready to start raising
```

## Section 3: Ready to Scale (Post-Series A Checklist)

**Definition of "Ready to Scale":** Aggressively deploying capital to grow revenue, headcount, and market presence — expecting to significantly increase burn rate in exchange for faster growth.

Scaling before the following conditions are met is the primary cause of "hypergrowth to implosion" in Indian startups.

### Product-Market Fit Validation (6 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| PMF1 | PMF Scorecard score ≥ 60 (see PMF Scorecard article) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| PMF2 | "Very disappointed" response on Sean Ellis survey > 40% | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| PMF3 | Organic/word-of-mouth referrals account for > 20% of new customer acquisition | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| PMF4 | NPS score > 50 (or equivalent customer satisfaction threshold) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| PMF5 | 3 cohorts of customers show NRR > 100% | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| PMF6 | You can describe clearly and specifically WHY customers stay and WHY they expand | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**On PMF3:** Word-of-mouth referrals are the most reliable PMF signal. Customers only refer a product to colleagues and peers when it has solved a real problem for them. If all your customers came from paid acquisition or direct outreach, you cannot assume PMF — the product may be good enough to buy once but not compelling enough to talk about.

---

### Unit Economics Validation (6 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| UE1 | CAC payback period < 18 months (SaaS) or < 9 months (D2C/marketplace) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| UE2 | Gross margin > 60% (SaaS) or > 40% (marketplace/D2C) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| UE3 | NRR > 100% for at least 2 consecutive quarters | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| UE4 | Unit economics at current scale are better than 6 months ago (improving trend) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| UE5 | LTV/CAC ratio > 3x | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| UE6 | Contribution margin positive (revenue - variable costs > 0) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**On UE6:** Scaling a negative contribution margin business is a guaranteed path to insolvency. If each new customer loses money at the variable cost level — before fixed costs — then more customers means more losses. Before scaling, contribution margin must be positive. Fixed costs can still be negative (pre-profitability is fine), but variable cost economics must be positive.

---

### Team and Management Readiness (7 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| TM1 | First layer of management in place: at least one VP/Head per function (Sales, Engineering, Product) | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| TM2 | No single function is still run entirely by a founder with no manager under them | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| TM3 | Founders have shifted from doing to managing in at least 2 of their core functions | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| TM4 | Key person risk: no single person (including founders) whose departure would break core operations | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| TM5 | Hiring process documented: job descriptions, interview process, evaluation criteria defined | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |
| TM6 | Compensation bands defined for all open roles | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| TM7 | ESOP pool established and option grants issued to key employees | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |

**On TM1:** The most common failure mode in scaling Indian startups is the "Superhero Founder" syndrome — a founder who is personally bottlenecking every decision in Sales, Product, and Engineering because they have not hired strong enough managers. Scaling requires founders to let go of execution and move to strategy and management. This transition requires hiring people who are genuinely better than the founders at specific functions.

---

### Process and Operational Readiness (8 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| OP1 | Sales playbook documented: ICP, value proposition, demo script, objection handling, pricing | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| OP2 | Customer onboarding process < 14 days for standard customers | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| OP3 | Customer success SLA defined and communicated to customers | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| OP4 | Customer support response time < 4 hours (business hours) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| OP5 | Revenue operations (CRM) tracking pipeline, conversion rates, and forecast | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| OP6 | Product release process: new features ship without breaking existing functionality | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| OP7 | Incident response process: when product goes down, who does what, within what SLA? | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| OP8 | Vendor contracts and SLAs reviewed: key vendors have contractual uptime guarantees | 🟢 Green | Yes / No |

**On OP1:** You cannot scale sales without a sales playbook. A playbook documents what your best salesperson does and makes it repeatable. If your best SDR is the only person who can sell your product, you are not ready to scale. A playbook should include: the ICP definition, the outbound sequence (what you say, in what order, on what channels), the demo structure, the discovery call structure, the pricing and discount framework, and the competitive differentiation script.

---

### Infrastructure Scalability (4 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| IS1 | System architecture can handle 10x current volume without an architectural rebuild | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| IS2 | Database is structured to scale (not a single Postgres instance with no read replicas) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| IS3 | Cloud infrastructure is auto-scalable (not fixed instance sizing) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| IS4 | Security: penetration test or security audit completed in last 12 months | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |

**On IS1:** The most painful scaling failures happen when a startup's technology cannot handle the increased load that growth brings. A system built for 100 users that breaks at 1,000 users means your growth causes customer experience failures exactly when you need customers to be most delighted. Before scaling, load-test your infrastructure to 10x current peak load.

### Finance and Governance Readiness (4 items)

| # | Checklist Item | Colour | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| FG1 | CFO or senior finance hire made (or clear plan to hire within 3 months of raise) | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| FG2 | Monthly management accounts produced within 15 days of month-end | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |
| FG3 | Board reporting deck produced monthly/quarterly with key metrics | 🟡 Amber | Yes / No |
| FG4 | Budget for the scaling period built with department-level detail | 🔴 Red | Yes / No |

**Scale Readiness Score:**

```
Maximum score:        35 points
Red items No:         ___
Amber items No:       ___

Go/No-Go:
If > 3 Red items = No → NOT ready to scale (systemic gaps)
If 2-3 Red items No → Develop a specific plan to fix each within 60 days,
                      then reassess
If 0-1 Red items No AND < 5 Amber items No → Ready to scale
If 0 Red items No AND < 3 Amber items No → Strongly ready; go aggressive
```

## The Most Common Checklist Failures (and What to Do)

### Failure 1: Launching without a tested pricing hypothesis

**What this looks like:** "We will figure out pricing after launch."

**The consequence:** You acquire users at the wrong price. Either you price too low and the customers you attract are price-sensitive and hard to upgrade, or you price too high and acquisition grinds to a halt. Repricing existing customers is extremely difficult without churn.

**The fix:** Before launch, do 20 willingness-to-pay conversations. Ask specifically: "If this product did X, Y, and Z, would you pay ₹A/month? What about ₹B/month?" Build your launch pricing on this data.

### Failure 2: Starting to raise without a clean cap table

**What this looks like:** Co-founder left 18 months ago and still holds 25% equity with no vesting schedule. Advisor holds 5% on a verbal promise with no documentation.

**The consequence:** Investors will not close a round without a clean cap table. You will spend 6-12 weeks (and significant legal fees) cleaning up the cap table after the investor asks for it. In many cases, deals fall through because the cap table cannot be cleaned fast enough.

**The fix:** Address cap table issues immediately after DPIIT recognition, not when you are fundraising. Departed co-founders should sign a buyback or vesting cessation agreement. Advisors should have signed advisory agreements with clear equity terms.

### Failure 3: Attempting to scale without a sales playbook

**What this looks like:** A founder closes the first 15 customers through personal hustle, intuition, and network. Then hires 3 sales people. The 3 sales people do not perform because they cannot replicate what the founder does intuitively.

**The consequence:** High sales team turnover, wasted hiring budget, delayed revenue targets. The funding round that was supposed to drive 5x growth produces 1.5x growth with much higher burn.

**The fix:** Before hiring the first dedicated salesperson, spend 2-4 weeks documenting exactly what you do. Record your demo calls. Transcribe your discovery call questions. Write down every objection and how you handle it. Document the pricing structure and when you give discounts. This is the seed of your sales playbook.

## Summary: The Three Transitions

| Transition | Key Gate | If You Fail the Gate |
|---|---|---|
| Launch | Core product works + ICP defined + 6 months runway | Do not launch publicly; keep testing in beta |
| Raise | Traction threshold met + clean cap table + financial model built | Extend runway, hit traction threshold, then raise |
| Scale | PMF validated + unit economics positive + first-layer management | Fix PMF or unit economics; do not scale a broken model |

The hardest discipline in startups is not executing on a good idea. It is knowing when you are not yet ready for the next stage — and having the honesty to stay focused on fixing the gaps instead of charging forward.
