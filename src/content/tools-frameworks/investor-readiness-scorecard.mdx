## The Direct Answer

To assess investor readiness, score your startup across 12 dimensions that VCs actually evaluate: problem-solution fit, market size, product traction, unit economics, team strength, competitive moat, go-to-market strategy, financial model, valuation expectations, legal compliance, data room readiness, and pitch quality. Rate each dimension from 1 to 5. A total score of 48 or above out of 60 means you are ready to start fundraising. Below 36, you need focused work before approaching investors.

---

## Why This Scorecard Matters: The Reality of Fundraising in India

Here is the uncomfortable truth about raising venture capital in India.

Of the roughly 80,000 to 90,000 startups registered in India every year, fewer than 1,500 receive any form of institutional funding. That is less than 2%. Of those that receive seed funding, only about 20% go on to raise a Series A. The funnel gets narrower at every stage.

Consider these numbers:

- **Average time to close a funding round in India:** 4 to 8 months from first pitch to money in the bank. For first-time founders without warm introductions, it stretches to 9 to 12 months.
- **Pitch-to-term-sheet conversion rate:** Top-tier VCs like Sequoia Capital India (now Peak XV Partners), Accel, and Matrix Partners (now Z47) see 3,000 to 5,000 pitches per year. They invest in 15 to 25 companies. That is a conversion rate of 0.5% to 0.8%.
- **Top reason startups fail in fundraising:** It is not a bad product. It is approaching investors too early, before the startup can demonstrate enough substance across the dimensions investors care about.
- **Cost of premature fundraising:** Founders who start pitching before they are ready burn through their best investor leads. VCs talk to each other. A weak first impression spreads across the ecosystem. You rarely get a second chance with the same fund.

The Indian startup ecosystem deployed approximately $8 to $10 billion in venture funding in 2025, a recovery from the 2023 correction but still selective. Investors have shifted from growth-at-all-costs to sustainable unit economics, clear paths to profitability, and founders who demonstrate capital efficiency.

This scorecard exists for one reason: to help you avoid the single most expensive mistake in a startup's life, which is pitching before you are ready. Every week you spend improving your weakest dimensions before fundraising reduces the total time to close your round and increases the amount you raise at better terms.

**This is not a theoretical framework.** The 12 dimensions below are drawn from actual term sheet evaluation criteria used by institutional investors in India. Score yourself honestly. Then fix what needs fixing.

## How to Use This Scorecard

**Step 1:** Read each dimension carefully. Understand what investors are actually looking for.

**Step 2:** Score yourself on each dimension from 1 (not addressed) to 5 (exceptional). Be brutally honest. Investors will be.

**Step 3:** Add up your total score out of 60.

**Step 4:** Check the verdict table to understand where you stand.

**Step 5:** Identify your 3 weakest dimensions and follow the improvement guidance.

**Tip for co-founders:** Have each co-founder score independently, then compare. Where you disagree reveals blind spots.

## The 12 Dimensions of Investor Readiness

### Dimension 1: Problem-Solution Fit

**What it is:** The foundation of everything. Does your startup solve a real, painful, frequent problem for a clearly defined set of people? And does your solution actually work?

**What investors look for:**

- The problem is specific, not vague. "Small businesses struggle with accounting" is vague. "Retail shop owners in Tier 2 cities manually track inventory across 3 notebooks and lose 8-12% of stock to mismanagement" is specific.
- The founder has personal or deep professional experience with the problem.
- You can articulate who has this problem, how often they face it, and what they currently do about it (the existing alternative, even if it is Excel or pen-and-paper).
- Early users or customers confirm the problem exists and your solution addresses it.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | You have a solution idea but have not validated the problem with real users |
| 2 | You have spoken to 10-20 potential users and some confirm the problem exists |
| 3 | You have 50+ problem validation interviews; users are actively looking for solutions |
| 4 | You have users who switched from an existing solution to yours and report clear improvement |
| 5 | Users pull the product from you (organic sign-ups, word-of-mouth growth) and express that going back to the old way is unthinkable |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- Founder cannot explain the problem without mentioning their solution.
- The problem is real but "nice to have," not "must solve." Investors call this a vitamin versus a painkiller. They fund painkillers.
- The founder has never spoken to end users, only to friends and family who say "great idea."
- The problem requires educating the market about why it is a problem. Market education is expensive and slow.

**How to improve your score:**

Conduct 30 more customer discovery interviews this month. Do not pitch your solution. Ask open-ended questions: "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [problem]. What did you do? What was frustrating? How much time or money did it cost you?" Record the answers. Look for patterns. The best problem-solution fit evidence is a spreadsheet of quotes from real users describing their pain.

### Dimension 2: Market Size and TAM

**What it is:** Is the market large enough to build a venture-scale business? Investors need to believe this can become a 500+ crore revenue company to justify the risk.

**What investors look for:**

- A bottom-up TAM calculation, not a top-down one. Saying "the Indian education market is $100 billion" means nothing. Saying "there are 400,000 coaching institutes in India, our product costs Rs 2,000 per month per institute, so our serviceable addressable market is Rs 960 crore per year" means something.
- The market is growing, ideally at 15%+ annually.
- There are clear tailwinds: regulatory changes, technology adoption curves, demographic shifts, or behavioral changes that expand the market.
- The founder can articulate a credible path from their beachhead market to a larger market over 5 to 7 years.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | You have not calculated market size or only have top-down numbers from Google searches |
| 2 | You have a rough bottom-up estimate but cannot defend the assumptions |
| 3 | You have a solid bottom-up TAM with clear assumptions, and the SAM is at least Rs 500 crore |
| 4 | Bottom-up TAM is well-researched, SAM exceeds Rs 1,000 crore, and you can demonstrate market growth with data |
| 5 | You have a detailed market map showing beachhead, expansion segments, and adjacent opportunities. Market is large, growing, and you have evidence of underserved demand |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- "It is a trillion-dollar market" with no bottom-up math. Every investor has heard this. It signals lazy thinking.
- The market is large but declining or commoditized.
- Your addressable segment is actually tiny, even if the overall market is large. A niche within a niche within a niche may not support venture returns.
- You are creating a new category with no existing spending. Category creation requires massive capital and patience that most VCs do not have at seed stage.

**How to improve your score:**

Build a bottom-up TAM model in a spreadsheet. Start with the number of potential customers you can count. Multiply by realistic annual revenue per customer. That is your beachhead SAM. Then map out 2 to 3 expansion segments you could enter in years 3 to 5. Validate your assumptions by talking to 5 industry experts. ### Dimension 3: Product and Traction

**What it is:** Do real people actually use your product? Do they pay for it? Do they come back? Traction is the strongest signal that your startup is solving a real problem.

**What investors look for:**

At different stages, they look for different things:

- **Pre-seed / Angel:** A working MVP with at least 50 to 200 active users. Evidence that people find value. Waitlists, pilot programs, or LOIs (Letters of Intent) from potential customers.
- **Seed:** Consistent month-over-month growth in a key metric (users, revenue, transactions). Typically 10-30% MoM growth for at least 3 to 4 months. Retention data showing users come back.
- **Series A:** Product-market fit demonstrated through strong retention curves, clear usage patterns, and revenue traction usually in the range of Rs 50 lakh to Rs 2 crore ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) for SaaS. For consumer businesses, DAU/MAU ratios above 20%.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | You have an idea or a prototype but no real users |
| 2 | You have an MVP with under 50 users; usage is inconsistent |
| 3 | You have 200+ active users or 10+ paying customers; some organic growth |
| 4 | Strong month-over-month growth (15%+ MoM) for 3+ months; clear retention; paying customers |
| 5 | Product-market fit signals are strong: high retention, organic growth, low churn, expanding usage per customer, and a clear path to scale |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- No live product. Just a pitch deck and a Figma prototype. For most Indian VCs at seed stage, you need a working product.
- High sign-ups but zero engagement. Vanity metrics are the fastest way to lose investor trust.
- All users are friends, family, or acquired through paid channels with no organic growth.
- The product works but only because the founder is personally onboarding and hand-holding every user. This does not scale.

**How to improve your score:**

Launch. Ship the simplest version of your product that delivers the core value. Get it into the hands of 100 real users within the next 4 weeks. Measure weekly active usage, not just sign-ups. Track your Day 1, Day 7, and Day 30 retention rates. If retention is below 40% at Day 7, your product needs work before you fundraise.

### Dimension 4: Revenue and Unit Economics

**What it is:** Do the numbers work at a unit level? Can you make money on each customer after accounting for the cost of acquiring and serving them?

**What investors look for:**

- **LTV:CAC ratio** of at least 3:1. Lifetime value of a customer should be 3 times what it costs to acquire them.
- **Gross margins** above 50% for SaaS, above 30% for marketplace businesses, above 15% for e-commerce or D2C.
- **Payback period** under 12 months. How quickly do you recover your customer acquisition cost?
- Evidence that unit economics improve with scale (declining CAC, expanding LTV through upsells, lower per-unit costs).

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No revenue; no pricing model defined |
| 2 | Some revenue but unit economics are negative or unknown |
| 3 | Revenue is growing; unit economics are roughly break-even at a gross margin level |
| 4 | Positive unit economics with LTV:CAC above 3:1; clear path to improving margins |
| 5 | Strong, proven unit economics across multiple customer segments; healthy margins; short payback period; economics improve with scale |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- "We will figure out monetization later." This was acceptable in 2015. It is not acceptable now.
- CAC is high and increasing. This suggests you are chasing low-quality customers or operating in a crowded market.
- Revenue is growing but only through heavy discounting. This is artificial and unsustainable.
- The business model requires scale that is 3 to 5 years away before unit economics turn positive. Most VCs in India have a 7-year fund life. They need returns within that window.

**How to improve your score:**

If you do not have pricing yet, implement pricing this month. Even if it is low. Even if it is "freemium with a paid tier." Get any amount of revenue flowing so you can measure unit economics. Build a simple model: what does it cost you to acquire one customer (ads, sales time, content), and how much revenue does that customer generate over their lifetime with you? ### Dimension 5: Team and Founders

**What it is:** Do you have the right people to execute on this opportunity? This is often the single most important dimension at pre-seed and seed stage. Investors bet on teams when traction is still early.

**What investors look for:**

- **Founder-market fit:** Why are you the right person to solve this problem? Domain expertise, lived experience, or a unique insight that outsiders do not have.
- **Complementary co-founders:** The ideal early team has a builder (CTO/technical) and a seller (CEO/business). A solo non-technical founder building a tech startup is a red flag.
- **Skin in the game:** Have the founders quit their jobs? Are they full-time? Have they invested their own money?
- **Execution velocity:** How fast have you moved from idea to product to first customers? Speed is a signal of founder quality.
- **Resilience indicators:** Previous startup experience (even failed ones), evidence of grit, ability to recruit talent despite limited resources.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Solo founder, part-time, no domain expertise, no technical capability |
| 2 | Small team but missing key skills (no tech co-founder, or no business co-founder); part-time commitment |
| 3 | Full-time founding team with complementary skills; some relevant experience |
| 4 | Strong founding team with deep domain expertise, technical capability, and evidence of fast execution |
| 5 | Exceptional team: deep domain expertise, prior startup or high-growth experience, strong network in the industry, and evidence of ability to recruit top talent |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- Founders are not full-time. This is non-negotiable for almost every institutional investor.
- Equal equity split among 4+ co-founders with no clear CEO. Investors see this as a governance risk and a sign that hard conversations have been avoided.
- The founding team has been together for less than 3 months. Investors worry about co-founder breakups.
- No technical capability on the founding team for a technology product. Outsourcing your core product development is risky and expensive.
- Founders who cannot articulate why they are uniquely positioned to win in this market.

**How to improve your score:**

If you are a solo founder, find a co-founder or at least a committed early team member with complementary skills. Strengthen your founder-market fit narrative: write a clear, specific story about why this problem matters to you personally. If you lack domain expertise, spend 3 months immersing yourself in the industry. Interview 50 practitioners. Visit 20 potential customers. Build the knowledge that makes investors believe you see things others do not.

### Dimension 6: Competitive Moat

**What it is:** What prevents a well-funded competitor, a large incumbent, or a copycat from replicating what you have built? Why will you win, not just today, but over 5 to 10 years?

**What investors look for:**

- **Network effects:** Does the product get better as more people use it? Think of how Dunzo became more useful as more delivery partners joined, or how Swiggy improves with more restaurants and customers.
- **Proprietary data:** Are you accumulating data that improves your product and is difficult for competitors to replicate?
- **Switching costs:** Is it painful for customers to switch away from you? Enterprise SaaS products with deep integrations have high switching costs.
- **Brand and trust:** In some categories (fintech, healthtech), being the trusted brand is a durable moat in India.
- **Regulatory or distribution advantages:** Licenses, exclusive partnerships, or access to distribution channels that are hard to replicate.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No moat. Anyone could build this in 2 months |
| 2 | Minor differentiation through features, but easily replicable |
| 3 | One meaningful competitive advantage (proprietary data, early brand, or key partnerships) |
| 4 | Two or more defensible moats that compound over time |
| 5 | Deep, compounding moats: strong network effects, proprietary data advantages, high switching costs, and brand trust that would take competitors years and significant capital to replicate |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- "We have no competition." Every investor knows this is either naive or dishonest. You always have competition, even if it is the status quo (Excel, WhatsApp groups, pen and paper).
- Your only moat is "we were first." First-mover advantage is a myth in most markets. Execution speed matters more.
- Large incumbents (Reliance, Tata, a major bank) could replicate your product as a feature within their existing platform.
- You are competing on price alone. That is a race to the bottom, not a moat.

**How to improve your score:**

Map your competitive landscape honestly. List every direct competitor, indirect competitor, and existing alternative. Then identify what you can build over the next 12 to 18 months that creates a defensible advantage. Data moats are often the most accessible for early-stage startups: every customer interaction generates data that makes your product smarter and harder to replicate.

### Dimension 7: Go-to-Market Strategy

**What it is:** Can you acquire customers repeatedly, predictably, and profitably? A great product with no distribution is a hobby, not a business.

**What investors look for:**

- A clear, tested customer acquisition channel that works today. Not 10 channels you plan to try. One or two that you have proven.
- Understanding of your ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) in specific, actionable terms.
- A sales cycle that is well understood: how long it takes, who the decision makers are, what the objections are, and how you overcome them.
- Evidence that your GTM can scale. If your only channel is the founder doing direct sales, that is fine at Rs 10 lakh MRR. It is not a plan for Rs 1 crore MRR.
- For B2B startups in India: understanding of the enterprise sales cycle, which often takes 6 to 12 months for larger companies.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No go-to-market strategy beyond "we will use social media and word of mouth" |
| 2 | A defined strategy on paper but no execution or results yet |
| 3 | One working acquisition channel with measurable results and defined ICP |
| 4 | Two or more proven channels, a repeatable sales process, and a clear plan to scale |
| 5 | Highly efficient, multi-channel GTM engine with proven scalability, strong conversion rates, and decreasing CAC over time |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- "Our product is so good it will sell itself." No product sells itself. Ever.
- Entirely dependent on paid acquisition with no organic or content-driven channels.
- No understanding of the sales cycle for your target customer. B2B founders who say "we will close enterprise deals in 2 weeks" have never sold to enterprises.
- The GTM requires partnerships that have not been secured yet. Partnership-dependent go-to-market is the riskiest strategy for a startup.

**How to improve your score:**

Pick one acquisition channel. The one where your target customers already spend time and attention. Execute on it for 30 days. Measure the results. For B2B SaaS in India, LinkedIn outreach combined with content marketing often works well. For consumer apps, community-driven growth on Instagram or WhatsApp. For D2C, performance marketing on Meta with strong creative. Test, measure, iterate. Document what works.

### Dimension 8: Financial Model

**What it is:** Are your financial projections credible? Can you explain the assumptions behind every number? A financial model is not a forecast of what will happen. It is a framework for how you think about your business.

**What investors look for:**

- A 3 to 5-year model built from the bottom up, not a top-down revenue projection.
- Clear, stated assumptions for each line item (customer growth rate, pricing, churn, cost structure).
- Sensitivity analysis: what happens if your growth rate is 50% lower than projected? Do you still have a viable business?
- Evidence that you understand your cost structure: fixed vs. variable costs, burn rate, runway calculation.
- A realistic path to breakeven or profitability, even if it is 4 to 5 years out.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No financial model exists |
| 2 | A basic spreadsheet with revenue projections but no cost analysis or stated assumptions |
| 3 | A complete P&L model with revenue, costs, and stated assumptions. Projections are reasonable |
| 4 | A detailed model with multiple scenarios, sensitivity analysis, and a clear understanding of key business drivers |
| 5 | A sophisticated financial model with cohort-based revenue projections, detailed unit economics, scenario analysis, cash flow forecasting, and clear link between operating metrics and financial outcomes |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- The "hockey stick" projection with no explanation of what changes in month 7 to cause exponential growth.
- Revenue projections that assume 100% market capture or unrealistic growth rates (100x in 2 years for a B2B product).
- No understanding of burn rate or runway. If you do not know how many months of cash you have left, you are not ready for a serious investor conversation.
- The model has never been updated since it was first created. A financial model should be a living document.

**How to improve your score:**

Build or rebuild your financial model from scratch. Start with your current monthly numbers. Project forward using conservative assumptions for growth. Add all costs: salaries, infrastructure, marketing, operations. Calculate your monthly burn rate and runway. Then create a "base case" and an "optimistic case." Investors want to see that you can build a valuable business even in the base case.

### Dimension 9: Valuation Expectation

**What it is:** Is your valuation expectation reasonable for your stage, traction, and market? Unrealistic valuation expectations are the number one reason term sheet negotiations collapse in India.

**What investors look for:**

- A valuation grounded in comparable transactions, not wishful thinking. What did similar startups at your stage raise at?
- Willingness to be flexible. The best founders focus on getting the right investor on their cap table, not on maximizing valuation at seed stage.
- Understanding of the tradeoff between dilution and strategic value. Taking money from Peak XV or Accel at a lower valuation may be worth more than taking money from an unknown angel at a higher valuation.
- Fair valuation that gives investors enough upside to justify the risk.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No idea what your startup is worth; or wildly unrealistic expectations (Rs 100 crore pre-revenue valuation) |
| 2 | A rough idea based on what you read about other startups, but no structured analysis |
| 3 | A reasonable valuation range based on stage-appropriate multiples and comparable transactions |
| 4 | A well-researched valuation supported by multiple methods (DCF, comparables, scorecard method) with flexibility for negotiation |
| 5 | A sophisticated valuation framework that accounts for stage, traction, market, team, and competitive dynamics, with data-backed justification and clear negotiation parameters |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- "We want Rs 50 crore valuation" for a pre-revenue startup with no traction. This tells investors you have not done your homework.
- Anchoring on a valuation because "my friend's startup got this valuation." Every startup is different.
- Unwillingness to negotiate. This signals ego over pragmatism.
- Valuation expectations based on the amount of money you want to raise and the dilution you are comfortable with, rather than on what the business is actually worth.

**How to improve your score:**

[Try our free valuation engine](/valuation) to get a data-backed estimate of your startup's current value. Research 10 to 15 comparable funding rounds in your sector and stage in India. Use platforms like Tracxn, Crunchbase, or Inc42 to find this data. Build a valuation range (low, mid, high) and understand the assumptions behind each. Go into investor conversations with a range, not a single number.

### Dimension 10: Legal and Compliance

**What it is:** Is your company properly structured for investment? Are there any legal or regulatory issues that would surface during due diligence and kill the deal?

**What investors look for:**

- **Company incorporation:** Private Limited company registered with MCA. If you are still an LLP, sole proprietorship, or partnership, you need to convert before raising institutional capital.
- **Clean cap table:** Clear ownership structure. Shares properly issued and documented. No ambiguity about who owns what.
- **Founder agreements:** Signed co-founder agreement with vesting schedules in place. This is critical. Without vesting, investors worry that a co-founder could leave with 40% of the company.
- **IP assignment:** All intellectual property (code, designs, patents) is owned by the company, not by individual founders or contractors.
- **Employment agreements and NDAs:** Key team members have signed agreements.
- **Regulatory compliance:** GST registration, sector-specific licenses (RBI for fintech, FSSAI for food, DPIIT registration for Startup India benefits).
- **No pending litigation or disputes.**

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Not yet incorporated or operating as a sole proprietorship / partnership |
| 2 | Private Limited company incorporated but missing key documents (no founder agreement, no IP assignment, messy cap table) |
| 3 | Properly incorporated with founder agreements and clean cap table; some regulatory compliance in place |
| 4 | All legal documents in order; IP assigned to company; vesting schedules in place; regulatory compliance complete |
| 5 | Fully compliant with all legal requirements; DPIIT registered; clean cap table with proper ESOP pool; all agreements executed; legal counsel retained; ready for investor due diligence |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- Company is not a Private Limited company. This is a non-starter for institutional investors in India.
- Cap table disputes or unclear ownership. If founders are fighting over equity, investors will walk away immediately.
- IP belongs to a contractor or a previous employer, not to the company. This is more common than you think, especially when founders build the first version while still employed elsewhere.
- Pending legal cases or regulatory violations.
- No founder vesting. If a co-founder with 30% equity leaves after 6 months, the remaining team is stuck with dead equity on their cap table.

**How to improve your score:**

If you are not yet a Private Limited company, convert immediately. Engage a startup-focused legal firm (firms like Algo Legal, LawSikho, or Vakilsearch offer affordable packages for early-stage startups). Get your co-founder agreement signed with 4-year vesting and 1-year cliff. Assign all IP to the company in writing. Register for GST and DPIIT Startup India recognition. These are table-stakes requirements, not optional.

### Dimension 11: Data Room Readiness

**What it is:** When an investor says "send me your data room," can you share a well-organized folder of documents within 24 hours? Speed and organization in the data room signal operational maturity.

**What investors look for:**

A complete data room typically includes:

**Company documents:**
- Certificate of Incorporation
- Memorandum and Articles of Association
- Board resolutions and shareholder agreements
- Cap table with full ownership breakdown

**Financial documents:**
- Audited financial statements (if available)
- Monthly P&L and balance sheet for the last 12 months
- Bank statements
- Tax returns (ITR, GST returns)

**Business documents:**
- Pitch deck (15 to 20 slides)
- One-page executive summary
- Financial model with projections
- Product demo or walkthrough video
- Key customer contracts or LOIs
- Team bios and LinkedIn profiles

**Legal documents:**
- Founder agreements with vesting details
- IP assignment agreements
- Employee agreements
- Any previous investment agreements (convertible notes, SAFEs)
- Trademark registrations

**Metrics and traction:**
- Dashboard or export showing key metrics (MRR, growth rate, retention, CAC, LTV)
- Customer testimonials or case studies
- Competitive analysis

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | You have a pitch deck and nothing else |
| 2 | You have some documents but they are scattered across email, Google Drive, and WhatsApp |
| 3 | You have most required documents organized in a shared folder; some gaps remain |
| 4 | A complete, well-organized data room that covers all sections above; can be shared within 24 hours |
| 5 | A comprehensive, professionally organized data room with clear folder structure, updated documents, version control, and additional materials (customer case studies, product roadmap, competitive analysis) ready for immediate sharing |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- Taking more than a week to share basic documents after an investor requests them. This signals disorganization and kills momentum.
- Financial documents that contradict what was presented in the pitch. If your deck says Rs 8 lakh MRR but your bank statements show Rs 3 lakh, the deal is dead and your reputation is damaged.
- Missing co-founder agreements or unclear equity splits.
- No financial model. Investors will build their own model if they have to, and they will be more conservative than you.

**How to improve your score:**

Create a Google Drive or Notion folder with the structure above. Spend one weekend populating it. For the documents you do not have, create them. Most of these are standard templates: co-founder agreements, IP assignment letters, employee NDAs. The financial model will take longer but is worth the investment. Update this data room monthly. When an investor asks for it, you should be able to share a link within an hour.

### Dimension 12: Pitch Quality

**What it is:** Can you tell the story of your startup in a way that is clear, compelling, and concise? Can you explain the problem, the solution, the market, the traction, the team, and the ask in 3 minutes? In 15 minutes? In a 45-minute meeting?

**What investors look for:**

- **Clarity:** The investor understands what you do within the first 60 seconds. No jargon. No "AI-powered blockchain-enabled SaaS platform for synergistic value creation."
- **Narrative arc:** Problem, solution, why now, traction, team, vision, ask. In that order.
- **Data-driven storytelling:** Every claim is backed by a number. "We are growing fast" means nothing. "We grew from Rs 2 lakh to Rs 12 lakh MRR in 5 months with zero marketing spend" means everything.
- **Founder energy and conviction:** Investors invest in people. Your passion, clarity of thought, and ability to communicate under pressure matter enormously.
- **Handling of tough questions:** How do you respond when challenged on your assumptions, competition, or risks? Defensiveness is a red flag. Thoughtful, honest responses build trust.

**Scoring rubric:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | No pitch deck; cannot clearly explain the business in under 5 minutes |
| 2 | Basic pitch deck exists but the narrative is unclear or unfocused; founder struggles with Q&A |
| 3 | Solid pitch deck and a clear 5-minute pitch; can handle basic investor questions |
| 4 | Compelling narrative with strong data support; pitch is well-rehearsed; handles tough questions with confidence and honesty |
| 5 | Exceptional storytelling: the pitch creates urgency, the data is irrefutable, the founder is magnetic. Q&A reveals deep understanding and intellectual honesty. Investors leave the meeting wanting to invest |

**Red flags that kill deals:**

- A 60-slide pitch deck. Your deck should be 12 to 15 slides. Maximum 20 if you have dense metrics to share.
- Reading from the slides. If you cannot pitch without your deck, you do not know your business well enough.
- Overselling or exaggerating. Investors do extensive due diligence. Any inflated metric will be caught and will destroy trust.
- Not knowing your numbers. If an investor asks "what is your month-over-month growth rate?" and you need to check a spreadsheet, you are not ready.
- Avoiding the question of risks. Every business has risks. Investors respect founders who acknowledge and address risks honestly.

**How to improve your score:**

Practice your pitch 30 times. Not 3. Not 10. Thirty. Pitch to friends who will give honest feedback. Pitch to other founders. Pitch to mentors. ## The Playbook: Fix Your Weakest Dimensions First

Now that you have your score, here is exactly how to use it.

**Step 1: Identify your 3 lowest-scoring dimensions.**

Write them down. These are your priority workstreams for the next 30 to 90 days.

**Step 2: Fix them in this order.**

Not all dimensions are equally important to fix first. Here is the priority sequence:

1. **Problem-Solution Fit and Product Traction** come first. Without a real problem and a working product that people use, nothing else matters. No amount of legal preparation or pitch polish will save a startup that has not validated its core value proposition.

2. **Team and Legal/Compliance** come second. If you need a co-founder, find one now. If your company is not properly structured, fix it now. These take time and cannot be rushed.

3. **Revenue/Unit Economics, Market Size, and Go-to-Market** come third. These require execution over weeks and months: running pricing experiments, documenting your CAC, building your bottom-up TAM model.

4. **Financial Model, Valuation, Data Room, and Pitch** come last. These are the packaging. They matter, but they should be built on the foundation of the first three groups.

**Step 3: Set a 30-day sprint for each weak area.**

Define what a 1-point improvement would look like for each of your weak dimensions. That is your monthly target. Example: if you scored 2 on Revenue and Unit Economics, a 1-point improvement means going from "some revenue but unit economics are negative or unknown" to "revenue is growing; unit economics are roughly break-even." What would it take to get there in 30 days? Write down the 3 to 5 specific actions.

**Step 4: Re-score monthly.**

Every month, go through the full scorecard again. Track your progress over time. When you hit 48, start fundraising. Not before.

## Practitioner Insight: What Valuers and Deal Advisors See Founders Get Wrong

Having worked with hundreds of founders preparing for fundraising, here are the patterns that separate founders who raise successfully from those who do not.

**Mistake 1: Pitching too broadly.**

Founders spray-and-pray, emailing 200 VCs with a generic pitch deck. This almost never works. The founders who succeed research 20 to 30 investors who have invested in their sector, stage, and geography. They seek warm introductions. They customize their narrative for each investor. Quality of outreach matters infinitely more than quantity.

**Mistake 2: Overvaluing the idea, undervaluing execution evidence.**

First-time founders believe their idea is worth Rs 20 crore. Experienced investors know that ideas are worth nothing. Execution is everything. A founder with Rs 5 lakh MRR, 35% month-over-month growth, and a clear repeatable acquisition channel is worth 10 times more than a founder with a brilliant idea and a polished deck.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring the legal and structural basics.**

This is the most preventable mistake and the most common one. We see startups with genuine traction lose investor interest because they are still operating as a partnership firm, have no founder vesting agreements, or have IP ownership ambiguity. These are fixable in 2 to 4 weeks. Fix them before you start fundraising.

**Mistake 4: Building the pitch deck before building the business.**

The pitch deck should be the last thing you create, not the first. Build the product. Get users. Generate revenue. Understand your unit economics. Then create a deck that presents what you have already accomplished. Investors fund traction, not slides.

**Mistake 5: Not understanding what stage-appropriate fundraising looks like.**

A pre-seed startup should not be pitching Series A investors. An angel-stage startup should not be building a 60-page data room. Match your fundraising approach to your stage:

- **Pre-seed (Rs 25 lakh to Rs 1 crore):** Angel investors, accelerators, friends and family. You need a strong team, a validated problem, and an early product.
- **Seed (Rs 1 crore to Rs 10 crore):** Micro-VCs, angel syndicates, early-stage funds. You need product-market fit signals, early revenue, and a clear GTM.
- **Series A (Rs 10 crore to Rs 50 crore):** Institutional VCs. You need proven product-market fit, Rs 50 lakh to Rs 2 crore ARR, strong retention, and a scalable GTM engine.

**Mistake 6: Treating valuation as the primary metric of success.**

We see founders celebrate a high valuation without understanding that a high valuation at seed stage sets a bar that is extremely difficult to clear at Series A. If you raise at Rs 40 crore pre-money with Rs 5 lakh MRR, you need to show 10 to 15x growth before Series A investors will consider you at a markup. Raise at a fair valuation that gives you room to grow into. [Use our valuation engine](/valuation) to benchmark what is reasonable for your stage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What score do I need before approaching VCs?

Aim for 48 or above before approaching institutional VCs (funds like Peak XV, Accel, Elevation Capital, or Z47). For angel investors or micro-VCs, a score of 40 to 45 is often sufficient, as they typically invest earlier and have higher risk tolerance. Below 36, you will waste time and burn investor relationships by pitching prematurely. The exception: if you have an exceptionally strong team score (5 out of 5) with deep domain expertise and prior exits, some investors will back you earlier on the strength of the team alone.

### Should I raise from angels first or go directly to VCs?

For most first-time founders in India, raising a small angel round (Rs 25 lakh to Rs 1 crore) before approaching VCs is the highest-probability path. Angel money gives you runway to improve your traction scores without the pressure of VC-level expectations. Use the angel round to get from score 30 to score 45. Then approach VCs with a stronger hand. The best angel investors in India also provide warm introductions to institutional investors, which dramatically improves your conversion rate. Consider angel syndicates like Indian Angel Network, Mumbai Angels, or platforms like LetsVenture and AngelList India.

### What if my unit economics are not proven yet?

It depends on your stage. At pre-seed, investors do not expect proven unit economics. They expect you to have a thesis about how the economics will work and early signals that support it. At seed stage, you should have at least directional evidence: early revenue, a pricing model that customers accept, and a rough sense of what it costs to acquire a customer. By Series A, positive unit economics are essentially mandatory in the current Indian fundraising environment. If your unit economics are not yet proven, focus on getting to a small scale (50 to 100 paying customers) where you can measure real CAC, LTV, and retention before fundraising.

### How long does fundraising typically take in India?

Plan for 4 to 8 months from your first investor meeting to money in the bank. Here is the typical timeline: 4 to 6 weeks of investor outreach and first meetings, 2 to 4 weeks of follow-up meetings and due diligence, 2 to 3 weeks for term sheet negotiation, and 4 to 8 weeks for legal documentation and closing. The biggest variable is how long it takes to get first meetings. Warm introductions shorten this to days. Cold outreach can take months with low response rates. Factor in time for the legal close: SHA (Shareholders Agreement), SSA (Share Subscription Agreement), and regulatory filings with MCA and RBI (if foreign investors are involved).

### What documents do investors typically ask for in India?

The core documents Indian investors request during due diligence include: pitch deck, financial model with projections, monthly MIS (Management Information System) for the last 6 to 12 months, cap table, Certificate of Incorporation and MOA/AOA, co-founder agreement, IP assignment agreements, key customer contracts, GST returns, ITR filings, bank statements, DPIIT Startup India recognition certificate, and any existing investment agreements (SAFEs, convertible notes, or prior round documents). Some investors also request a product demo, reference calls with customers, and background verification of founders. Having these organized in a data room before your first meeting signals professionalism and accelerates the process.

### Should I get a valuation before fundraising?

Yes. Understanding your startup's approximate value before entering investor conversations gives you a significant negotiation advantage. It prevents two common mistakes: asking for too little (leaving money on the table or raising at a valuation that is hard to explain to future investors) and asking for too much (scaring away investors or setting unrealistic expectations). Use multiple valuation methods: comparable transactions (what did similar startups in your space raise at?), revenue multiples (for revenue-generating startups), and the Berkus or Scorecard method (for pre-revenue startups). [Try our free valuation engine](/valuation) to get a data-backed starting point, then adjust based on your specific circumstances.

### What do top Indian VCs specifically look for at seed stage?

While every fund has its own thesis, the most active seed-stage VCs in India generally prioritize these factors in this order: (1) Founding team quality and founder-market fit, which is weighted most heavily when traction is still early. (2) Market size and growth potential, as they need the market to be large enough to support a $100M+ outcome. (3) Early traction and product-market fit signals, even if small in absolute terms, as the trajectory and retention matter more than the absolute numbers. (4) Differentiated insight, meaning the founder sees something about the market or the problem that others have missed. (5) Capital efficiency, especially post-2023, as investors want to see founders who can do more with less.

### Can I fundraise with a score of 35 if one dimension is a 5?

Having a single exceptional dimension (a 5) can compensate for weakness elsewhere, but only in specific cases. If your team score is a 5 (serial entrepreneur with a previous exit, deep domain expertise), investors may overlook weaker traction because they trust the team's ability to execute. If your traction score is a 5 (explosive organic growth, strong retention), investors may overlook a weak financial model because the product clearly works. However, certain low scores are deal-breakers regardless of other strengths: a 1 on Legal and Compliance means the deal cannot close, a 1 on Problem-Solution Fit means the foundation is missing, and a 1 on Team means no institutional investor will proceed. Fix the critical gaps first.

## Key Takeaways

**1. The scorecard is a diagnostic tool, not a judgment.** A low score does not mean your startup is bad. It means you are not yet ready for the specific, demanding process of institutional fundraising. That is fixable.

**2. Time spent improving your score before fundraising saves time during fundraising.** Every month you invest in fixing weak dimensions reduces the time to close your round by 2 to 3 months. The math works in your favor.

**3. The 3 most important dimensions at seed stage are Problem-Solution Fit, Product and Traction, and Team.** If these three scores are all 4 or above, you can compensate for weakness in other areas. If any of these three is below 3, fix them first.

**4. Legal readiness is the most underestimated dimension.** It is also the easiest and fastest to fix. Do not let a missing co-founder agreement or incorrect company structure cost you a term sheet.

**5. Your valuation should be the last thing you optimize, not the first.** Build value first. The valuation will follow.

**6. The fundraising process rewards preparation.** The founders who raise fastest are not the ones with the best ideas. They are the ones who have done the most preparation before their first investor meeting.
