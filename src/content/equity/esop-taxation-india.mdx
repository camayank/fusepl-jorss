# ESOP Taxation in India: From Grant to Exercise to Sale

Your best engineer just vested 50,000 ESOP options worth ₹25 lakh at current valuation. She's excited. She exercises them.

Six months later, she gets her Form 16. There's a perquisite income of ₹20 lakh added to her salary — and she owes ₹6.2 lakh in additional tax. Nobody told her.

She's furious. Not at the government — at you.

This scenario plays out in Indian startups constantly, because ESOP taxation has three separate events that are poorly understood by founders, HR teams, and often even the CA who runs the payroll.

This guide covers every tax event in the ESOP lifecycle: grant, vesting, exercise, and sale. With the exact tax treatment, the rates, the timing, and what founders need to communicate to employees.

---

## The ESOP Lifecycle: 4 Stages, 3 Tax Events

```
GRANT → VESTING → EXERCISE → SALE
  ↓          ↓         ↓         ↓
No tax     No tax   PERQUISITE  CAPITAL
                      TAX       GAINS TAX
```

Let's go through each.

---

## Stage 1: Grant

**What happens:** The company gives the employee a formal ESOP grant letter specifying:
- Number of options granted
- Exercise price (price at which employee can buy shares)
- Vesting schedule
- Expiry date

**Tax treatment: No tax at grant.**

The grant itself creates no tax liability. The employee has a right to buy shares in the future — but hasn't bought anything yet and hasn't received any economic benefit.

**What founders often get wrong:** Thinking the ESOP is "taxed at grant." It isn't. Many founders have told employees their options are "already taxed" when they're not. Don't do this.

**Important:** The exercise price is set at grant and locked in. If your share FMV is ₹100 today and you grant options at ₹100 exercise price, the employee has the right to buy shares at ₹100 regardless of future FMV. If FMV rises to ₹500, the employee buys at ₹100 and the ₹400 difference is the taxable perquisite.

---

## Stage 2: Vesting

**What happens:** Options vest over time per the vesting schedule. Typically 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff.

**Tax treatment: No tax at vesting.**

Vesting simply means the options become exercisable. The employee still hasn't bought anything. No tax event.

**The confusion:** Employees sometimes think "my options vested — I need to pay tax." They don't. Vesting is not a tax event.

---

## Stage 3: Exercise

**What happens:** The employee exercises vested options — pays the exercise price and receives shares.

**Tax treatment: PERQUISITE TAX — this is the big one.**

Under Section 17(2)(vi) of the Income Tax Act, the value of ESOPs is treated as a **perquisite** — a benefit provided by employer to employee. The perquisite value is:

```
Perquisite value per share = FMV on exercise date − Exercise price

Total perquisite = Perquisite value per share × Number of shares exercised
```

This perquisite is added to the employee's salary income and taxed at their **marginal income tax rate**.

**Tax rates at exercise (FY 2025–26):**

| Annual Income (including perquisite) | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹3L | Nil |
| ₹3L–₹7L | 5% |
| ₹7L–₹10L | 10% |
| ₹10L–₹12L | 15% |
| ₹12L–₹15L | 20% |
| Above ₹15L | 30% |

Plus surcharge (if income exceeds ₹50L) and health & education cess of 4%.

**For most startup employees exercising significant ESOPs, the effective tax rate is 30–34.9%.**

### The FMV Problem: How Is FMV Determined?

For listed companies, FMV = market price on exercise date. Simple.

For **unlisted companies** (which all private startups are), the FMV must be determined by a **Category I Merchant Banker** registered with SEBI. The Merchant Banker issues a valuation certificate that becomes the FMV for tax purposes.

**Critical point:** This FMV valuation must be done as of a date not more than 180 days before the exercise date. It cannot be a stale valuation.

**What this means for startups:** You need a fresh Merchant Banker valuation every time employees exercise. Many startups set up quarterly or semi-annual valuation windows precisely to manage this.

### When Is the Tax Actually Paid?

The tax on the perquisite is deducted via **TDS** (Tax Deducted at Source) by the employer at the time of exercise. The amount is deducted from the employee's next salary or via a special deduction, depending on how the company handles it.

**This is a cash flow problem for employees.** An employee might exercise options worth ₹20 lakh, receive shares (not cash), and immediately owe ₹6 lakh in tax which gets deducted from their monthly salary.

The shares are illiquid (can't be sold in a private company). The tax is real and immediate.

**The founder's responsibility:** Communicate this clearly before employees exercise. Run through the exact tax calculation. Nobody should be surprised.

## Stage 4: Sale of Shares

**What happens:** Employee sells the shares (at IPO, acquisition, secondary sale, or buyback).

**Tax treatment: CAPITAL GAINS TAX**

The employee has already paid perquisite tax on the "acquisition" value of the shares. Now they're selling — any additional gain is capital gain.

**Cost of acquisition for capital gains = FMV at exercise date** (the same value that was taxed as perquisite).

```
Capital gain = Sale price − FMV at exercise date

NOT: Sale price − Exercise price
(You'd be double-taxed if it were exercise price)
```

### Short-Term vs Long-Term Capital Gains

**For unlisted company shares (private startups):**

| Holding period | Classification | Tax rate |
|---|---|---|
| ≤ 24 months from exercise | Short-Term Capital Gain (STCG) | 30% (slab rate) |
| > 24 months from exercise | Long-Term Capital Gain (LTCG) | 20% with indexation |

**For listed company shares (post-IPO):**

| Holding period | Classification | Tax rate |
|---|---|---|
| ≤ 12 months from exercise | Short-Term Capital Gain (STCG) | 15% (flat, no indexation) |
| > 12 months from exercise | Long-Term Capital Gain (LTCG) | 10% on gains above ₹1 lakh (no indexation) |

**The IPO timing implication:** If your company IPOs and employees immediately sell shares:
- If they exercised within 12 months of IPO: STCG at 15%
- If they exercised more than 12 months before IPO: LTCG at 10% above ₹1L

**Strategic advice to employees:** If an IPO is anticipated, exercise early (when FMV is lower, reducing the perquisite tax base) and hold for 12+ months post-IPO to qualify for LTCG treatment.

## The Full Tax Calculation: A Worked Example

**Setup:**
- Employee: Senior Engineer, annual CTC ₹24L (₹2L/month gross)
- ESOP grant: 50,000 options at exercise price ₹10 per share
- Exercise date: FMV = ₹60 per share (Merchant Banker certified)
- Number exercised: 20,000 options
- Sale: 18 months after exercise at ₹150 per share (after IPO)

### Step 1: Exercise Tax (Perquisite)

```
Perquisite value = (FMV − Exercise price) × Shares exercised
= (₹60 − ₹10) × 20,000
= ₹50 × 20,000
= ₹10,00,000 (₹10 lakh)
```

This ₹10 lakh is added to annual salary income of ₹24L = ₹34L total taxable income.

**Tax on ₹34L (New Tax Regime, FY25-26):**
- Up to ₹3L: Nil
- ₹3L–₹7L: 5% = ₹20,000
- ₹7L–₹10L: 10% = ₹30,000
- ₹10L–₹12L: 15% = ₹30,000
- ₹12L–₹15L: 20% = ₹60,000
- ₹15L–₹34L: 30% = ₹5,70,000
- **Total tax: ₹7,10,000**
- Add 4% cess: ₹28,400
- **Total: ₹7,38,400**

Tax attributable to ESOP perquisite ≈ 30% × ₹10L + cess ≈ **₹3.12 lakh**

This is deducted via TDS from the employee's salary over the remaining months of the financial year.

### Step 2: Sale Tax (Capital Gains)

```
Sale price per share = ₹150
Cost of acquisition = FMV at exercise = ₹60

Capital gain per share = ₹150 − ₹60 = ₹90
Total capital gain = ₹90 × 20,000 = ₹18,00,000 (₹18 lakh)
```

Holding period: 18 months from exercise. Since shares are of a listed company (post-IPO) and held > 12 months: **LTCG at 10% above ₹1L exemption.**

```
LTCG tax = 10% × (₹18L − ₹1L) = 10% × ₹17L = ₹1,70,000
Add 4% cess: ₹6,800
Total LTCG tax: ₹1,76,800
```

### Total Tax Summary

| Tax Event | Gain | Tax Paid |
|---|---|---|
| Exercise perquisite | ₹10L | ₹3.12L |
| Sale capital gain | ₹18L | ₹1.77L |
| **Total** | **₹28L** | **₹4.89L** |
| **Effective tax rate** | | **17.5%** |

**Net proceeds to employee:** ₹28L − ₹4.89L = **₹23.11L on 20,000 shares sold at ₹150**

## The Startup India ESOP Tax Relief

For **DPIIT-recognised startups**, there is a significant ESOP tax relief available under Section 192 of the Income Tax Act (as amended by the Finance Act 2020):

**The relief:** For employees of eligible DPIIT-recognised startups, TDS on ESOP perquisite can be deferred — it doesn't have to be paid at the time of exercise. Instead, TDS is paid at the earliest of:

1. **5 years** from the date of allotment of shares
2. **Date of sale** of shares by the employee
3. **Date the employee ceases to be an employee** of the startup

**Why this is massive:** It solves the "illiquid shares, real tax" problem. Employees don't have to pay tax in cash when they have no cash proceeds.

**Conditions for the deferral:**
- The startup must have DPIIT recognition
- The startup must be incorporated on or after April 1, 2016
- The annual turnover must not exceed ₹100 crore in the financial year of exercise
- The employee must be a resident Indian

**Important:** This is a **deferral**, not an exemption. The tax is still owed — it's just paid later. But for employees in a pre-IPO startup, this is the difference between "I can't afford to exercise" and "I'll pay when I have the money."

**What founders should do:** If you have DPIIT recognition, communicate this benefit to employees explicitly. It may make your ESOP grants significantly more attractive.

## The Buyback Alternative: Tax-Efficient Exit for Employees

Instead of a secondary sale, many Indian startups run **ESOP buyback programs** — the company itself buys back vested, exercised shares from employees at a fair price.

**Tax treatment of buyback:**

Under Section 115QA of the Income Tax Act, the company pays a **Buyback Distribution Tax (BDT) of 20%** on the distributed income (the amount paid above the issue price).

**From the employee's perspective:** The proceeds from a buyback are **exempt from income tax in the hands of the employee** (they're taxed at the company level via BDT).

**This creates a tax efficiency:** The combined tax is 20% at company level rather than up to 30-34% at the employee level. For high-income employees, this is significantly more efficient.

**Why startups do ESOP buybacks:**
1. Provide liquidity to early employees without a full exit
2. Efficient tax treatment (20% vs 30-34%)
3. Increase employee satisfaction and retention
4. Clean up the cap table by removing small ESOP holders before a funding round

## Common ESOP Tax Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake 1: Not Getting a Merchant Banker Valuation Before Exercise

Without a certified Merchant Banker valuation, you have no defensible FMV. The IT department will impute their own FMV (usually higher), resulting in higher perquisite income and a tax demand.

**Fix:** Do a Merchant Banker valuation before any exercise window opens. Typically ₹25,000–₹75,000 for a standard valuation report.

### Mistake 2: Employees Exercising Without Understanding the Tax

Employees who exercise without understanding the perquisite tax get surprised by their Form 16. This creates resentment and a permanent bad impression of ESOPs.

**Fix:** Before every exercise window, send employees a simple tax calculation example: "If you exercise X options at current FMV of ₹Y, your approximate additional tax is ₹Z, deducted from your salary over the following months."

### Mistake 3: Not Telling Employees About the DPIIT Tax Deferral

If you're a DPIIT-recognised startup, employees can defer their ESOP exercise tax for up to 5 years. Many startups with DPIIT recognition never communicate this benefit.

**Fix:** Add the DPIIT deferral option to your ESOP policy document and communicate it explicitly when opening exercise windows.

### Mistake 4: Setting Exercise Price at ₹0 or ₹1

Some founders set the exercise price at ₹0 or ₹1 to make options "free" for employees. This actually increases the perquisite tax burden — because the full FMV is treated as perquisite.

Setting exercise price at current FMV (nil discount) means there is no perquisite tax at exercise — the entire gain is deferred to capital gains at sale (which may be more tax-efficient).

**Fix:** Understand the perquisite tax implications of different exercise prices before setting them in your ESOP plan.

### Mistake 5: Not Tracking the 24-Month LTCG Threshold

For private company shares (unlisted), LTCG treatment requires 24 months holding after exercise. Many employees sell at IPO without checking whether they've crossed the 24-month mark from exercise date.

**Fix:** Track exercise dates for all employees. When an IPO is imminent, identify which employees are at or approaching the 24-month mark and advise them on timing.

## What to Put in Your ESOP Policy Document

Every startup should have a clear ESOP policy document that explains the tax treatment. Minimum required sections:

1. **What an ESOP is** (plain English, not legal jargon)
2. **How the 4-stage lifecycle works** (grant, vesting, exercise, sale)
3. **Tax treatment at each stage** (with examples)
4. **How FMV is determined** (Merchant Banker process)
5. **How TDS is handled** (deducted from salary, timing)
6. **DPIIT deferral benefit** (if applicable)
7. **Exercise windows** (when employees can exercise)
8. **Expiry** (options typically expire 90 days after leaving the company)
9. **What happens at IPO, acquisition, or buyback**
10. **Who to contact with questions** (HR / CFO / CA contact)

A clear ESOP policy document reduces confusion, prevents resentment, and makes your options more attractive to candidates.

## ESOP vs. RSU: The Indian Context

Some larger Indian startups are moving from ESOPs (options) to RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) for senior hires. The tax treatment is different:

| | ESOP (Options) | RSU (Restricted Stock Unit) |
|---|---|---|
| **At grant** | No tax | No tax |
| **At vesting** | No tax | **Perquisite tax on FMV at vesting** |
| **At exercise** | Perquisite tax (FMV − exercise price) | Not applicable (shares directly allotted at vesting) |
| **At sale** | Capital gains (sale price − exercise FMV) | Capital gains (sale price − vesting FMV) |

RSUs are simpler (no exercise mechanics) but create perquisite tax at vesting even though the employee hasn't sold anything. For unlisted companies with illiquid shares, RSUs can create the same "illiquid shares, real tax" problem as ESOPs — so think carefully before using RSUs in a private company.

## Summary: The ESOP Tax Cheat Sheet

| Event | Tax Treatment | Rate | When Paid |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Grant** | No tax | — | — |
| **Vesting** | No tax | — | — |
| **Exercise** | Perquisite (salary income) | 20–34.9% marginal | TDS at exercise (or deferred 5yr for DPIIT startups) |
| **Sale — unlisted, held < 24 months** | STCG | 30% (slab rate) | At time of filing |
| **Sale — unlisted, held > 24 months** | LTCG | 20% with indexation | At time of filing |
| **Sale — listed (post-IPO), held < 12 months** | STCG | 15% flat | At time of filing |
| **Sale — listed (post-IPO), held > 12 months** | LTCG | 10% above ₹1L | At time of filing |
| **Buyback by company** | Exempt for employee | 0% for employee | Company pays 20% BDT |
