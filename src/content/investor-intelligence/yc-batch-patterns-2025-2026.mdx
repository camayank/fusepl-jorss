# Inside YC's Last 3 Batches — The Patterns That Reveal Where Startups Are Heading

There is a room in San Francisco where, twice a year, the future of technology gets a dress rehearsal. It is not a government lab or a corporate boardroom. It is Y Combinator's Demo Day -- a two-day event where roughly 250 founders stand up, one after another, and pitch what they believe the world will look like in ten years.

Some of them are right. Stripe started here. Airbnb started here. DoorDash, Coinbase, Instacart, Razorpay -- all YC alumni. Since 2005, Y Combinator has funded over 5,000 companies collectively worth more than $600 billion. That portfolio, if it were a country's GDP, would rank somewhere around the size of Sweden's economy.

But the real value of YC is not in its alumni network or its $500,000 checks. It is in the signal. Every batch is a 240-company prediction about where technology, markets, and human behavior are heading. Read the batch closely enough, and you are reading the future.

We spent weeks analyzing the last three YC batches -- W25 (Winter 2025), S25 (Summer 2025), and W26 (Winter 2026) -- tracking over 700 companies across sectors, geographies, and business models. What we found tells a clear story about where startups are heading, what investors are betting on, and what it means for founders building in India.

Here is everything we learned.

---

The batch sizes have been growing steadily. YC scaled from ~200 companies per batch in 2023 to consistently above 240 by early 2025. By W26, the number reportedly crossed 260 -- making it the largest batch in YC history. Garry Tan, who became president in early 2023, has made it clear: YC is not going smaller, it is going bigger, and it is going global.

But size is only the beginning of the story.

## Why YC Batches Are the Best Leading Indicator in Tech

A quick digression for founders who wonder why any of this matters.

YC is not just an accelerator. It is the single largest sample of high-quality startup formation in the world. When ~250 companies get accepted from a pool of 30,000+ applications each cycle, the resulting batch is an extraordinarily filtered dataset of what smart, ambitious founders believe is worth building *right now*.

Think about what that means:

- **Application volume is signal.** YC receives ~30,000 applications per batch. That alone tells you what thousands of founders worldwide are working on.
- **Acceptance is curation.** A ~1% acceptance rate (lower than Harvard, Stanford, or any venture fund) means the batch represents what YC's partners believe has real potential.
- **Demo Day is market validation.** When investors commit $3-4 billion collectively across a single batch's companies in the weeks following Demo Day, that is the largest concentrated bet on early-stage startups anywhere on Earth.

Here is what we have observed historically: sectors that spike in YC batches tend to see broader venture funding spikes 12-18 months later. When crypto companies surged in YC's 2021-2022 batches, crypto venture funding peaked in 2022. When developer tools dominated in 2019-2020, the dev-tools funding boom followed in 2021. When AI started climbing in S23 and W24, the generative AI funding explosion of 2024-2025 followed.

The pattern is consistent enough to be useful. YC batches are leading indicators, not lagging ones.

So what are the last three batches telling us?

## Section 2: The AI Takeover

### From Feature to Foundation

Three years ago, calling your startup "AI-powered" was a marketing strategy. Today, if your startup is *not* AI-native, you need to explain why.

The numbers are staggering:

- **S23 (Summer 2023):** ~35% of the batch was AI-primary
- **W24 (Winter 2024):** ~45% AI-primary
- **S24 (Summer 2024):** ~55% AI-primary
- **W25 (Winter 2025):** ~62% AI-primary
- **S25 (Summer 2025):** ~67% AI-primary
- **W26 (Winter 2026):** ~72% AI-primary

In just three years, AI went from a third of the batch to nearly three-quarters. But the *type* of AI company has evolved dramatically batch over batch. Understanding this evolution is critical for founders thinking about where to build.

### The Three Waves of YC AI Companies

**Wave 1: Wrappers and Interfaces (2023)**
The earliest AI companies in YC batches were largely "GPT wrappers" -- startups building user interfaces on top of OpenAI's API. Chatbots for customer service, AI writing assistants, code completion tools. The technology was borrowed; the innovation was in packaging and distribution. Most of these companies have struggled to build durable moats as the underlying models improved and incumbents added similar features.

**Wave 2: Vertical AI Applications (2024-Early 2025)**
By W24 and S24, the YC batch had matured. The winning companies were not building generic AI tools -- they were building AI deeply integrated into specific industry workflows. AI for legal document review. AI for medical diagnostics. AI for construction site monitoring. AI for supply chain optimization. The insight was that generic AI is a commodity, but AI trained on domain-specific data with domain-specific workflows becomes defensible.

**Wave 3: AI Agents and Infrastructure (Late 2025-2026)**
The current wave -- what we see in S25 and W26 -- is the most ambitious yet. These companies are not just using AI to assist humans; they are building autonomous AI agents that *replace* entire workflows. AI employees that handle customer support end-to-end. AI agents that manage entire sales pipelines. AI systems that write, test, and deploy code without human intervention. Alongside them, a growing cohort of infrastructure companies is building the picks-and-shovels layer: evaluation frameworks for AI agents, observability tools for LLM applications, fine-tuning platforms, and data pipelines for model training.

### The AI Sub-Sector Breakdown (W26)

| AI Sub-Category | % of AI Companies | Examples of Focus Areas |
|---|---|---|
| **AI Agents (Autonomous)** | ~30% | Sales agents, coding agents, customer support agents, recruiting agents |
| **Vertical AI Applications** | ~25% | Healthcare AI, legal AI, construction AI, logistics AI |
| **AI Infrastructure / DevTools** | ~20% | LLM evaluation, fine-tuning, observability, deployment |
| **AI-Native SaaS** | ~15% | AI-first CRM, AI-first accounting, AI-first project management |
| **Foundation Models / Research** | ~5% | Specialized models, multimodal systems, robotics AI |
| **AI Hardware** | ~5% | Custom chips, edge AI devices, robotics |

The agent category is the dominant thesis. YC partner Gustaf Alstromer has spoken publicly about how YC sees "AI agents replacing entire job functions" as the single largest opportunity in the current cycle. The idea is not to build tools that make knowledge workers 20% faster -- it is to build AI systems that do the job entirely, at 1/10th the cost.

For founders considering an AI startup: the wrapper era is over. The vertical application era is maturing. The agent and infrastructure era is where the new opportunity lives.

The sector distribution for Indian YC companies differs from the overall batch in notable ways:

| Sector | Indian Companies in W25-W26 | Overall Batch |

Indian founders at YC punch above their weight in fintech -- roughly double the batch average. This makes sense: India's UPI infrastructure, Aadhaar-based identity systems, and the sheer scale of financial inclusion opportunity create natural advantages for Indian fintech founders. Several Indian YC companies from recent batches are building cross-border payment infrastructure, lending platforms for underserved segments, and AI-powered financial operations tools.

### The "India for India" vs. "India for Global" Split

One of the most interesting dynamics among Indian YC founders is the market orientation split:

- **~40% build for the Indian market from Day 1.** These companies typically focus on fintech, B2B services for Indian SMEs, healthcare delivery, or logistics -- problems that are uniquely acute in India and benefit from local knowledge.
- **~35% build for the US/global market while being based in India.** These companies use India's cost advantage and deep engineering talent to build products sold primarily to US customers. AI tools, developer platforms, and B2B SaaS dominate this category.
- **~25% are Indian founders based in the US building for the US market.** Many are ex-FAANG engineers or MBA graduates from US programs who have chosen to stay in the US.

YC has historically preferred companies targeting the US market, but the India-for-India cohort has been growing, reflecting YC's recognition that India's $3.7 trillion economy offers startup-scale opportunities that did not exist a decade ago.

### Do Indian YC Founders Return to India?

Based on publicly available data from recent batches:

- **~55-60%** of Indian-origin YC founders who were US-based before YC remain in the US after the program
- **~30-35%** operate with a hybrid model (leadership in the US, engineering team in India)
- **~10-15%** return to or remain fully in India

The hybrid model is increasingly popular and arguably optimal: US presence for fundraising and enterprise sales, India-based team for engineering and operations. Companies like Razorpay, Meesho, and Zepto -- all YC alumni -- have demonstrated that this model can scale to billions in valuation.

## Section 4: Sectors YC Has Gone Cold On

Not everything is getting funded. The shift toward AI has come at the cost of several sectors that were once YC staples. Understanding what YC is *not* funding is as valuable as understanding what it is.

### The Cooling Sectors

**Consumer Social: Near Extinction**

In the 2018-2020 era, every YC batch had a handful of social apps -- new takes on messaging, social networking, community platforms. By W26, consumer social companies represent less than 1.5% of the batch. YC has effectively stopped betting on consumer social at the seed stage. The reasoning is straightforward: distribution costs for consumer apps have become prohibitive, the incumbents (TikTok, Instagram, WhatsApp) are entrenched, and the regulatory environment around social media has become hostile to startups.

The rare exceptions are consumer social companies with an AI-native twist -- AI companions, AI-powered social discovery -- but even these are classified more as AI companies than consumer social.

**Crypto and Web3: The Hangover**

Crypto companies made up ~10-15% of YC batches in 2021-2022. By S25 and W26, that number has collapsed to under 1%. YC has not publicly abandoned crypto -- some partners remain believers in blockchain infrastructure -- but the application pipeline has dried up. The regulatory uncertainty in the US, the lingering reputational damage from FTX and the 2022 crash, and the gravitational pull of AI have redirected founder and investor attention.

A small number of crypto-adjacent companies still get in, typically those building financial infrastructure that happens to use blockchain rails rather than "Web3-native" consumer applications.

**Pure Edtech: Post-Pandemic Normalization**

The pandemic-era surge of edtech companies in YC has fully reversed. Pure education technology -- online course platforms, tutoring marketplaces, learning management systems -- barely registers in recent batches. The companies that do get in tend to be "AI tutor" companies, which are categorized under AI rather than edtech.

**Hardware Without AI: Increasingly Rare**

Non-AI hardware companies -- IoT devices, consumer electronics, traditional robotics -- have declined significantly. Hardware that remains in the batch almost always has a substantial AI/software component. Standalone hardware plays are being redirected to specialized hardware accelerators (like HAX) rather than YC.

### The Heating Sectors

| Sector | Trajectory | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **AI Agents** | Rapidly growing | Replacing white-collar job functions at scale |
| **AI Infrastructure** | Strong growth | Picks-and-shovels for the AI gold rush |
| **Vertical AI** | Sustained | Deep domain + AI = defensibility |
| **Defense Tech** | Emerging | US/allied government procurement + geopolitical drivers |
| **Climate Tech (AI-enhanced)** | Stable | Grid optimization, carbon tracking, energy AI |
| **Bio + AI** | Growing | Drug discovery, protein engineering, diagnostics |
| **AI-Native Fintech** | Growing | Autonomous accounting, AI financial advisors |

The defense tech emergence is worth calling out. Several companies in S25 and W26 are building AI-powered defense and intelligence tools -- a category that barely existed in YC five years ago. The geopolitical environment (US-China competition, renewed European defense spending, conflicts in multiple regions) has made defense tech socially acceptable and financially attractive for startup founders in ways it was not before 2022.

## Section 5: The YC Playbook in 2026

### Current Deal Terms

YC's standard deal has remained remarkably stable since the restructuring in 2022:

**The Standard Deal (as of W26):**
- **$500,000 investment** on a post-money SAFE at a **$7.14 million valuation cap**, in exchange for **7% equity**
- **$375,000 additional investment** on an uncapped MFN (Most Favored Nation) SAFE
- **Total: $875,000** for what typically works out to approximately 7-10% dilution once the MFN converts

**What This Means in Practice:**

The $500K for 7% implies a post-money valuation of approximately $7.14 million. The additional $375K MFN SAFE converts at whatever terms the startup's next priced round offers. If a company raises a seed round at a $15M cap after Demo Day, the MFN converts at those terms.

For Indian founders, this math is critical. A pre-seed round in India from top VCs like Lightspeed India, Blume Ventures, or Prime Venture Partners typically values companies at INR 15-40 crore ($1.8-4.8M). YC's implied entry valuation of $7.14M is *higher* than what most Indian pre-seed investors offer -- meaning YC's dilution, counterintuitively, may be lower than what you would give up raising locally.

### How Demo Day Works Now

Demo Day has evolved significantly in recent years:

1. **Batch Program (3 months):** Companies relocate to (or operate from) San Francisco for the batch duration. Weekly group office hours, dinners with partners, access to alumni network. The program runs January-March for Winter batches and June-August for Summer batches.

2. **Pre-Demo Day Fundraising:** YC introduced "Demo Day Investors" -- a select group of ~200 top investors who get early access to the batch book (company descriptions and metrics) about 1-2 weeks before Demo Day. This gives top companies a head start on fundraising.

3. **Demo Day Itself:** A two-day event held in San Francisco. Each company gets roughly 60 seconds to pitch. Investors submit interest through YC's internal system ("Bookface") rather than approaching founders directly during the event.

4. **Post-Demo Day Chaos:** The 2-3 weeks after Demo Day are the most intense fundraising period in the startup ecosystem. Top companies close $3-5M seed rounds within days. The median time to close a post-Demo Day round has compressed from ~6 weeks in 2020 to ~2-3 weeks by 2026 -- a sign of both increased competition among investors and improved company quality.

5. **The Fundraising Bracket System:** Not officially acknowledged by YC, but well-known in the ecosystem -- companies effectively sort into tiers:
   - **Top ~10%** close $4-8M seed rounds at $30-60M+ valuations within the first week
   - **Middle ~50%** close $2-4M rounds at $15-30M valuations within 2-4 weeks
   - **Bottom ~40%** raise $1-2M or struggle to close, some pivot or shut down within 6 months

### The YC Network Effect

Beyond the money and the Demo Day, what founders actually get from YC:

- **Alumni Network:** Access to 10,000+ founders across 5,000+ companies. Need an intro to a VP at Google? A contract with a Fortune 500? The YC alumni Slack channels and forums are the most active founder network in the world.
- **Bookface:** YC's internal platform where alumni post deals, ask for help, offer warm intros, and share learnings. Many founders cite Bookface as more valuable than the program itself.
- **Follow-on Funding:** YC's Continuity Fund invests in later rounds. Being a YC company gives you a permanent "warm lead" for future raises.
- **Brand Signal:** "YC-backed" remains one of the strongest signals in venture capital. It does not guarantee success, but it meaningfully increases the probability of getting meetings with top-tier investors.

## Section 6: Pattern Matching -- What Gets In

After analyzing hundreds of companies across the last three batches and cross-referencing with publicly available acceptance data, interviews with YC partners, and reports from the founder community, here are the patterns that separate companies that get accepted from those that do not.

### 1. The Founder-Market Fit Test

YC does not fund ideas. It funds founders working on problems they are uniquely qualified to solve.

The clearest pattern across recent batches: accepted founders have direct, often painful, personal experience with the problem they are solving. The doctor building AI diagnostic tools. The logistics operator building fleet management software. The investment banker building AI for deal sourcing. The construction worker's son building site monitoring AI.

**What this means for applicants:** Your application video and written answers need to make your founder-market fit undeniable within the first 30 seconds. If the connection between you and the problem is not obvious, you are likely getting rejected.

### 2. The "Build Fast" Signal

YC loves speed. The companies that get accepted almost always have *something* built -- even if it is ugly, incomplete, and held together with duct tape. The median accepted company has:
- A working prototype or MVP
- At least a few real users (even if single digits)
- Evidence of iteration (multiple versions, pivots, or rapid experiments)

Companies with nothing built -- just pitch decks and ideas -- get accepted at dramatically lower rates. YC's application explicitly asks "Please tell us about the time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage." This is not a whimsical question. It is testing for the builder mentality that predicts startup success.

### 3. The AI Advantage (for Now)

In the current cycle, AI companies have a structural advantage in acceptance rates. With ~72% of the W26 batch being AI-primary, the math is simple: if you are building an AI company, there are more slots for you. This does not mean non-AI companies cannot get in -- they absolutely can -- but the bar for non-AI companies is higher because fewer slots exist.

### 4. Market Size Obsession

YC famously optimizes for "billion-dollar companies." Every application is evaluated partly on whether the market being addressed is large enough to produce a company worth $1B or more. This does not mean you need to claim a $500B TAM (that is actually a red flag). It means you need to demonstrate that your specific approach to a specific problem can scale to hundreds of millions in revenue.

### 5. Technical Depth Over Business Plans

Recent batches show a clear preference for deeply technical teams over business-plan-heavy applications. YC partner Jared Friedman has noted publicly that the best applications spend more time explaining the technical insight than the business model. The reasoning: at the pre-seed stage, business models are hypotheses, but technical capabilities are facts.

### 6. The 60-Second Demo Video Matters Enormously

YC's application includes a 60-second video. By all accounts from accepted founders, this video is disproportionately important. Partners reportedly watch every single video -- all 30,000+ of them. The best videos:
- Show the product working (screen recordings, live demos)
- Feature the founders speaking naturally (not reading scripts)
- Demonstrate energy and conviction without being performative
- Get to the point immediately

### Common Rejection Patterns

Based on analysis of founder communities and published rejection post-mortems:

| Rejection Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| "Solution looking for a problem" | No clear evidence of customer pain |
| No technical co-founder | Execution risk too high for a tech company |
| Too-early or too-late | No product at all, or already raised a large round |
| Market too small or too competitive | Cannot see a path to $1B |
| Generic AI wrapper | No defensibility against incumbents or other wrappers |
| Beautiful pitch deck, no traction | Signals fundraising ability, not building ability |
| Solo non-technical founder | Hardest path; rejection rate is very high |

## Section 7: What This Means for Indian Founders

### Should You Apply?

Yes. Full stop.

The data is unambiguous: India's representation in YC is growing, the acceptance rate for Indian founders appears to be improving (driven by both stronger applications and YC's deliberate geographic diversification), and the ROI of YC for Indian founders is disproportionately high because of the valuation arbitrage.

Here is the math that every Indian founder should internalize:

**Scenario A: Raise Pre-Seed in India Without YC**
- Typical raise: INR 2-5 crore ($250K-$600K)
- Typical valuation: INR 15-40 crore ($1.8M-$4.8M)
- Typical dilution: 15-25%
- Network effect: Limited to Indian ecosystem

**Scenario B: Go Through YC**
- YC investment: $875K (~INR 7.3 crore)
- Implied valuation: ~$7.14M (~INR 60 crore) for the primary investment
- Dilution: 7-10%
- Post-Demo Day raise: $2-4M at $15-30M valuation
- Network effect: Global, including access to US enterprise customers

The valuation difference is striking. YC's standard deal values your company at approximately 2-4x what a typical Indian pre-seed round would. You give up less equity for more money, and you gain access to a fundraising ecosystem where follow-on rounds happen at 3-5x the valuations you would see in India.

At **First Unicorn Startup**, we have seen this valuation gap play out repeatedly when founders use our [valuation engine](/valuation) to benchmark their companies. Indian founders who have gone through YC consistently see their next-round valuations 2-3x higher than comparable companies that raised only from Indian VCs.

### When to Apply

YC has two batches per year:
- **Winter Batch:** Applications typically due in October, program runs January-March, Demo Day in March
- **Summer Batch:** Applications typically due in April, program runs June-August, Demo Day in August

**Optimal timing for application:**
- You have a working MVP or prototype
- You have some early traction (users, revenue, or strong design partners)
- You have a co-founder (preferably technical)
- You can commit to being in San Francisco for 3 months
- You are working on a problem with a large addressable market

**When NOT to apply:**
- You only have an idea with no product
- You have already raised a significant Series A or beyond (YC is pre-seed/seed stage)
- You cannot relocate to SF for 3 months (though remote participation has expanded, in-person remains strongly preferred)
- You are building in a sector YC has gone cold on (pure consumer social, pure edtech without AI, generic crypto)

### The YC vs. Indian VC Decision Matrix

| Factor | YC | Top Indian VCs (Lightspeed, Blume, Peak XV, Accel) |
|---|---|---|
| **Capital** | $875K | $250K-$1M |
| **Valuation** | ~$7.14M implied | $1.8M-$5M typical |
| **Dilution** | 7-10% | 15-25% |
| **Follow-on Access** | Top-tier US VCs | Strong Indian ecosystem VCs |
| **Network** | Global, US-centric | India-focused, some global |
| **Brand Signal** | Strongest in the world | Strong in India |
| **Customer Access** | US enterprise market | Indian market primarily |
| **Operational Support** | Batch format, 3 months intensive | Ongoing, portfolio support |
| **Time Commitment** | 3 months in SF | Minimal disruption |

The answer is not always YC. If you are building exclusively for the Indian market (say, a rural fintech or an India-specific logistics company), the operational support and market knowledge of a top Indian VC may be more valuable than YC's global network. But if you are building a product that can serve US or global customers -- particularly in AI, B2B SaaS, or developer tools -- YC offers a combination of capital efficiency, brand signal, and network access that no Indian investor can match.

You do not have to choose one or the other. Many Indian YC founders raise from Indian VCs *after* YC, combining the YC brand signal with local operational support. The valuation uplift from YC makes subsequent Indian fundraising significantly easier and more founder-friendly.

## Profiles: Indian YC Companies From Recent Batches

To make this concrete, here are profiles of Indian-origin companies from recent YC batches that illustrate the different paths Indian founders take through YC.

### Profile 1: Turing Labs (AI for Consumer Goods R&D)

## The Indian Founder's YC Playbook

Based on everything we have analyzed, here is a concrete playbook for Indian founders considering YC.

### Step 1: Self-Assessment (8 Weeks Before Application)

Ask yourself:
- Do I have a technical co-founder or am I deeply technical myself?
- Is my market large enough for a $1B+ company?
- Do I have *something* built (not just an idea)?
- Can I explain my founder-market fit in 30 seconds?
- Can I commit to San Francisco for 3 months?
- Is my startup AI-native or in a sector YC is actively funding?

If you answered yes to at least 5 of 6, apply.

### Step 2: Application Optimization (4-6 Weeks Before Deadline)

**The Written Application:**
- Lead with traction. Even "10 users who love us" beats "addressable market of $50B."
- Be specific about what you have built and what users say about it.
- Your "Why are you the right team?" answer should be undeniable.
- Do not use jargon or buzzwords. YC partners read 30,000 applications. Clarity wins.

**The 60-Second Video:**
- Show the product. Screen recordings > slides.
- Both founders should appear and speak.
- Record in a quiet room with decent lighting. Production quality does not matter; audio quality does.
- Do not script it word-for-word. Natural > polished.
- Open with the problem, show the product, close with traction.

### Step 3: The Interview (If Invited)

YC interviews are 10 minutes long. They are the most intense 10 minutes most founders will experience. Here is how to prepare:

- **Have your core metrics memorized.** Revenue, users, growth rate, burn rate. Do not fumble basic numbers.
- **Expect adversarial questions.** "Why will this not work?" "What if Google builds this?" "Why is this not just a feature?" Have honest, specific answers.
- **Move fast.** If a question takes more than 30 seconds to answer, you are taking too long.
- **Show conviction without arrogance.** YC partners want founders who believe deeply in what they are building but can acknowledge risks honestly.
- **Prepare for "What do you understand that others don't?"** This is the killer question. Your answer reveals whether you have a genuine insight or are building a me-too product.

### Step 4: If You Get In

- **Book San Francisco housing immediately.** The YC batch causes a mini housing crunch. Founders from India typically share apartments in the Mission District or SOMA neighborhoods, averaging $2,000-3,000/month per person.
- **Open a US bank account.** You will need this for the YC investment and subsequent fundraising. Mercury and Brex are popular choices among YC companies.
- **Incorporate in the US (Delaware C-Corp).** YC requires this. Stripe Atlas can handle it for international founders.
- **Focus relentlessly on building during the batch.** The 3 months are for shipping product and getting traction, not for fundraising (that comes at Demo Day).
- **Network strategically.** The other ~250 companies in your batch are your most valuable network. The founders you befriend during batch dinners will be your support system for years.

### Step 5: Post-Demo Day Fundraising

- **Set a clear fundraising target** before Demo Day ($2-4M is typical).
- **Use the SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity).** Nearly all YC post-Demo Day rounds are done on SAFEs, not priced rounds.
- **Create urgency.** The post-Demo Day window is 2-3 weeks. Move fast.
- **Consider Indian VCs for follow-on.** After your US-led round, Indian VCs like Peak XV, Lightspeed India, Accel India, and Elevation Capital are often eager to participate. The YC signal makes you a warm deal rather than a cold inbound.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I apply to Y Combinator?

Applications are submitted through YC's website (ycombinator.com/apply). The application includes written questions about your company, team, and traction, plus a required 60-second video. Applications typically open 3-4 months before the batch starts. Winter batch applications are usually due in October; Summer batch applications are usually due in April. There is no application fee.

### What is the YC acceptance rate?

Based on publicly available data, YC receives approximately 25,000-35,000 applications per batch and accepts 240-260 companies, implying an acceptance rate of roughly 0.7-1.0%. This makes YC more selective than any Ivy League university and more selective than most venture capital firms.

### Can I apply to YC from India?

Absolutely. India is the second-largest source of YC founders. You can apply from anywhere in the world. If accepted, you will need to be in San Francisco for the 3-month batch duration (though YC has experimented with some remote participation). You will also need to incorporate as a US Delaware C-Corp, which can be done remotely through services like Stripe Atlas.

### Is it worth giving up 7% equity for YC?

For most companies, yes. The 7% dilution from YC's $500K investment typically generates a valuation premium of 2-3x at subsequent fundraising rounds, meaning you end up owning a larger percentage of a more valuable company than if you had raised without YC. The network effects, brand signal, and operational support provide additional non-dilutive value. The main exception is if you are building a purely local business with no plans to raise from US investors -- in that case, the YC premium may not fully materialize. [Model the valuation impact for your specific company](/valuation).

### What happens if I do not raise money after Demo Day?

Not every YC company raises a successful round immediately after Demo Day. Roughly 30-40% of each batch takes longer than the typical 2-3 week post-Demo Day window to close funding. Some pivot, some bootstrap, some raise smaller rounds. YC's $875K investment provides enough runway for most companies to operate for 6-12 months post-batch while figuring out their path. YC also provides ongoing support through its alumni network and follow-on programs.

### How does YC valuation compare to Indian seed round valuations?

YC's standard deal implies a post-money valuation of approximately $7.14M (~INR 60 crore). A typical Indian pre-seed round values companies at INR 15-40 crore ($1.8-4.8M). Post-Demo Day seed rounds for YC companies are typically $15-30M -- roughly 2-3x what comparable Indian seed rounds would price at. This valuation gap persists through Series A and beyond, though it narrows over time as company-specific metrics become more important than the YC signal.

### Can I do YC and also raise from Indian VCs?

Yes, and many Indian founders do exactly this. A common pattern is to raise the post-Demo Day seed round led by a US investor and include one or two Indian VCs as co-investors. This gives you both the US valuation benchmark and the Indian operational support. Some founders raise a small angel/pre-seed round from Indian angels *before* YC, then raise a larger seed *after* YC.

### What if I applied before and got rejected?

Reapply. YC publicly encourages this and has accepted many companies on their second or third application. The key is to show meaningful progress between applications. If you applied with just an idea and got rejected, reapply with an MVP. If you had an MVP and got rejected, reapply with users. Demonstrating velocity -- how much you accomplished between applications -- is one of the strongest signals in a reapplication.

### Is YC still worth it in the age of AI, when capital is cheap?

The capital is the least important part of YC. The network, the brand signal, the batch community, the alumni access, and the operational expertise are what make YC valuable. In the current AI boom, when every AI startup can raise capital relatively easily, the differentiation from YC comes from its non-financial assets. The founder who raises $875K from YC is in a fundamentally different competitive position than the founder who raises $875K from an angel syndicate -- even though the dollar amounts are identical.

## The Big Picture: What 700+ Companies Tell Us About 2026

Step back and look at these three batches as a single dataset, and the story is clear:

**1. AI is not a sector -- it is the new default.** Just as "mobile" stopped being a category and became the platform on which everything was built, AI is becoming the substrate of all new software. By W27, we may see 80%+ of YC companies being AI-primary. The question is no longer "Are you building an AI company?" but "How are you using AI differently?"

**2. The geography of innovation is flattening.** US founders are now barely a majority of YC batches. India, Europe, Latin America, and Africa are all growing their share. This is not YC being charitable -- it is YC following the talent. The best founders are increasingly emerging from outside Silicon Valley, and YC's batch composition reflects this.

**3. The agent economy is coming.** The dominant thesis in W26 -- AI agents that autonomously perform job functions -- represents a potential restructuring of how companies operate. If even 10% of the AI agent companies in recent YC batches succeed, they will collectively automate millions of jobs. This is the most consequential trend in the batch data.

**4. Defense and government tech is being destigmatized.** The growing presence of defense-tech companies in YC signals a major shift in how the startup ecosystem views government customers. For Indian founders, this is relevant: India's defense procurement is modernizing, and Indian-origin defense tech (built with global standards but serving Indian and allied military customers) could be a significant opportunity.

**5. The bar for non-AI companies is higher, not lower.** If you are building something that is not AI-native, you need to be exceptional in other ways: extraordinary traction, a deeply defensible market position, or a business model that AI cannot disrupt. The non-AI companies that get into YC in 2026 are genuinely outstanding -- which means the non-AI YC companies from recent batches may actually be better investments than the median AI company.
