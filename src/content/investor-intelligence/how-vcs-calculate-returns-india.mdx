# How VCs Actually Calculate Their Returns: The Math Behind "10x"

Every founder has heard the VC phrase: "We need 10x returns."

Most founders nod and move on without understanding what it actually means — or why it means your startup might never be fundable by that VC regardless of how good it is.

Understanding fund math is the single biggest unlock for founder-investor alignment. It explains why VCs pass on solid businesses, why they need you to be aiming for ₹1,000 crore, and why the investor who "really liked your business" still said no.

---

## The VC Fund Structure: Start Here

A VC fund is a pooled investment vehicle. The structure:

- **LPs (Limited Partners):** The investors who put money into the fund. Typically pension funds, endowments, family offices, HNIs, fund-of-funds.
- **GPs (General Partners):** The VC firm. They manage the fund and make investment decisions.
- **Fund size:** Total capital committed by LPs. A typical Indian VC fund ranges from ₹200 crore to ₹2,000 crore.
- **Management fee:** GPs charge ~2% of fund size annually to cover operations. On a ₹500 crore fund, that's ₹10 crore/year = ₹100 crore over 10 years.
- **Carried interest (carry):** GPs take 20% of profits above a hurdle rate. The remaining 80% goes to LPs.
- **Fund life:** Typically 10 years (5 years investing, 5 years managing/exiting).

---

## The Return Math: Why VCs Need Outliers

A ₹500 crore fund with 2% management fees and 20% carry:

**Capital deployed:** ₹500 crore raised − ₹100 crore management fees = **₹400 crore for investments**

**LP expectation:** 3x net returns (a typical institutional LP target). That means LPs expect ₹1,500 crore back on their ₹500 crore.

**GP economics:**
- Gross fund return needed: ~₹1,875 crore (to net ₹1,500 crore to LPs after 20% carry)
- That's 3.75x gross on the ₹500 crore fund

**Now the portfolio math:**

A typical VC portfolio has 20–30 investments. Using a standard power law distribution:

| Category | # of Investments | Expected Return |
|---|---|---|
| Complete failures (0x) | 10–12 | ₹0 |
| Living dead (0.5–1x) | 4–6 | ₹50–150 crore total |
| Modest success (2–3x) | 3–4 | ₹100–200 crore total |
| Good outcomes (5–10x) | 2–3 | ₹200–500 crore total |
| **Fund returner (20x+)** | **1–2** | **₹800–1,500 crore** |

**The math is brutal:** 1–2 companies in a 25-company portfolio need to return the entire fund. The rest are almost irrelevant to GP economics.

This is why VCs say "we need 10x." They're not asking for 10x on every investment. They're saying: this particular investment needs to be a potential fund-returner, which at their check size means 10–30x return.

## The Ownership-Return Equation

Here's the math that determines whether your startup is fundable by a specific VC:

```
Required Exit Value = Target Return Multiple × Investment Amount / Target Ownership at Exit

Or rearranged:

Investment Amount = Exit Value × Target Ownership at Exit / Required Multiple
```

**Example: A ₹500Cr fund investing ₹20Cr at Series A**

- The VC invests ₹20 crore
- They need this to be a potential "fund returner" = ₹500 crore+ returned
- Required multiple: ₹500Cr / ₹20Cr = **25x**
- But they know they'll get diluted from 20% at Series A to ~10% at exit (through Series B, C, ESOP expansion)
- Exit value needed for 25x: ₹500Cr / 10% = **₹5,000 crore exit**

The company needs to be worth ₹5,000 crore at exit for this VC to return their fund from this investment.

**This is why your TAM matters so much.** Not because VCs are greedy. Because if the market can't support a ₹5,000 crore outcome, this VC mathematically cannot invest — even if your business is excellent.

## IRR: The Time Dimension of Returns

VCs don't just care about return multiples — they care about **when** the return happens. A 10x return in 2 years is dramatically better than a 10x return in 10 years.

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return)** captures this:

| Return Multiple | Years to Exit | IRR |
|---|---|---|
| 10x | 3 years | 115% |
| 10x | 5 years | 58% |
| 10x | 7 years | 39% |
| 10x | 10 years | 26% |
| 3x | 5 years | 25% |
| 3x | 7 years | 17% |

**Most institutional LP targets: 20–25% net IRR.**

A 10x in 10 years barely clears the hurdle. A 10x in 5 years is exceptional. This is why VCs prefer companies that can exit within 5–7 years.

**The implication for founders:** A company that grows steadily but exits late is less attractive than a company that grows faster and exits sooner — even if the absolute return multiple is similar. "We'll be ready in 10 years" is a hard pitch to a fund with a 10-year life that's 3 years in.

---

## The DPI vs. TVPI Distinction

VCs report two types of returns to LPs:

**TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In):** Current portfolio value (marked-to-market) + distributions / capital invested. This is the "on paper" return.

**DPI (Distributions to Paid-In):** Actual cash returned to LPs / capital invested. This is the "real money" return.

Indian VC funds from 2015–2019 vintages often have strong TVPI (inflated valuations) but weak DPI (few actual exits). LPs are increasingly judging fund managers on DPI, not TVPI.

**What this means for founders:** VCs whose LPs are pressing for DPI are more motivated to support exits — including secondary transactions, M&A, and IPOs. If your VC has been investing for 7+ years from a fund, they may actively support a good acquisition offer that founders might otherwise resist.

## How Different Fund Sizes Think About Your Deal

| Fund Size | Typical Check | Required Exit for Fund Return | Minimum Target Valuation |
|---|---|---|---|
| ₹50–100Cr (micro-VC, angel) | ₹50L–2Cr | ₹100–500Cr | ₹200–800Cr |
| ₹200–500Cr (seed/Series A) | ₹2–15Cr | ₹500Cr–2,000Cr | ₹1,000–3,000Cr |
| ₹500Cr–2,000Cr (Series A/B) | ₹15–75Cr | ₹2,000–8,000Cr | ₹3,000–10,000Cr+ |
| ₹2,000Cr+ (growth) | ₹75Cr+ | ₹8,000Cr+ | ₹10,000Cr+ |

**Reading this table:** If you're pitching a ₹200 crore fund and your realistic exit is ₹500 crore, you might get funded. If you pitch a ₹2,000 crore fund with the same exit case, you won't — because ₹500 crore doesn't move the needle for their portfolio.

**This is why fund size matching matters.** A great ₹300 crore outcome is irrelevant to a ₹2,000 crore fund and very exciting to a ₹100 crore fund.

## The Portfolio Construction Logic

VCs don't invest in isolation. Every investment decision is made in the context of their portfolio construction strategy.

**Typical seed fund portfolio (₹200Cr):**
- 20–25 investments
- Average first check: ₹2–4 crore
- Reserve 40–50% for follow-on (pro-rata in winning companies)
- Target ownership: 10–15% post-seed

**Why follow-on reserves matter:** A VC who leads your seed round has reserved money to participate in your Series A at their pro-rata. If you do very well, they need that reserve. If they've deployed it elsewhere, they lose ownership in their best company at the worst time.

**What this means for founders:** Ask your VC how much follow-on reserve they have. A VC who has exhausted their follow-on capital cannot protect your round in later stages — other investors may set terms knowing the existing VC can't fight for you.

## Why "We Loved Your Business" Doesn't Mean "We'll Fund It"

Now you understand the math, the "passes" make more sense:

**"Your market is too small"**
Even if the business is great, if the market can't support the exit multiple needed for the fund, the math doesn't work.

**"We already have a portfolio company in this space"**
VCs often won't invest in two directly competing companies. But more importantly, having a related company already means they don't need another.

**"The timing isn't right for us"**
Translation: we're either too early in our fund (not ready to deploy) or too late (reserve for follow-ons, not new investments).

**"We're not the right fit"**
This is honest when it means "our fund size / ownership target / exit timeline doesn't match what you're building."

## How to Use This as a Founder

**1. Target fund-size-appropriate VCs**

Your realistic exit scenario should be 10–30x the VC's check size. If you think you're building a ₹1,000 crore business, target funds that write ₹5–15 crore checks (which put them at ₹1,000 crore at 10x). Don't pitch ₹2,000 crore funds with a ₹1,000 crore ceiling.

**2. Make the fund math explicit in your pitch**

Show the investor: "Here's the exit scenario, here's your expected ownership at exit, here's the return." Doing their math for them is unusually sophisticated for a founder and stands out.

**3. Understand their portfolio position**

What other investments do they have? Are they early in the fund or late? Do they have follow-on capital? These questions tell you whether they're ready to deploy and whether they can support you long-term.

**4. Don't pitch consensus**

VCs make consensus decisions in IC. If your business is "a solid ₹300 crore outcome," a rational IC will pass — it doesn't move the fund. You need to make the "₹2,000 crore outcome if everything goes right" case credible, even if the base case is more modest.

**5. Use fund math to identify the right lead**

The right lead investor for your deal is one where:
- Your potential exit is 10–30x their check
- Their fund is in the right part of its lifecycle to deploy
- You're not competing with their existing portfolio
- Their ownership target is achievable without over-diluting founders

Finding investors who match on all four dimensions narrows your list considerably — but dramatically improves conversion rates.

---

## The Alignment Question No One Asks

Before taking any VC's money, ask them one question:

**"At what exit outcome would you be happy, and at what outcome would you push for another round instead of exit?"**

This question reveals their fund math and their expectations for your company. If their answer to "happy exit" is ₹5,000 crore and you're building a ₹500 crore business, you have misaligned incentives that will play out badly at the moment of a real acquisition offer.

Misaligned incentives between founders and investors cause more startup failures than bad products. Know the math before you take the money.
