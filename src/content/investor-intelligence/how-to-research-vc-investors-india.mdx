## The Research Problem That Kills Most Indian Fundraises

The average Indian founder raising a Series A spends 4-6 months in the fundraising process, sends their deck to 40-80 investors, and closes a round — if they close at all — with less than 5% of the investors they contacted. That 5% hit rate is not primarily because the product is wrong or the team is weak. It is because the other 95% of pitches were sent to the wrong investors.

The wrong investor might be a fund in its harvest phase (no new capital to deploy). It might be a fund that has already backed a direct competitor. It might be a partner whose thesis is deep-tech hardware and you are building a B2B SaaS product. It might be a fund that writes $500K checks and you need ₹15 crore. Each of these mismatches wastes 2-4 weeks of founder time — time that should be spent building the product or closing customers.

The solution is systematic research conducted before the first outreach email. The founders who close the best rounds — faster, with better terms, from more strategically valuable investors — do this research methodically. This guide is the exact process they use.

## Phase 1: Reading a VC Fund's Portfolio Page

The portfolio page is the most information-dense public resource available about a VC fund. Most founders glance at it for 60 seconds and move on. Here is how to extract the full intelligence value from it.

### Step 1: Map the Stage Distribution

Every portfolio company on the page is at a different stage. Build a rough distribution:
- What percentage of portfolio companies are pre-revenue or very early?
- What percentage are post-Series A with clear revenue traction?
- What percentage appear to be late-stage or near-exit?

If a fund's portfolio is 80% Series B and C companies, they have moved upmarket from their original seed mandate — and your seed-stage startup is not their target.

If a fund's portfolio is 80% seed-stage companies, they specialize in early-stage. Check whether they follow on into Series A themselves or hand off to larger funds — this affects how strategic the relationship will be.

### Step 2: Identify Sector Concentration

List the sectors of the last 15-20 portfolio companies. Look for patterns:
- Which sectors appear 3+ times? Those are clear fund priorities.
- Which sectors are absent? Either they do not invest there, or they are looking to add exposure.
- Are there competitors to your company in the portfolio? (A fund that already backs a direct competitor almost certainly will not invest in you — their existing portfolio company gets first right of access to the fund's network and follow-on capital. Investing in you would be a conflict.)

For example, if you are building an HR tech platform and you see the fund has backed three HR tech companies in the last 4 years, one of two conclusions applies: they understand HR tech well (positive signal) or they are already portfolio-concentrated in your space (conflict risk). Research each portfolio company to determine which.

### Step 3: Assess Portfolio Company Quality as a Signal

A VC fund's portfolio companies tell you what calibre of companies the fund attracts and can support. Look specifically at:
- Have portfolio companies raised follow-on rounds from top-tier funds? (Indicates the fund adds enough value that other funds trust their judgment)
- Have any portfolio companies achieved successful exits (acquisition or IPO)? At what multiples?
- Are portfolio companies' names familiar in their category? (Recognition is a rough proxy for traction)
- Check LinkedIn for a few portfolio companies — how many employees? Who are the key hires? (Funded companies with strong recent hiring are performing; funded companies with stagnant headcount may be struggling)

A fund where 5 of the last 10 portfolio companies have gone quiet (no new funding, no press, no hiring activity in 24+ months) is telling you something important about their selection judgment and their ability to support portfolio companies post-investment.

### Step 4: Look for Portfolio Gaps as Opportunities

Sometimes the most valuable intelligence is what is absent from the portfolio. A fund that has invested across B2B SaaS, fintech, and healthtech but has zero logistics tech exposure may be looking for a logistics investment. A fund that has backed multiple D2C consumer brands but nothing in food/beverage D2C specifically may be building toward that. These gaps represent potential alignment.

Frame your outreach accordingly: "I noticed your portfolio has strong coverage of B2B SaaS across HR and sales tech, but you don't have exposure to legal tech yet. We are building the first AI-native legal workflow tool for Indian MSMEs, and I think there may be a fit."

## Phase 2: Understanding Fund Lifecycle and Vintage

This is the research most Indian founders skip entirely, and it is the single most time-saving piece of intelligence you can gather.

### How VC Fund Cycles Work

A typical India-focused VC fund has a 10-year structure:
- **Years 1-4:** Investment period. The fund is actively deploying capital into new portfolio companies. This is when you want to pitch them.
- **Years 5-6:** Transition. The fund may make follow-on investments in existing portfolio companies but limited new investments.
- **Years 7-10:** Harvest phase. The fund is focused on managing existing portfolio companies toward exits. New investments are extremely rare.

A fund that raised its Fund III in 2018 is now in Year 8 of that fund in 2026. They are almost certainly not deploying new capital from Fund III. The question is: have they raised a Fund IV? If yes, when? A Fund IV raised in 2023 is in Year 3 of deployment — actively investing. If they have not raised a Fund IV, they may not be deploying at all.

### How to Find Fund Vintage

**Crunchbase:** Search for the fund and look at the "Funds" section of their profile. Crunchbase often shows the fund name and vintage year for institutional VC firms.

**SEBI Registration:** Indian VC funds registered as Alternate Investment Funds (AIFs) must file with SEBI. The SEBI AIF registration list is publicly available and shows registration dates. A fund registered in 2020 that has not filed a new AIF in 2023-24 may be on the harvest end of its cycle.

**LinkedIn + Press:** Search for "[Fund Name] raises [Fund IV / Fund III]" in Google News. Fund announcements are always press-released. If you cannot find a new fund announcement for a firm in the last 3 years, be cautious.

**Direct Ask in Introductory Email:** It is completely acceptable to ask: "I understand you are deploying from [Fund Name] — are you actively making new investments from this fund, or should I connect with you when the timing is better?" Most fund managers appreciate the directness.

## Phase 3: Finding Which Partner Leads Which Sector

Most Indian VC firms have 3-8 investment partners, and each partner has a personal investment thesis that influences their decision-making. Pitching to the right partner — the one whose thesis matches your startup — increases your probability of getting a meeting and a yes.

### Research Approach 1: Crunchbase Partner-Level Data

Crunchbase shows which specific partner at a fund led which investment. Look at 10-15 investments from the past 2 years and check which partner name appears. You will quickly see patterns: Partner A leads fintech investments, Partner B leads SaaS, Partner C leads consumer.

### Research Approach 2: LinkedIn Writing and Posting History

Every active VC partner in India has a LinkedIn profile. Some are prolific writers (Sajith Pai of Blume, Prashanth Prakash of Accel have published extensively); others are minimal but still post. Look at their last 6 months of posts and articles:
- What topics do they write about?
- What companies do they publicly celebrate?
- What conferences do they speak at?
- What research reports do they share?

A partner who writes 4 articles about AI-driven enterprise software is signaling their current thesis area. A partner who consistently shares articles about India's rural internet user growth is thinking about that opportunity. Align your framing accordingly.

### Research Approach 3: Podcast Appearances

Indian VC partners appear regularly on startup podcasts: The Seen and Unseen (Amit Varma), 100x Entrepreneur, Neon Show, Founders Unfiltered, and others. These long-form conversations (60-90 minutes) reveal thesis, values, and pattern-matching in ways that a 30-minute pitch meeting never will. Listen to 2-3 episodes featuring the specific partner you are targeting before your pitch.

### Research Approach 4: Twitter/X Activity

Several prominent Indian VCs are active on Twitter (Shailendra Singh of Peak XV, Anand Daniel of Accel, Karthik Reddy of Blume). Their real-time reactions to news, startups, and market developments reveal live thesis updates. A VC who tweets positively about a competitor in your space last week might be signaling that they are actively exploring that sector.

## Phase 4: Due Diligence on the VC Firm Itself

Before you pitch a VC firm, do to them what they will do to you: conduct due diligence. This is not adversarial — it is how you decide whether you want this firm on your cap table for 8-10 years.

### Talk to 2-3 Portfolio Founders Before You Pitch

This is the most valuable research you can do. Spend 30 minutes with a founder who has received investment from the fund you are targeting. Ask them:

```
Questions to ask portfolio founders:

1. How responsive is the fund post-investment? Do partners attend board meetings
   consistently?

2. Do they make introductions to customers, hires, or follow-on investors?
   Can you give me an example?

3. Have they pushed back on your decisions in a way that was helpful, or
   in a way that felt like micromanagement?

4. What did the process from first meeting to term sheet look like? How long?
   What was the due diligence like?

5. Were there any terms in the deal that you wished you had negotiated differently?

6. If you were starting again, would you take money from this fund? Why or why not?

7. What is something about working with this fund that surprised you —
   positively or negatively?
```

Finding portfolio founders: Use LinkedIn to identify founders of portfolio companies. Look for people who were at the company 2-3 years ago (they have enough time-distance to speak candidly). Send a direct LinkedIn InMail. The request is reasonable and founders generally respond to other founders.

### Check Follow-On Investment Behavior

Does the fund follow on into its portfolio companies' subsequent rounds? This is publicly visible on Crunchbase — look at Series B and C deals for companies that raised their seed or Series A from the fund. If the fund consistently does not participate in follow-on rounds, they may lack confidence in their portfolio, lack the capital to follow on, or lack the relationships to get allocation in competitive follow-on rounds. All three are negative signals.

### Research the Fund's Terms History

Typical Indian VC deal terms by tier:

| Term | Market Standard | Founder-Friendly | Founder-Unfriendly |
|---|---|---|---|
| Liquidation preference | 1x non-participating | 1x non-participating | 2x+ or participating |
| Anti-dilution | Weighted average broad-based | Weighted average broad-based | Full ratchet |
| Board seat | 1 investor seat per round | Minority observer only | 2+ investor seats at seed |
| Pro-rata rights | Standard for lead | Lead only | All investors claim pro-rata |
| Information rights | Standard financials quarterly | Minimal | Onerous reporting requirements |
| Drag-along | Majority trigger | Majority trigger | Small minority can trigger |
| Founder vesting | 4-year with 1-year cliff | Single-trigger acceleration | No acceleration whatsoever |

The best way to learn a fund's term pattern: ask portfolio founders about the specific terms they accepted. Lawyers who have closed deals with multiple VC funds also track term patterns and can advise. The startup law firms in Bangalore and Mumbai (Khaitan, AZB, Trilegal, Indus Law for startup-focused work, iSPIRT's template documents as baseline) have institutional knowledge of which funds are consistently founder-friendly.

## Phase 5: The Tier Classification of Indian VC Funds

Understanding the tier structure of Indian VC helps you calibrate your strategy, your pitch, and your expectations.

### Tier 1: Brand Funds

These are the funds where a single portfolio association provides lasting credibility, where the partner networks are globally connected, and where the follow-on capital and co-investment relationships are the deepest.

**Peak XV Partners (formerly Sequoia India):** India's most recognized VC brand. Manages multiple vehicles including Surge (accelerator/seed), main fund (Series A-C), and growth. Partners include Shailendra Singh, Rajan Anandan, Vaibhav Agarwal. Strongest in consumer internet, SaaS, and fintech. Extremely competitive to get a meeting; requires strong warm introduction.

**Lightspeed India:** Backed Oyo, Udaan, ShareChat, Razorpay. Active at seed through Series C. Partners include Bejul Somaia and Hemant Mohapatra. Strong in enterprise SaaS and fintech.

**Accel India:** One of India's oldest active VC firms (since 2008). Portfolio includes Flipkart (early), Freshworks, BrowserStack, Chargebee. Deep SaaS expertise. Partners include Prashanth Prakash, Shekhar Kirani, Anand Daniel.

**Matrix Partners India (now Z47):** Strong Series A focus. Portfolio includes Razorpay (co-led), Ola, Practo. Partners include Rajinder Balaraman and Tarun Davda.

**How to pitch Tier 1:** You need a warm introduction from a credible source — another founder they have backed, a senior operator they respect, or a co-investor from your current round. Cold outreach has very low conversion rates. The pitch must demonstrate a large market, differentiated product, and a team with specific domain credibility. Expect a rigorous 6-10 week due diligence process.

### Tier 2: Established Mid-Tier Funds

These funds are institutionally credible, have strong track records, and provide significant strategic value. Competition for meetings is intense but more accessible than Tier 1.

**Elevation Capital:** Formerly SAIF Partners. Deep portfolio across fintech, edtech, consumer, SaaS. Partners include Ravi Adusumalli and Mayank Khanduja. Known for being patient capital.

**Blume Ventures:** India's leading seed fund with a strong community. Partners include Karthik Reddy and Sajith Pai (known for thought leadership). Strong in SaaS, developer tools, and deep-tech.

**Kalaari Capital:** Has backed Dream11, Myntra (exit). Partners include Vani Kola and Pavan Srinath. Strong in consumer and enterprise.

**BEENEXT:** India + Southeast Asia focus. Partners include Teruhide Sato and Karan Mohla. Strong in B2B and SaaS.

**3one4 Capital:** Known for being founder-friendly and for backing companies in healthcare, gaming, and enterprise. Partners include Pranav Pai and Siddarth Pai.

**Stellaris Venture Partners:** Spin-out from Helion. Partners include Rahul Chowdhri, Alok Goyal, Ritesh Banglani. Strong SaaS and B2B focus.

**How to pitch Tier 2:** Warm introductions are helpful but not mandatory. A well-researched cold email with clear sector fit and genuine knowledge of the fund's portfolio has a meaningful response rate. Expect a 4-8 week process.

### Tier 3: Micro-VCs, Sector Specialists, and New Funds

This tier includes sub-₹100 Cr funds, sector-specific vehicles (Omnivore for agtech, Asha Impact for social enterprise, Mela for consumer), and corporate venture arms (Reliance Ventures, Tata Capital VC, various bank innovation funds).

Advantages of Tier 3: Higher accessibility, faster decision-making, more flexible deal structuring (check sizes, ownership targets, board requirements). They are often willing to lead small rounds where Tier 1 and 2 funds would not lead.

Disadvantages of Tier 3: Less signal value from association, potentially limited follow-on capital, fewer warm connections for Series A introductions, may have less experience with founder-complex situations (co-founder disputes, pivots, bridge rounds).

**How to pitch Tier 3:** Direct outreach works. Sector-specific context and evidence of domain knowledge are more important than network signals. These funds often invest in companies that are too early or too niche for Tier 1 and 2 consideration.

## Phase 6: Finding a Fund's Sweet Spot

Every fund has a specific "sweet spot" — the deal profile they are most enthusiastic about. Investing outside the sweet spot is possible but requires extra conviction from the partner.

### Sweet Spot Dimensions

**Average first check size:** This is the median of their last 10-15 first investments, not the range. A fund that has written one ₹20 crore check and ten ₹3 crore checks has an average of ₹4.5 crore — not ₹20 crore. Find the median.

**Preferred ownership percentage:** Most institutional Indian seed funds target 10-20% ownership post-investment. Series A funds target 15-25%. If your round structure implies less than 10% ownership for the check size they would write, they will likely ask for a larger check or pass.

**Lead vs co-invest:** Some funds only invest when they lead the round (set the terms, take the board seat). Others are happy to co-invest as a non-lead at Series A+ if they trust the lead investor. Knowing this prevents the awkward situation where you approach a fund expecting them to lead and they say "we love the company, but we'd like to follow — who else is leading?"

**Stage specificity:** Some funds will invest across seed to Series B (Blume, 3one4). Others are extremely stage-specific: "We only lead Series A rounds of ₹10-25 crore" is a real fund mandate.

## Phase 7: Red Flags in VC Firms

Not all VC firm attention is desirable. Know what to avoid.

### Red Flag 1: No New Investments in 12+ Months

Check the fund's Crunchbase portfolio sorted by "most recent." If the most recent investment was more than 12 months ago, the fund may not be deploying. Before spending time on this relationship, confirm directly whether they are raising a new fund.

### Red Flag 2: High Partner Turnover

If a fund has seen 2-3 partner departures in the last 3 years without corresponding new partner additions, there is likely an internal conflict or strategy misalignment. A fund with partner instability is less able to maintain portfolio relationships and follow-on commitments. Check LinkedIn tenure of current partners.

### Red Flag 3: No Portfolio Founder References

When you ask for introductions to 2-3 portfolio founders and the fund is unable or unwilling to make those introductions, this is a significant red flag. A confident, founder-friendly fund welcomes this diligence. A fund that discourages it is hiding something about how their portfolio relationships work.

### Red Flag 4: Consistently Founder-Unfriendly Terms

Evidence of term aggressiveness: founders who have worked with a fund consistently mention 2x liquidation preferences, participating preferred stock, or punitive anti-dilution. This information travels through founder networks. Cross-check with the iSPIRT Playbook term benchmarks and with startup lawyers who have reviewed their standard term sheets.

### Red Flag 5: Slow Decision Making Without Transparency

A fund that takes 6+ months to make an investment decision — without communicating a clear timeline — is either not seriously considering your deal or has internal governance issues. While diligence should be thorough (4-8 weeks is reasonable), excessive delays combined with vague feedback are a signal that the fund is not the right partner. Good investors are transparent about their process and timeline.

### Red Flag 6: Concentrated Conflicts in Portfolio

A fund that has backed 3 direct competitors and still wants to meet with you has one of two motivations: they are interested in acquiring market intelligence for their existing portfolio, or they genuinely think the market is large enough to support multiple investments. Ask them directly: "I see you have backed [Competitor A] and [Competitor B]. How do you think about potential conflicts?" Their answer is very informative.

## Building Your Investor Target List

Now apply all of the above research to build a prioritized list of 25-30 investors for your round. Here is the structure:

### The Research Template (Spreadsheet Fields)

```
Fund Research Template — Fields Per Fund:

Fund Information:
  [ ] Fund name
  [ ] Fund vintage (which fund number, when raised)
  [ ] Fund size (AUM of current fund)
  [ ] Deployment status (actively investing / mid-deployment / harvest)
  [ ] AIF registration number (SEBI)

Contact Information:
  [ ] Partner name (the one who covers your sector)
  [ ] Partner LinkedIn URL
  [ ] Partner email (if known)
  [ ] Mutual connections (who can introduce you)
  [ ] Warm intro source (who specifically will make the intro)

Investment Profile:
  [ ] Typical first check size (₹ Cr range)
  [ ] Target ownership percentage
  [ ] Stage focus (seed / Series A / Series B / growth)
  [ ] Sector focus (list top 3 sectors)
  [ ] Lead investor or co-investor?
  [ ] Board seat requirement? (yes / no / observer)

Portfolio Analysis:
  [ ] Direct competitor in portfolio? (yes / no — name if yes)
  [ ] Adjacent portfolio companies (list 2-3 most relevant)
  [ ] Portfolio quality score (1-5, based on follow-on and exit data)
  [ ] Portfolio founder contacted? (yes / no / name)
  [ ] Portfolio founder feedback summary (2-3 sentences)

Partner Thesis Research:
  [ ] Partner's last 3 notable investments
  [ ] Partner's public thesis (1-2 sentences from blog/podcast/LinkedIn)
  [ ] Partner's specific interest in your sector? (yes / probably / no)
  [ ] Last article/podcast link most relevant to your pitch

Fit Assessment:
  [ ] Stage fit (1-5)
  [ ] Sector fit (1-5)
  [ ] Check size fit (1-5)
  [ ] Conflict risk (none / potential / high)
  [ ] Overall priority score (1-5)
  [ ] Outreach status (not contacted / intro sent / meeting scheduled /
      in diligence / passed / soft yes / term sheet)
  [ ] Notes on last interaction
```

### Tier and Sequencing Your Outreach

Do not pitch all 25-30 funds simultaneously. Sequence strategically:

**Week 1-2:** Pitch 3-5 funds that are your genuine "dream" investors but have the lowest probability — use these to refine your pitch narrative with real feedback.

**Week 3-6:** Pitch your core 15-20 target funds in batches of 5-7 per week. This creates natural competition as multiple funds are in diligence simultaneously.

**Week 7-10:** Pitch remaining funds, including those who have not responded to initial outreach. If you have a term sheet from another fund, this is now your primary leverage in all ongoing conversations.

The sequencing creates time pressure that helps close decisions. A fund that has been "considering" for 6 weeks will often move faster when they learn another fund is close to term sheet.

## The Warm Introduction Framework

Cold outreach to Indian VC firms has very low conversion rates — approximately 3-5% meeting rate from cold email versus 30-50% meeting rate from a warm introduction from a trusted source. Warm introductions are not optional; they are the primary channel.

### Sources of Warm Introductions

**Portfolio founders of the target fund:** The most powerful introduction. A founder who has had a positive experience with Fund X introducing you to Fund X's partner carries enormous weight — it signals you are serious, credible, and the kind of person who does their research.

**Co-investors from your current round:** If you have angels or a seed fund in your current round, ask them explicitly for introductions to Series A funds. This is a core service that good seed investors provide.

**Your existing customers:** Enterprise customers who are well-networked (especially if they have senior executives from premier institutions) can introduce you to VC partners. A glowing reference from a customer contact who knows a partner personally is extremely compelling.

**Accelerator alumni networks:** Y Combinator, Surge by Peak XV, Accel Atoms, Antler — all of these programs have alumni who have been funded by Indian VC firms. The alumni networks are active and responsive to founder-to-founder introductions.

**LinkedIn mutual connections:** If you have a mutual connection with a target partner, reach out to the mutual connection first. Explain what you are building, why you think the fund is a fit, and ask if they would be comfortable making an introduction. Keep the ask specific: "Would you be comfortable forwarding this email to [Partner Name]?"

## Cold Outreach When Warm Introductions Are Not Available

When you must go cold, the email format matters enormously. Indian VC partners receive hundreds of cold emails per week. The ones that get responses share four characteristics:

1. **Evidence of research:** Reference a specific portfolio company, a specific piece of writing the partner has done, or a specific thesis they have articulated. "I noticed you led the investment in [Portfolio Company] in 2023 and have written about the opportunity in B2B SaaS for regulated industries" — this tells them in the first line that you have done homework.

2. **Specific fit claim:** "We are building X, which I think fits your fund's focus on Y because Z." Be specific about why you believe you fit, rather than generic claims.

3. **One concrete signal:** One number, one customer name, or one specific milestone that establishes credibility. "₹40 lakhs MRR, growing 15% month-on-month for the past 6 months" says more than three paragraphs of market opportunity text.

4. **A low-friction ask:** Do not ask for a full partner meeting in the first email. Ask for "a 20-minute call to see if there's mutual fit." Lower friction = higher response rate.

```
Sample cold email structure:

Subject: [Your Company] — [1 line description] | Series A

Hi [Partner First Name],

I read your [article/podcast/post] on [specific topic] — your point about [specific
insight] matches exactly what we're seeing with our customers.

I'm [Name], co-founder of [Company] — we [1 sentence description of what you do and
for whom]. We have [1 key metric: MRR/ARR/customers/growth rate].

I noticed [Fund] has backed [Portfolio Company 1] and [Portfolio Company 2] in
[Sector]. [Company] sits at the intersection of [connection to their portfolio thesis].

We are raising a [Series A / Seed] of ₹[X] Cr. I'd appreciate a 20-minute
introductory call to see if there is mutual fit.

[Name]
[Title], [Company]
[One-line traction stat]
[Website]
```

## The Intelligence Advantage: Summary

The founders who close rounds faster, with better terms, and from more strategically valuable investors do one thing differently: they treat investor research as seriously as they treat customer research.

Before the first customer call, you research the customer's company, their industry, their specific problem, and who the decision-maker is. You would not walk into an enterprise sales meeting without that preparation. Walking into a VC pitch without equivalent preparation wastes your most limited resource: the attention of the investors who could change your company's trajectory.

The research process outlined in this guide takes 2-3 hours per fund for the first 10 funds, and about 1-2 hours per fund for subsequent funds as you develop pattern recognition. For a 25-fund target list, the total research investment is approximately 40-60 hours. Against the potential value of a ₹15-30 crore Series A round, this is the highest-ROI activity a founder can undertake during the pre-fundraising phase.
